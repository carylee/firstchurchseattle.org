# First Church Seattle — written voice guide

How to write for firstchurchseattle.org so it sounds like what people actually hear
on Sunday morning. Every rule here is derived from the worship-transcript corpus
(`../youtube/transcripts/`, 2024–2026); dates in parentheses cite a source service.

## The voice in one sentence

Unconditional welcome first, plain warm language, honest about doubt, justice spoken
plainly, everything phrased as invitation — never as requirement.

## Seven attributes (with receipts)

1. **Welcome precedes everything — and it's unconditional.**
   The spoken welcome uses a three-part "whether… or… or anything in between" shape,
   always landing on the same words:
   > "Whether you've got your faith all figured out or you're really not sure about
   > God or anything in between, you are welcome here." (2026-05-17)

   > "…whether it's your first time here or you've been here a thousand times…
   > maybe someone has dragged you here, you are welcome to belong." (2026-03-29)

   Web copy should open with welcome before information, and never make welcome
   conditional on belief, membership, or familiarity.

2. **Invitation, never command.**
   The liturgy says "we invite you," "we hope that you'll," "you are welcome to" —
   and "I invite you to rise *in body or spirit*" (54 occurrences in the corpus).
   Write "you're invited to…" / "join us if you'd like," not "members should" or
   "please make sure to."

3. **Doubt is named, not papered over.**
   "…or you're really not sure about God," "full of certainty or doubt" (2026-02-22).
   Don't write copy that presumes the reader already believes, already prays, or
   already knows church culture.

4. **Justice is integral, not a program.**
   The land acknowledgment is read every single week (verbatim in 14+ transcripts),
   introduced without hedging:
   > "Part of moving the world toward justice is acknowledging where we've been
   > wrong… we're only here because someone took this land from its earliest
   > inhabitants." (2026-05-17)

   Write about justice work in the same plain, unembarrassed register — and frame it
   as faith expressed, per the recurring mission triad: *build community, grow in
   faith, change the world for the better.*

5. **Inclusive and expansive language.**
   Worship leaders introduce themselves with pronouns; God-language avoids gendered
   pronouns ("Worship God's holy name"); ability-inclusive phrasing is habitual
   ("rise in body or spirit"). Carry all three habits into web copy. People are
   "beloved children of God," not "visitors" or "guests" for long.

6. **Warm, plainspoken, lightly funny.**
   > "If you're stuck in traffic because of the hot chocolates, take a deep breath
   > and get here safely — there will still be plenty of worship when you arrive."
   > (2026-03-01)

   > "All you all are invited to come and celebrate with us this day. I was an
   > Oklahoman." (2024-06-02)

   Humor is gentle and self-aware, never snarky. Specificity is hospitality: the
   transcripts don't say "children's materials are available," they say the worship
   activity bags are "on the little bookshelf in the back" (2026-03-01 et al.).

7. **Communal "we," personal "you."**
   The church speaks as "we" about itself and addresses the reader directly as
   "you." Avoid third-person institutional voice ("First Church offers…").

## Signature phrases — use verbatim where they fit

- "You are welcome here." / "…or anything in between, you are welcome here."
- "You are welcome to belong." (2026-03-29) — prefer this over the declarative
  "You belong here": belonging is *offered*, never assigned. The assertion form
  can read as presumptuous ("culty") to a newcomer who hasn't chosen it yet.
- "Rise in body or spirit."
- "This is not the table of First Church. It's not the table of the United Methodist
  Church — it is Christ's Holy Table." (2024-06-02; the open-table invitation)
- "Connected by the Spirit, even if we're not in the same room." (online worshippers,
  2026-05-17)
- The land acknowledgment (use the exact wording from any recent bulletin/transcript;
  it is stable): "We acknowledge that we are on the unceded traditional homelands of
  the Duwamish, the first people of Seattle…"
- "Build community, grow in faith, change the world for the better."

## Don'ts

- Don't write "All are welcome!" as a throwaway — *show* the welcome (who, how,
  no matter what) the way the spoken litany does.
- Don't use insider terms bare: gloss them ("the narthex — the lobby at the back of
  the sanctuary").
- Don't use shame, urgency, or obligation framing ("don't miss," "you need to").
- Don't use corporate or marketing register ("vibrant community," "dynamic worship
  experience"). The corpus never talks like this.
- Don't pepper with exclamation points; the warmth is in the words, not the punctuation.
- Don't gender God; don't assume the reader's church background.

## Per-surface notes

- **Homepage:** lead with a line of the welcome litany, not service times.
- **Event blurbs:** invitation + one concrete sensory/practical detail (what, where
  in the building, food?) — mirroring how announcements are spoken.
- **Sermon pages:** let the preacher's text speak; editorial framing stays minimal.
- **E-news:** same voice, slightly more familiar — it's written to people who were
  in the room on Sunday.
