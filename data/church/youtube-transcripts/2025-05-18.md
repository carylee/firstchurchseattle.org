## Sermon: Why Does the Path Appear Narrow and Dry?
**Preacher:** Rev. Dan Wolpert
**Scripture:** 1 Kings 17:1-7, Matthew 7:13-14

Will you please pray with me?

Amen.

Just got to love technology, right? It's wonderful, but then everybody's like, "Where is that noise coming from? What is that?"

Ah, yes, modern life.

All right, well, it's uh wonderful to be with you all again. Thank you very much for having me. I'm really looking forward to these four times of being with you over the summer, where we are going to explore this question of a spiritual life and practice and social transformation.

So for today, I want there to be a question that is lingering in your mind. Right? Would you go? That's the question. Would you go?

What is this whole series about? Uh, what is this whole series about? Where is this coming from? Uh, the book that I've written follows the Sermon on the Mount, and Jesus is really talking here, uh not about this question of, "Can I be good enough to get to heaven?" Uh sadly, this is the way that uh old Christianity has really twisted most of Jesus's spiritual teachings. Right? So this uh New Testament passage that we've heard today has often been used to make people feel bad or scared, right? That they're not going to make it, right? You're not going to find that secret narrow path. You you better behave, uh or you're going to uh take the elevator down after you die. Uh this is not what Jesus is talking about at all.

And for many people, uh who have grown up in the church and things, that is sort of a revolutionary idea because you've heard this so many times over and over and over again. Of course, the people that are talking about that don't tell you that the modern understanding of hell uh was invented several hundred years after Jesus lived, so he couldn't have possibly been talking about it because it wasn't something he would have been thinking about.

Right, so the question then is, if Jesus is much more interested in, "How are we living now?" How is it that we begin to understand the practice of our life in relation to what he does talk about, which is contrasting the kingdoms of the world with the kingdom of God? Right? And really, if we look at the entire arc of scripture, in many ways, we can see that it is really about God trying to solve one very specific problem.

One very specific problem. And it is not a problem of individual salvation, it is, again, not a problem of the afterlife. Everything is fine in the afterlife, God's in charge of that and God's doing a great job with all of that. So we don't really even have to worry about that a whole lot. The problem though is us. Right? The problem is us. And how we organize society. We just listened to the prayers of the people, and it's kind of the same prayers of the people that you always hear. Right? Why are there poor people? Why are there people being killed? Why are there people that don't have places to live? These are all problems that are actually imminently solvable.

Right? In America, for example, depending on the study that you read, there are anywhere between six and 20 homes for every person who does not have a home. Right? Empty homes. In China, another part of the world, they have built an extra billion empty homes. Right? Why is it that uh we still have arguments in this society about whether or not it is okay to feed children at school for lunch? I mean, really?

So this is the issue that God is trying to solve. How is it that a group of people, right, this is what a kingdom is, just any group of people, why is it that a group of people, why do we have such a hard time trying to engage and create a society where everyone's basic needs are met? Right? If wisdom is indeed this brilliant woman where all her ways are pleasant and all her paths are peace, why is it that we as human beings insist on what Thomas Merton called an innate proclivity towards violence, right, over and over and over again?

And so this wide road of destruction is not, again, any sort of special or unusual thing or some particular uh path of individual badness. But this wide road of destruction that Jesus is talking about, this is the normal course of business. Right? The wide road of destruction is always present, it is always well populated, it is always well funded. Do we not notice that in every society throughout history, there is always money for war? Right? There's always money for war. When the United States was going to war after war after war over these last several decades in the Middle East, all of this money suddenly appeared, right? Extra budget funding. Right? DOE didn't have anything to say about that. Extra six trillion dollars, suddenly just appeared. Right? On the other hand, if we say, "Well, why can't we have money so that everybody gets fed?" Suddenly, we're evil communists. I don't know, I guess communists are really well fed. Is it maybe that's a big problem? I don't know.

So the interesting thing when we look at these two scripture passages, right? On the one hand, we have Elijah, Elijah, who is this great figure of the Old Testament. And Elijah gets this message, "Okay, big part of what I'm up to is you get to go into the desert where there's not much food and we're running out of water." Now, you know, when we hear a story like this out of the Bible where we really sort of know the answer, we've heard it again and again, it all sounds very easy, straightforward. Oh yeah, you know, God came to Elijah, told him to do this, and Elijah was like, "Okay, God, you know," kind of as if, you know, they're BFFs and and uh Elijah's on the phone with God and, "Oh yeah."

But I don't think that that's how this happened at all. You see, because Elijah is a human being just like all of us. And so if we look at our experience, if we look at our experience, what we see, of course, is that we've got all kinds of voices in our head. We've got all kinds of desires, we have all kinds of ideas about what the right thing to do is or what we're interested in. Right? We've got a lot of conflicting thoughts and opinions, things like that. It's hard to tell which is the voice that is of God in your head. Right? How did Elijah know that that was God? And particularly if God is telling us to do something that we are not used to doing.

Right? In the church world, all you have to say is something like, "Well, maybe we should move the worship time half an hour." This strikes terror into the heart of every pastor. Because we know what is going to happen. That could not possibly be of God, we've always done it this way. One particular church that I served, I literally had a couple come in the door, and this was in 2005, and say to me, "Oh, you've changed the time of the worship service." I said, "Oh, that's interesting. When was the last time you were here?" They said, "1964." They were very upset. 41 years later, the worship was at a different time.

And so we can think about, what if one of the voices in your head says, "Go into the desert where there's no food and water"? Are we really going to consider that that is the voice of God? The answer is probably not. Probably not. We would we have gone? Would we have gone? You see, if if the way of God out of the kingdom of the world is not wide, well populated, and well funded, is that the thing that we would consider doing? Right? Is that the thing that we would consider doing? The way of God appears narrow and dry not because it actually is narrow and dry, but because that's the way it seems from the perspectives of the kingdom of the world. Right? From the perspective of normal society, normal society, right, where there's a lot of glitz and glitter and wealth, and where we're under pressure all the time, and where our attention is being diverted in all kinds of different ways, where we're being told over and over and over again from the time that we're in school and can remember that you've got to get a good job and you've got to make a lot of money and then you've got to get your retirement accounts together and then you've got to get a bigger and better thing and over and over more and more and more. Right? From that perspective, this way of God seems almost invisible or silly or too hard. Right? I can't tell you the number of times that people have said to me, "Oh, that sounds like a lot of work." As if our regular life in the world isn't a lot of work. But it's just the work that we're used to. Right? It's the habitual pattern that we have been given.

I want to take us back a long, long time. And sometimes when we say a long, long time, we think, oh, you know, a few hundred years, or a few thousand years, maybe even. No, I want to take us back a few hundred thousand years, which is really a point in human history that is so long ago that we almost don't have any collective memory of this. But one of the things about human beings that we have to recognize, you see, is that with the exception of Africa, human beings are invasive species on this entire planet. Right? Even the people that us white folks talk about as being indigenous are not indigenous to most of the places on Earth. And the fact is that slowly this species developed and began to just fan out over the Earth. Right? And we went to all these different places. And as time grew, there were more and more waves of taking over and accumulation, one group of people on top of another. And as species, as biological creatures, we developed this habit of just taking, right? Taking, accumulating, creating societies where we were trying to survive and where we were extracting from the environment. And that really is this incredibly long history, this incredibly long history of what we call this collective ego development. Right? That we become worried and concerned about our survival, and so we feel like we have to have more and more and more and more. And that habit is so old and is so ancient that we just talk about it as, "Well, that's just human nature." Right? This is just the way we are. There's no way out of that. I hear this all the time. Right? We're very sort of satisfied with our nature. Even though our nature visits suffering upon each other over and over and over again.

And so the spiritual life, when Jesus comes along, when these various other figures and people and teachers in different societies come along, what they're essentially saying and pointing to is we need a different nature. We need a different nature. Right? This particular iteration of ourselves as a species is getting to the point where our habit of accumulation, our habit of lack of sharing, our habit of lack of care is literally threatening the entire ecosystem of the planet.

Right? And so when Jesus comes along and says, "You know, there's a road to life that goes in a different direction." When God comes along and says to Elijah, "You know, there's this other place for you to go and spend some time before I call you again." And what is it that God does when God calls Elijah out of that place? It's a highly symbolic and important act. Elijah emerges from the desert and is sent to the gate of the city where there is a widow with a child. Right? And God says to Elijah, "Go and ask the widow for something to eat." And the widow says, "Well, I was just going to go home and bake our last meal, we have enough flour for one loaf of bread, and then we are going to die. We're going to starve to death." And you see, this is a highly symbolic moment because in the Bible, the the gold standard of the just society is that widows and orphans have enough to eat. And so God is sending Elijah to the lowest of the low, the person who is starving to death, who is completely expendable in this ancient society. And Elijah says, "Well, no, I will go home with you and let's see what happens." And this flour, this meal multiplies and she is able to feed herself and her son. Right? So out of this dryness, right, in many ways the spiritual life, spiritual practice is talked about as being the path of dryness. This path when we are expunging all of this other habit from ourselves. And we enter into this space that feels empty, it feels dry. We we kind of panic because there's this sense of, "Oh my gosh, what am I going to do with myself when I'm not doing all the things that I'm used to doing, when we as a society are not doing all the things we're used to doing?"

But the thing is we emerge from that path of dryness into a land of life. Right? One of the things in one of the communities that I was a part of, we we found out our our church was made aware of the fact because, you know, churches are filled with people generally speaking that are pretty well off. Right? So they're not often aware of some of the things that are happening in their community. And the church was made aware of the fact that there were children in the school district that did not have enough money to pay for lunch. Right? And we said, "Well, gee, that doesn't sound so great." So somebody in the congregation came up with this incredibly simple idea. He said, you know, "Bring some spare change, whatever spare change you've got, put that spare change in the offering plate. Any change that's in the offering plate, we're going to give to the school to pay for lunch. We'll see how that does." And of course, the people that did that, nobody missed that money. You know, I didn't notice anybody losing their house or losing their car or anything because they put a little spare change in the offering plate. And just that money, without anybody even thinking about it, without anybody truly sacrificing, without anybody even almost noticing, that money was enough uh to pay for every lunch that needed to be paid for in the school system. This is the thing about the path of life. It may seem small, it may seem dry, it may seem unappealing, but actually when you get on it, when you get on it, we find that the Spirit moves us and enlivens us and transforms us. As we're willing to let go of the kingdoms of the world, to seek first the kingdom of God. Amen.
