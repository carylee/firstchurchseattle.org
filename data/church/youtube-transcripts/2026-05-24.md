### Sermon
**(Pastor Elizabeth - "What We Need Is the Spirit")**
Thank you, Ann.
Just a few weeks before the story that Ann just read for us from the second chapter of Acts, remember that the Apostle Peter was in despair because he had lied and cursed and denied even knowing Jesus. He had told Jesus that he would follow Him to the death. And of course, Jesus said, "No, you'll deny me three times." And Jesus was right, as Jesus usually is. And then Jesus died and rose again. And after spending 40 days with His followers, He ascended into heaven, which is in Acts 1. It's one of the my favorite stories in the whole Bible because, this isn't in the sermon, because the apostles are all standing around staring at the clouds where Jesus just disappeared. And then these angels show up and are like, "Why are you staring at the sky?" And it's just such a great image for us, for today. But anyway.
So that's all happened. But before Jesus leaves them, He tells the apostles, "Stay here, don't leave this place until you receive the Holy Spirit. That you'll receive power in the Spirit, and you will be my witnesses to the ends of the earth."

And sure enough, when we get to Acts chapter 2, they're all together in one place when suddenly this sound of a violent wind comes from heaven and fills the place where they are sitting. The doors are not open. They don't know where this wind comes from. And then tongues of fire come to rest on each of them. They're all filled with the Holy Spirit. And suddenly, they can speak other languages, not made up languages, but real languages they had never studied, which travelers from all over the world could understand.

You see, there was already a huge crowd in Jerusalem at that time, with faithful Jews from all over the known world, because even before this big event that we celebrate in the church, Pentecost was already an important religious holiday. On the 50th day after Passover, Jews would gather for a big agricultural festival, when farmers would bring the first bundle of wheat from the year, and offer it to God, partly in gratitude and partly hoping for blessings that the rest of the harvest would come in just as well.

It's also the day that they remembered that 50 days after the very first Passover, after the Israelites were freed from slavery in Egypt and passed through the Red Sea into the wilderness on the way to the promised land. On that 50th day, Moses went up on Mount Sinai to meet with God and came down with the stone tablets containing the Ten Commandments. So they gathered on Pentecost also to celebrate the law by which they lived.

And when the writer of Acts begins telling this story, he assumes we know all of this background, the significance of the day of Pentecost before this happens. That we remember the liberation of Jews from slavery in Egypt. That we remember the giving of the law as a gift. That we remember the offering of the first fruits, praying God will continue to be faithful and bring even more.

And the first hearers of this story would also have known the story of the Tower of Babel, maybe a story you heard in Sunday school from Genesis 11. And that story begins by saying, "Now the whole world had one language and common speech." And with that common speech, the people had decided to build a city with a tower that would reach to the heavens so that they could make a name for themselves. With that common speech, they were working together, not for the kingdom of God, but for their own recognition. So, God gave them different languages so they could not understand each other. And then, instead of building that great tower, they dispersed all over the world.

Then, in the very next chapter of Genesis, after the Tower of Babel, God gave a promise to Abraham, saying, "I'll make of you a great nation, and I will bless you, and through you all the peoples of the earth will be blessed." And while Abraham and his family followed God faithfully, going wherever God asked them to go, they wondered how this blessing would ever take place. How he could possibly reach the entire world to bless all the peoples of the earth, because they had just been scattered to the four winds. They were speaking dozens of languages now. They couldn't even understand one another. And yet, God said that this thing would happen.

So the first hearers of this story from Acts 2 would have known all of that. And what's more, is that they would have been waiting for this time when things would become plain. When God would make clear just how all the peoples of the earth would be blessed. Because up to this point, God had seemed to just inspire one or two people at a time, prophets or priests or kings. But on this day, suddenly, in a burst of divine, fresh energy, God's spirit was poured out on a lot of people all at once. In fact, the text says, "the Spirit was poured out on all people," or it can be translated, "on all flesh." All people, regardless of age or gender or social status or occupation. And they started speaking languages they never knew, so that even though they came from the ends of the earth, everyone could hear something they could understand.

And that seems wild to us. But imagine how much more unfathomable it was to them. Before the dawn of things like translation apps, or those earbuds that can do the translating for you in real time. People who spoke different languages, who had never maybe encountered the other, could understand each other? Half the time, we don't even understand each other when we're speaking the same language. So we know how remarkable this is.

And so, it's not a huge surprise that some of the onlookers said, "What does this mean?" And others assumed the apostles were drunk. That they'd had too much wine. Though I don't know about you, but I'm not sure I've ever seen wine make people speak more clearly than they did before. Maybe it was different wine.

So Peter gets up, again, just a couple of weeks after he's denied Jesus. And he addresses this huge crowd. And I have to say that if all of my friends were spouting foreign languages, as a result of the Holy Spirit resting on their heads like tongues of fire, I don't know for sure that my first instinct would be to turn to a lengthy quotation of scripture. But this seems to work for Peter, who knows what God is doing, and turns to the prophet Joel, in chapter 2, amending Joel's prophecy just a bit. He says, "In the last days, God says, 'I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh, on all people. Your sons and daughters will prophesy, your young men will see visions, your old men will dream dreams. Even on my servants, both men and women, I will pour out my Spirit in those days and they will prophesy. And everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.'"

Peter begins by saying, "in the last days," to indicate that this is the beginning of something new. That the promise of Abraham is finally coming to fulfillment. That this band of frightened, confused, largely uneducated people, these misfits with very few credentials, have just become prophets. And he doesn't mean prophets like Nostradamus or fortune tellers, people who predict the future. He means prophets like messengers for God.

Through these people, this group of ordinary individuals who've gathered because they're interested in this man, Jesus, who died and came back to life and then ascended into heaven, and they're not sure what they're supposed to do now that He's gone. These people are the ones who will tell the whole world that grace is available to everyone. The flames don't descend on the heads of the seminarians, or in the halls of the government, or in the inner rooms of the temple. The flames don't appear over the heads of the wealthy, or the powerful, or the especially beautiful. These ordinary people get overwhelmed, in the best way, by the Holy Spirit. And as a result, they can't stop talking about what they've seen and heard.

And it makes me wonder about us. We talk sometimes about the Holy Spirit. We talk sometimes about the spiritual life. But I wonder how much we really believe that the Spirit will show up for us. That we are included when the prophet says, "I will pour out my Spirit on all people." I wonder how much we believe that God wants us to gather together around the stories of Jesus, to be present for that wind, which sometimes comes in like an earthquake, and sometimes is much more subtle.

I wonder how much we believe that God will continue to pour out God's Spirit on all people, regardless of gender identity or age, regardless of whether they feel bound or have already found freedom. I wonder how open we are to receiving this Spirit, which according to Acts, is the very power of God.

What would we do if that wind rushed into this room? If tongues of fire rested on our heads? Would we begin to prophesy? Go to tell the good news of God's grace to the ends of the earth? Or would our skepticism, or our cynicism, have us make fun of those who embrace their new gifts, and say simply, "They must be drunk."

Other biblical writers talk about the Spirit in different ways. Paul largely refers to it as the force that unites us, that draws us together, that makes us a part of the body of Christ, together, and gives us gifts to serve that body. And John talks about the Spirit as our advocate. And other scriptures tell us that the Spirit will intercede for us when we don't know how to pray, and that the Spirit is the source of all wisdom, the wisdom within us and around us, that is the one that allows our lives to bear fruit, the fruits of the Spirit, like love and joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. That the Spirit gives us our vocation. That it leads us toward sanctification or holiness. The Spirit is so important in the life of faith, and we read about it from this point on in the New Testament. And it, we'll just say, the Spirit also then becomes the feminine part of God, which is not a small thing.

But in this story, the focus of today, of Pentecost, the Spirit is the unruly, unpredictable power of God that is given to all people. Now, what the Spirit will do when it arrives for you is anybody's guess. We are not generally in control of the agenda when the Spirit shows up. But what's clear from the book of Acts is that when the Spirit falls on us, not if, but when, amazing things happen, things beyond our abilities. And it's all for the glory of God. When the Spirit arrives, it's part of the new creation being ushered in. It's the ability and the inspiration to speak the good news in whatever language it needs to be told, to bless all the peoples of at least our corner of the earth.

This little group of followers was ignited on that first Pentecost, the day we refer to as the birthday of the church. And the good news began to spread like wildfire. This little spark, this little group of misfits, started a movement that in short order did reach the ends of the earth. A movement that we are still part of, thousands of years later. Those gathered on that early Pentecost brought in the first of the wheat to ask God to bless the crops and bring in a bountiful harvest. And that little group of apostles was the first fruits for the kingdom of God, the initial harvest, on which God poured out a blessing so great, its fulfillment is still to come and is dependent largely on us.

So we often think of Pentecost as the birthday of the church. But I hope it's not a day when we just look back at faded pictures. When we just tell stories of days gone by, the good old days when everything was perfect. I hope it's not a day that stays in the past. As something that happened once, but will never happen again. Because God said that the Spirit would come for all people. Not just the people in that room. Not just the people of that day. That you and I can prophesy, and dream, and have visions in our own language, not for our glory, but for the sake of the kingdom of God. That everyone to the ends of the earth would know God's blessing. That all who call on the name of the Lord would know God's saving power.

Jesus promised that He wouldn't leave them alone. He left earth for heaven, but He sent heaven to earth. And He sends heaven still. To anyone who's open to receiving such unpredictable fire. A new age was ushered in on that Pentecost. An age in which there's no discrimination. But everyone among us is a prophet, a dreamer, a visionary. We all have the power to transform the world with good news. And so on this Pentecost, we pray, "Come, Holy Spirit, come. Rest on our heads."

We need You. Amen.
