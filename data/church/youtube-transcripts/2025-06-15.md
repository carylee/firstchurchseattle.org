## Sermon: Bridges Made of Music: Where Words Alone Can't Go
**Preacher:** Don Crawley
**Scripture:** Psalm 96:1-3, 11-13; Ephesians 5:18-20

[55:04]
Ever sat by someone's hospital bed and realized you had no words? Words failed you. That's when music speaks. Words stumble, they falter in hospital rooms. They collapse into the weight of grief. They scatter in the face of awe. When your heart is broken, when your soul rejoices, when your spirit is searching, language often fails, but music steps in.

Music goes where words can't. It builds bridges where silence settles. It reaches across time, across cultures, across pain, joy, and memory. It connects us to God, to one another, and to our deepest selves. The prophet Zephaniah said, "The Lord your God will rejoice over you with singing." Let that settle in for a moment. God is singing over you. Music is not just our gift to God, it's God's gift to us. You don't need to be a trained singer. You don't need to have perfect pitch. You just need a heart that listens and a spirit willing to sing.

Over the next three messages—they're short, I promise—we'll explore how music becomes more than sound. It becomes a sacred bridge. First, the bridge that exists between us and the divine. Then the bridge between us and each other. And then the bridge between us ourselves: the person we used to be, the person we are today, and the amazing person that we're becoming. Somewhere in the music, the one who created you, who created you from stardust, is already singing back to you.

Part one: Music as a bridge between us and God. Music has always helped humans reach for the divine. It expresses what words cannot, in joy, sorrow, confusion or clarity. Music closes the gap between us and the Holy Spirit. It softens us, it lifts us. It opens space for God's presence. Not as an idea, but as a lived experience. Music bypasses logic and speaks directly to the soul. That's why it's so central to worship. When we sing, we offer more than our voices. We offer our whole selves. Music gives shape to what we can't say and draws us closer to the sacred. Scripture is filled with song. The Psalms are ancient hymns of course, some jubilant, some full of lament. Psalm 96, as you just heard, invites us to "sing to the Lord a new song. Sing to the Lord, all the earth." Singing reminds us of who we are and whose we are.

Music transcends boundaries. A Southern spiritual, a Latin chant, a modern worship song, or a 300-year-old hymn. They each offer a path to the divine. When we sing together, we're no longer individuals, but we are a community, joined in one voice and one spirit. Think of the African-American spirituals, songs born out of suffering and strength. "There Is a Balm in Gilead" or "Swing Low, Sweet Chariot." These were not just melodies, they were they were lifelines. They carried people through the worst with hope in their hearts. When everything else was stripped away, music remained. Music prayed. And music believed.

Songs also hold sacred memory. Maybe there's a song tied to a baptism or a funeral or a mountaintop experience or a moment of stillness in prayer that resonates with you. Hear it again, even decades later, and it's as if God taps you on your shoulder and says, "I was there then, and I'm here with you now." For me, one of those songs is "In the Garden," which is the last song I remember playing when my father was preaching. And today, when I hear that silly old song "In the Garden," it always takes me back to that time, and I feel the touch of the divine. Even without lyrics, a simple melody can pull you into that thin place where the spirit moves. Romans 8 reminds us that the spirit prays for us with "sighs too deep for words." You see, sometimes it's not about singing the right notes or the right words, it's about being present and letting the music do the praying for you. Think of the "Barber Adagio," not one single word, and yet it's a direct expressway to the Lord.

And that brings us to "Yonder Come Day." This powerful choral anthem, rooted in the spiritual tradition of the Georgia Sea Islands, is a declaration. The day is a-breakin', the sun is a-risin' in your soul. Yonder come day.

**Don Crawley:**
Music is one of the most powerful tools we have for building connection. This part is called "Music is a bridge between us and other people." Music crosses boundaries of language, culture, age and ideology. In a world so terribly divided as ours is now, music opens space for people to relate, not as strangers or opponents or competitors, but as fellow humans, one human to another. From lullabies sung to children to the songs that we share at weddings, in school, around a campfire, some of us remember singing "Michael Row the Boat Ashore" or "Kumbaya" around a campfire. These shared songs become emotional touchstones, familiar sounds that bind us to the people we sing them with. Whether humming along with a friend, harmonizing in a choir or even singing the national anthem with thousands, music connects us deeply.

Music also helps us enter experiences that may be very different from our own. A protest song. A deeply disturbing spiritual or folk tune from another culture becomes an invitation into someone else's story. Billie Holiday's haunting "Strange Fruit," which if you've never heard, you need to hear it. It offers a searing look into racial violence in America. Songs like that don't just educate. They awaken compassion and empathy. Music can even bridge generational divides. A hymn passed down through generations or a timeless Beatles song might connect a grandparent and grandchild who share little else in pop culture. Singing or listening together becomes a shared moment that words alone simply can't create. Group music-making, like being part of a choir or a band, builds connection to yet another way. It requires cooperation, attentiveness and mutual respect. Everyone has a role. The sopranos can't overpower the altos. The drummer can't overpower the bass. The conductor leads the ensemble, but everyone plays an important role and everyone everyone's role is critical to the success of the ensemble. It's it's a picture of healthy human relationships, really. Listening, adjusting, and contributing to something bigger than ourselves. And my father always said that one of the keys to happiness is to believe in something bigger than yourself. So maybe one of the keys to connection is to believe in something bigger than yourself.

Even in times of conflict or grief, music can be a bridge to healing. Think about after apartheid in South Africa. Music became the connection between people who were formerly enemies, now working to achieve some sort of peace. In hospital rooms, at vigils, in times of loss, music often says what words cannot. A familiar song, like "Amazing Grace," can bring comfort to a room full of strangers who for just a few minutes are united. Think of President Obama spontaneously singing "Amazing Grace" during the eulogy for Reverend Clementa Pinckney following the tragedy at Emanuel AME Church. And that's the power of of music. It draws the humanity out in each of us and offers it to others.

Which brings us to our next anthem, "Sing, My Child." This piece celebrates joy, resilience and connection. Its message is simple and profound. Even when the night is long, you are not alone. And you still have a song to sing. When troubles come and worry is all around, gather your strength. Hear your voice and sing, my child.

**Don Crawley:**
Part three: Music as a bridge across our lifelong journey. Music doesn't just connect us to God, as we explored in part one. And it doesn't just connect us to each other, as we explored in part two. Music connects us to ourselves. Music weaves through every season of our life. It becomes the soundtrack to our growth, our struggles, our joy, and our transformation. In fact, the radio station consultant Lee Abrams once said that the music that was popular when you were in high school in the early days of college becomes the music, the soundtrack of your entire life. And there's some truth to that.

Think back to a song you loved as a child. Maybe it was a lullaby, a hymn in church, or a pop song you played over and over on your Walkman or Spotify playlist, or for some of you it was a 78 RPM record player. Hearing it now takes you back instantly, doesn't it? You remember where you were, how you felt, even what it smelled like in that moment. Music has that kind of power. It stirs memory, awakens emotion, and draws a line from your past self to your present one and to the future. But this this connection isn't just nostalgia. It's a reminder that you've been on a journey all along, haven't you? That younger version of you, hopeful, unsure, maybe even hurting, left musical footprints along the way. And when you revisit those songs, they remind you of how far you've come.

Just as music opens a sacred bridge to God, and just as it helps you relate more deeply to each other, it also helps you make sense of your own story. It holds up a mirror. It helps you see yourself more clearly. In moments of transition, graduation, marriage, retirement, loss, we often turn to music, don't we? We don't just talk about what's happening, we sing. That favorite hymn at a funeral, that first dance song, that breakup playlist. The songs we choose are more than just background noise. They're markers that say, "I was here. I felt this. I lived through it." And music doesn't stop at remembering. It also invites us forward. Songs of courage, hope, healing, and purpose can become anthems for the person we're becoming. When we listen to the music that reflects who we want to be, it encourages us to take a step toward that person. That's why the anthem "Sing, My Child" matters so deeply. It reminds us that we still have a song to sing. Not just someday, but now. Not once we've figured everything out, but in the middle of becoming. It says, "Your voice matters. Your story matters. Sing anyway." And "Yonder Come Day"? It's a song of hope on the horizon. It doesn't pretend everything is perfect because it's not. It simply says, "The dawn is a-coming. The sun is a-rising. You're not finished. You're in motion." And music walks with you every step of the way.

Music helps you remember where you've been. Stay rooted in where you are. And reach toward that amazing person you're becoming. Music sends the message, "You are worthy. Your life matters. You are a beautiful and loved child of God." Music is not just a sound. It's a bridge to your soul. Say Hallelujah and Amen.

God of glory, God of love;
hearts unfold like flowers before thee,
opening to the sun above.

giver of immortal gladness,

lift us to the joy divine.

victors in the midst of strife;
joyful music leads us homeward
in the triumph song of life.
