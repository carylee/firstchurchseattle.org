## Sermon: What We Need Is Justice
**Preacher:** Pastor Elizabeth
**Scripture:** Isaiah 58:1-12, Luke 10:25-37

A few weeks ago, um, whenever people drove over the metal plates in the corner here, it was so loud that you all kind of um, startled a little bit. And so after that service, I sent a message to the Department of Transportation and said, you know, this is difficult for our services, and I just was wondering about the timeline. Can we just know how long this is going to be a major distraction during our worship services. And they didn't, um, write back, but they did bring a backhoe, uh, into the street for today's service. And I'm going to take that to mean, um, they really want to get the project done quickly, uh, so that we will not be disturbed much longer. Um, and in the meantime, we'll just, you know, focus and it'll be character building for us and we'll, um, we'll take that as a gift.

We have the joy of welcoming new members into our congregation, uh, later in the service today. And it's always an honor and a privilege when folks feel drawn to the church, uh, and then also feel moved to commit to it, to making the congregation better, to supporting and being supported by this community. And it's also a really fun thing and a gift for pastors to get to meet with folks who are considering membership, to learn more of their stories and, um, and it's also a great exercise for us, uh, to go back to the basics in these conversations and to remember, uh, and to articulate again what it means to be a Christian and a United Methodist and a member of a congregation. What it means to commit oneself to a particular expression of faith, knowing no church will ever be perfect, will always be human, we will always make mistakes. But that church is the strange and extraordinary way that God wants us to organize ourselves to be community for one another.

And each time I meet with folks who want to learn more about church membership, I tend to start at the very beginning, with what it means to be a Christian. Something we don't always talk a lot about, but assume we're all on the same page about. And I think being a Christian doesn't necessarily mean that we agree with or can affirm all of the different phrases of the historic creeds of the church, though we do hope and trust that together those are our beliefs. But I think being a Christian does mean that we're compelled to follow Jesus. That we want to be his disciples, to model our lives after his life and to live as both recipients and conveyers of God's grace.

But church membership is really just an extension of our baptism. Whether we were baptized as infants or children or we've made the decision as adults. Our ability and the call to commit to a congregation actually begins with our being initiated into the family of God at baptism. Where we're asked among other things to accept the freedom and power God gives us to resist evil, injustice, and oppression in whatever forms they present themselves.

And a few years ago a small group in a campaign for inclusivity, uh, particularly gender and sexuality inclusivity in the UMC, um, produced T-shirts that said those words in rainbow letters, resisting evil, injustice, and oppression in whatever forms they present themselves. The words kind of got smaller as they went because there's a lot of words uh for one T-shirt. But I got one of these shirts and it quickly became one of my favorites, not only because it's rainbow and it matches with everything, but also because it was really soft and just a comfortable T-shirt. And sometimes I forgot that I was carrying these words around so publicly. I mean I still wear it sometimes. It's getting really faded, so I don't wear it as much. That's why I'm putting this in the past tense. But sometimes I forget that I'm wearing these kind of powerful words um really publicly. And so then I'm surprised when someone says, oh, I like your shirt. And I have to look down and say, what shirt am I wearing? And then my response is always, oh, this is part of my baptismal vows. And it's just amazing to me how many people, many of whom have been baptized, even as United Methodists, who don't recognize those words. How many people don't recognize that resisting evil and injustice, working for justice is part of our call as Christians.

And this is one of the things that I think is most distinctive about being a United Methodist and one of the things that I love the most about our very imperfect denomination. And that's that we have always tried to strike a balance between what John Wesley called personal piety and social holiness. Which means in our tradition, faith and belief are important, and they move us to action. We don't think that Christianity is just about our own personal relationship with God, and it's certainly not all about earning a ticket to heaven, which seems to be the focus of many Christian groups. We are serious about our own personal faith. We're serious about our own spiritual journey. We think it's really important to grow closer to God throughout our lives, to keep listening for God's voice, so that we might get closer and closer to what Wesley called perfection in love, that we might get closer and closer to the heart of God. But we don't think that is the end of the life of faith.

Instead, we believe as Methodists that faith must move us to action. If our faith is only for us, then we have missed the point. If it is only about being certain that we'll go to heaven after we die, then we've only gotten a tiny sliver of the movement that God has called us into. Instead, we believe that because of God's grace, because God loved the world enough to send God's only Son, because we are made in the image of a God who creates and redeems and sustains and transforms, we too are called to change the world for the better. And this is the call of justice.

We use the word justice in our worship services every week. Someone will say something about the call to justice or moving the world toward love and justice or peace and justice, all the time. And we can be proud of our history as a church of working for justice. But it's also occurred to me recently that we might not all mean the same thing when we use that word. Because there's so many ways to define justice. So I thought it might be helpful for us to spend a little time today thinking about what the word justice means, particularly in the context of the readings that we've just heard, first from the prophet Isaiah, who asks us to loose the bonds of injustice. And then how that might be related to Jesus's command for us to love our neighbor as in the story of the Good Samaritan.

And one thing to note as we begin that exploration, is that throughout scripture, God calls us not just to want justice, or to campaign for justice, or even to pray for justice, though those are good things. But God wants us to do justice, all throughout the Bible. It's an active thing, something that requires our energy and effort, a habit that we build across time and not just a one-time event. But God is not asking us in scripture to enforce justice. In the way we might think of the justice system in our country as the organization of laws and the consequences we face if we break them. When we talk about justice in the church, we're talking about something bigger than a community's laws, or even the rules that we ourselves have agreed to. God's version of justice isn't a political system, and it's certainly not partisan. Though many people in American culture seem to assume that it is a particularly liberal quest. Instead, the goal of justice, according to the prophets, is community. It's about the ongoing relationship of both family and strangers. And doing whatever it takes for those relationships in community to reflect the heart of a God who so loves the world. And in order for us to be community, even with strangers, and for that community to look like the heart of God, justice necessarily means working for a world in which everyone belongs, where the neediest are taken care of and where no one is hungry or disenfranchised.

Now sometimes, if we're thinking about justice and we want to simplify it for ourselves, we might think about justice as simply being what's fair. Sometimes we equate those two words, justice and fairness. Which would mean that everyone gets the same thing, no matter where we've started from. And in that sense, if we believe justice is just about fairness, then we can argue that things like supplemental food assistance are not fair. Because everyone doesn't receive the same thing. Or sometimes we think of what's fair in terms of getting what we deserve. If I've worked really hard, then I deserve the good things that I have. But if we equate justice with fairness in that way, it allows us to argue that it is fair for you to struggle, if your struggle is the result of your decisions. If I've made good decisions and I'm thriving, so be it, that's fair. And it allows us to keep distance between one another.

But if we go deeper into what we believe about God and creation and grace, then we have to remember that nothing is actually ours. We believe that all of life is a gift from God. It is essentially on loan to us. We didn't earn life or breath or motivation or inspiration or intelligence. None of that is ours to claim. All of it is gift. And if we believe that all of life belongs to God, if it's all gift, then we have no claim. We have no right to keep what is currently in our possession. Or to withhold it from someone else. Because God's intention has always been that we share what we have and we support one another in this community of friends and strangers.

And that means justice isn't just doing what's right for me, or working on my relationship with God, or my ticket to the afterlife. And it also isn't only doing the things that I'm interested in. Advocating for the things I think are the most important. Instead, then, the call to work for justice is about enacting God's will for all creation. Working to ensure that everyone has what they need. That even the most hard to love stranger is included. It might mean stopping on the side of the road because someone needs your help. Even if you have really good other work to do. Like the priest and the Levite in the story of the Good Samaritan, had good work to be doing. It also means stopping to help, even if it's someone you don't especially like. Or someone you've been taught to avoid. Even if you have no assurance that that someone will take what you offer and do good with it. And because doing justice isn't a one-time event, because it's a daily habit, it also means that when we see someone in need on the side of the road, someone who has been attacked in a place where attacks often happen, then the call to justice should lead us to work with the local government to put up lights on the road. The call to justice would lead us to walk beside that traveler so that they won't be afraid next time. And it would also call us to be in relationship with the robbers who wait in hiding along the way. Asking what it is they need so that they might not feel the need to attack anyone on the road anymore.

The reading we heard from Isaiah 58 today is one of my favorites from the prophets. And a lot of that is because of the imagery in it. I just love this idea of being repairers of the breach and restorers of streets to live in. There's just so much hope and beauty in those words. But I also love this text because it calls us out of ourselves. And if you go back and read it, not right this minute, but later today, um it can also sound a little bit like the writer is a pastor. And he does what pastors sometimes do occasionally when they get together, and he starts complaining about his church. But what's interesting about this complaint is that while everyone else is saying, oh gosh, my people just love brunch and they won't come to Sunday service, this pastor is saying, my congregation will not stop coming to church. They're just there all the time. I mean, he's just complaining, but he's also saying, um, this group seeks God continually, and they delight to know God's ways day after day. They're fasting, they're humbling themselves, they are doing the spiritual practices. And all the other pastors are saying, wait, what is it we're complaining about because that's precisely what I was hoping for. He's saying, oh, this church is on fire for God. And we're all thinking, oh, gosh, that sounds really hard. And yet, the prophet then says, the issue isn't that they keep showing up. It's that they've only gotten part of the story. They haven't realized yet that their call is also to look for the people who are bound and to liberate them. To not only find food for the hungry, but to share their own food. To not only build shelters for those who don't have homes, but to invite them into their own homes. He says, they are on fire for their own relationship with God, but what they haven't figured out yet is that all we have is a gift. And the call to justice means sharing it.

And you can absolutely do that outside the church. There are all kinds of organizations that are working for justice and doing very good work. But I think church is an especially helpful place to learn how to work for justice. And part of that is because church is one of the only places that people still gather without being in affinity groups. It's one of the only places now where we're asked to commit to a community without yet knowing if we agree with everyone in the community. Or without knowing whether we would really like to spend more time with everyone else in the community. Or if we like the same things or vote the same way. Or invest our time and energy in the same activities. It's one of the only places that we come where our stories are so widely different. We come for very different places, different times. We have different gifts, and different struggles, and different experiences. We let anyone in the door. And part of why we come here is to practice that sharing. We share our prayers, our presence, our gifts, our service, our witness, even with people who might rub us the wrong way. Even with people we may have been taught to avoid.

When we come to church, we're reminded that everything we have is a gift. That we have not earned even one iota of God's grace. And our call is not only to soak up as much of God's love and grace as we can. But also to pour it out in love and compassion and peace in the world. To loose the bonds of injustice. To be repairers of the breach. Restorers of cities to live in. May God make us faithful to that call. Amen.
