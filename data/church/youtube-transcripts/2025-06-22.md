## Sermon: Compassion is Liberation
**Preacher:** Rev. Dan Wolpert
**Scripture:** Jeremiah 31:31-34 (NRSVUE), Matthew 5:33-37 (NRSVUE)
Will you please pray with me?

Well, thank you for having me back again. I guess I didn't do too badly the first time. And this is the second in four four parts, uh looking at these different practices that Jesus teaches us about helping us to move into a transformed society. How do we actually get there? What are the things that we can do to move our communities in that direction? So the first month we're looking at this very basic practice of where do we put our attention? Our attention is demanded by many, many things. We have choices about where we can put our attention. And the spiritual life calls us to put our spiritual life and practice as the primary focus of our attention, something that is very rare.

So this time, this time we are looking at one of several relational practices. So again, we're looking at ourselves and community, so I am an individual, but spiritual life is lived out in community. And so what is it that we need to do to engage relationship in a new and different kind of way, right? If if the kingdoms of the world are inviting us into kinds of relationship that ultimately lead to things like oppression, violence, things like that, what are new ways of doing relationship? And so the the one particular practice that I'm going to spend time on is this practice of compassion, and compassion as liberation.

So the first war in the Middle East in my lifetime was the 1967 war. And so here we are six decades later, and every decade, there is at least one, sometimes two or three wars in the Middle East. This seems to be the thing that we love to do and can't stop doing. Right? And so uh, so once again, here we are in this place of what is basically has been described by a number of theologians that war is the ultimate failure of our humanity. So here we are once again, failing. Go team. Right. Uh, and but that's important that that's the starting place, right? Because that is what is real, what is actually happening.

Uh so one of my favorite movies is a movie called K-PAX, like a radio station, K-P-A-X. Uh this is a movie that was not a commercial success, uh but is absolutely brilliant. It is one of the now, I was going to say, it is probably the best portrayal of what it would be like to actually meet Jesus that I've ever seen. But it's a science fiction movie, and the main character is named Prot, and Prot is an alien, and he appears in Grand Central Station one day, and is very quickly ushered into the psychiatric unit at Bellevue Hospital. Because the thing about Prot is that he always tells the truth. And our basic relational stance that we are taught by the kingdoms of the world is to not tell the truth. Right? We we lie all the time. We lie all the time. Right? Uh, oh, yeah, I'd love to get together with you. Actually, that is the thing I would hate the most, but I'm not going to say that, right? Oh, yes, I'll I'll call. I'll email. Oh, yes, I would love to be on that committee. Right? These these are all lies. Right? Uh, and and we are taught that that is what is the socially acceptable way of functioning in our society, and these little lies begin to add up to bigger and bigger and bigger lies. And of course, when we enter the the whole socio-political realm, uh, we this this practice, this habit selects for people who are completely sociopathic. Right? who have no problem lying every single time they open their mouths. But because we are all trained into this, what happens is, is that we very easily uh follow these people and forgive these things because we are in denial about the fact that our entire social structure is based on these falsehoods in relationship.

So Prot shows up in Grand Central Station and people ask him who he is, and he says, well, I'm an alien. I'm here visiting Earth. Which is the truth, except that's not an appropriate answer. So off he goes to the locked ward in Bellevue Hospital. And there's all kinds of really interesting things that happen in the movie, but most of the film centers around the relationship that he has with the lead psychiatrist on the unit who becomes his therapist. And of course, uh, Prot ends up doing a lot more therapy on the doctor than the other way around. And the doctor starts asking him about, well, what's it like on your planet, you know? And he's explaining to him what's it like on your planet and on his planet. And uh, he says, well, well, do you have any laws on your planet? And he says, no, we have no laws and no lawyers. And the doctor is very puzzled by this of course, and he says, uh, he says, well, if you don't have any laws, how do people know what is right and wrong? And Prot says to him, well, every being in the universe knows the difference between right and wrong.

This idea that Jesus presents to us of don't make an oath about something on something, but simply tell the truth, sounds so ridiculously simple. Just say yes when you mean yes and no when you mean no. I talk to people all the time about how this is almost impossible for them. Their whole books written on how to say no. Right? And I just said, somebody say to me yesterday, I just I I haven't been able to figure out how to say no. Right? So this practice of compassion is a practice that begins with our relationship to ourselves. As a spiritual director and a therapist, I talk to people all the time about a very simple practice, which is this practice of asking yourself on a regular basis throughout the day, what do I really want? And you would be amazed at the number of people who say to me, I have absolutely no idea. I have absolutely no. It has been completely trained out of us. Because we are told from a very early age what's important is not what we want, what's important is what somebody else wants. Right? What's important is what our teachers want, what's important is the right answer on the exam, what's important is what our parents want, what's important is what our peer group wants. And over the years, we are trained out of this basic attention to who we are. So we become completely disconnected from ourselves. And we're even taught in many circumstances and situations that what we want is inherently bad.

So compassion is the practice of beginning to undo all of that. Can I even within the privacy of our own mind, my own mind, tell myself the truth? Can I even tell myself the truth when even nobody else is listening? You'd be amazed how hard that is for lots of people. I can't even admit to myself what I am thinking and feeling. This is the ultimate form of internalized oppression. Right? These are the things that the kingdoms of the world put inside of us. I can't say yes or no in a simple truthful way because I don't even know what the yes and no are. So to begin to practice compassion starts with that safe space that we are allowed to create inside of ourselves, where we can actually listen deeply for what is the reality of my experience and who I am. And rather than judging that very harshly, right? I can begin to search for that image of God that is placed inside of myself. I can begin to have compassion on all my imperfections. Well, if I paid attention to myself, I really am not perfect. Join the club. Join the club. None of us are. None of us are. And part of having compassion is being able to admit that. But you see, then this begins to go out in community, right? All these practices, remember, are communal practices.

And one of the things that we know about our communities, right, is that this inauthentic relationship just goes out farther and farther and farther. How could I possibly, right, speak truth to other people or in other situations that are not safe where I'm immediately going to be criticized? Where my truth is going to be negated, where I'm going to be gaslit, where people are not going to listen to the realities that I have experienced in life. Right? The practice of compassion is the practice of creating these spaces where each being in the universe who knows what's right and wrong for themselves and for others can begin to speak that.

There's a brilliant black woman who is a journalist and a professor of journalism in Baltimore. Her name is Stacy Patton. And she is doing some really interesting work on how uh how colonization and Imperial societies uh create sociopathic people by exposing children to massive amounts of violence. Right?
