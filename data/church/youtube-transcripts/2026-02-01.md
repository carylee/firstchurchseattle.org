## Sermon: The Promise of Guidance
**Preacher:** Rev. Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** John 1:29-42

**Rev. Jackie Celin:**
Let us pray. Let the words of my mouth and the meditation of our hearts be acceptable to you, O Lord, our rock and redeemer. Amen.

Growing up in the 90s meant we didn't have access to digital maps at our fingertips. Long road trips often involved quibbles among partners or friends when the navigator, usually the person in the passenger seat up front, missed a critical turn or exit shown on the map. There was no instantaneous recalculating. Instead, you were left trying to get back on track as quickly as possible while traffic continued to move quickly around you. One of our homework assignments in our summer's driver's ed class prepared us well for this. As we had to take home a paper map and chart out an imaginary road trip, highlighting the roads and highways we would take, figuring out directions and calculating the overall mileage, time, amount of gas and approximate cost of the trip.

Sometimes in life we must respond quickly to situations that arise. However, having some direction in front of us helps us to know what to do and where to go next. In our current sermon series, we are reflecting on the promises of God that remain steady and constant, even in times of uncertainty and change. Last week, Pastor Elizabeth talked about the promise of ministry, reminding us that we are all called to follow Jesus and invited to respond to that call. Today we'll look at how God helps us take some of those next steps of following Jesus.

In our reading today from the Gospel of John, we see something quite beautiful and spectacular happening. The fullness of who Jesus is being revealed to John the Baptist. John was there baptizing people in the water so that he could testify about this moment that God chose to show the world. The light that had been sent to bring us into closer relationship with God. As we learned during Advent, John the Baptist and Jesus were related. However, in this passage, John says twice, "I myself did not know him." It wasn't until this moment that John the Baptist fully realized who Jesus was to the world.

Can you imagine what he must have felt then? As he saw and sensed and experienced the spirit of God descending on Jesus. Can you imagine him standing there, awestruck? I wonder, what did it look like? What did it feel like for John in that moment? The spirit of God was not a new concept in Jesus's time. It's referenced all the way back to the second verse of the Bible, in Genesis chapter 1 verse 2 that says, "The earth was a formless void and darkness covered the face of the deep, while a wind from God swept up over the face of the waters." The Hebrew word for both spirit and wind is Ruach. Therefore, it was Ruach, the spirit of God, that descended on the void of our cosmos and across the vast dark waters, creating life and bringing light.

The spirit of God was also present in leading many of the prophets to speak boldly towards change. Such as seen in Micah, chapter 3 verse 8, where the prophet speaks without hesitation about the injustices occurring in their community. Or in Ezekiel, when the spirit of God is given to the to the leaders of the communities when the spirit of God is given to lead the community towards a right relationship with God and each other. As verse verse 26 in chapter 36 says, "A new heart I will give you, and a new spirit I will put within you. And I will remove from your body the heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh." The spirit brought the truth of God to the prophets, and it gave them the ability to recognize and know the truth. The spirit then inspired them, providing them with the power and the courage to speak that bold truth.

It is it is the same Ruach, spirit of God, that we see descending on Jesus and remaining there. Jesus would go on to baptize with this Holy Spirit. And so let us take a deeper look into the Gospel of John to further understand this Holy Spirit. In his last discourse with his disciples before his death and resurrection, Jesus describes how this time with them will soon come to a close, saying, "Little children, I am with you only a little longer." He teaches them his foundational piece of guidance, saying, "I give you a new commandment. Just as I have loved you, you also should love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples."

Jesus assures them that they will not be left alone after he departs, saying, "I will ask the Father, and he will give you another advocate to be with you forever. This is the spirit of truth. The advocate, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, will teach you everything and remind you of all that I have said to you." Jesus is explaining how he will send the same spirit to be with us, so that his love, his teaching, his being, will continue to move with us, to lead us, to guide us. When Jesus baptizes us with the Spirit, Jesus provides a deep knowledge of God's will for us, an illumination of God's vision for our world, of a new kingdom built on love and justice for all creation.

And so even though Jesus is no longer physically present with us with us on Earth at this moment, we have not been left alone to try to figure it out ourselves on how to follow. With the spirit, Jesus Jesus gives us the strength and courage, the prophets received to see the truth and to boldly seek change. The spirit of God then is also God's promise of guidance. When we trust in God and choose to humbly follow Jesus, we accept that guidance. Jesus's teachings provide us general guidance on how to follow him, but we also recognize that Jesus is more than just our teacher. Such as we see through the many wonderfully rich and vivid names for Jesus in this passage.

Towards the end of the passage in the Gospel of John, two of John the Baptist's disciples learn who Jesus is and follow after him. First calling him Rabbi or teacher, they ask him where is he staying? Jesus invites them, "Come and see." Later they tell of finding the Messiah, the anointed one, when they meet up with one of their brothers, showing the depth of which they have come to know who Jesus is. John the Baptist himself refers to Jesus twice as the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world. The Lamb of God is only mentioned in a few other places in the Bible, in the letters of John, in the book of Revelation.

The Lamb of God may have several different understandings. And one is in recognizing the Lamb as small, gentle, and vulnerable. Reminding us that the power of God, of redemption, of righteousness, and of a new kingdom on Earth, comes not in mighty warriors, as perhaps would have been expected, but in the calm and meek, little lamb. The salvation and liberation that this gentle little Lamb provides is a freedom from the burden that sin holds on us, as it tries to separate us from God. However, this does not absolve us from the responsibility we have to pursue just action and right relationship with God, each other and with all creation. Jesus, as the Lamb of God, describes how Jesus leads us in that direction gently and with the care and compassion that we associate with the image of a lamb.

We can recognize the sins of our world, acknowledge and confess our part in it, and ask God for guidance on how to shift our thinking and actions to a mindset focused on the way of love, as Steve Garnaas-Holmes used in our call to worship today. As we seek to live into God's vision for our world. Today is February 1st and marks the start of Black History Month. A time when we celebrate Black history, culture and community. And we can continue to do that all year long, not just this month. My daughter Laurie helped prepare this slide for us today. This is also a time for us to recognize the work still to be done to create a more just world for all. And so, while some try to dismantle important diversity, equity, and inclusion programs, now more than ever, we must speak up and call out how racism is still active and present in our society. We must seek God's guidance to do better.

Discrimination and oppression didn't go away when the sin of slavery ended. It continued to leave behind decades of ongoing racism embedded into our systems and institutions that continue to cause harm. For instance, property promised to enslaved individuals was never fulfilled. Redlining practices denied home loans to Black communities. And segregation provided unequal access to housing, education, and other basic human rights. These discriminatory policies and practices of our past, along with many others still present today, have left lasting affect effects on people's lives in our present time. Generational trauma also continues to be experienced because of the violent ways people were torn from their families, communities, and homelands, and treated brutally by oppressors over and over again.

Even today, practices like racial profiling continue to perpetuate racism and were allowed to continue under a September 2025 Supreme Court procedural ruling, giving immigration agents the go ahead to question people's immigration status on the basis of the languages they speak, the communities they are present within, and even the color of their skin. This causes harm to the most vulnerable people and communities in our midst, creating immense fear that leaves people afraid to leave their homes for school, for work to sustain their families, or even to attend their places of worship. We must continue to speak boldly about these harms being done to God's beloved people and creation. And ask to experience a new spirit that transforms our societal hearts of stone.

Sometimes the change needed can feel daunting and overwhelming. And so we may ask ourselves, where do we start? How do we seek God's guidance? We can turn to the words of the psalmist who reminds us to open our ears, to wait patiently, to practice humility and to place our trust in God in order to allow ourselves to be led by God. And so I encourage you to search for God's guidance through the quietness of studying scripture. The stillness of contemplative prayer. The gentleness of dialogue with a spiritual conversation partner. The liveliness of small group theological discussions. And the spirit filled moments of worship. Pray daily, read scripture, seek spiritual direction, and participate in covenant groups, Bible studies, small group classes or other spiritual education events.

And let us remember to build off of the work of one another, recognizing we are all one in the body of Christ. We don't need to do this work alone. Look into resources available through the General Commission on Religion and Race. The General Commission on the Status and Role of Women. And the General Board of Church and Society. Such as these Faith and Facts cards that provide a theological basis for responding to social issues we see affecting our world today. We've got several of those available under the TV screen in the Narthex.

Today after worship, you'll learn more specifically about our denominational social principles, the guidelines we have created as a denomination, rooted in our faith in God and the life and teachings of Jesus. We use these to respond to social matters and concerns, considering how our faith can be put into action into practice and action when we engage in the world around us. Come check it out. I won't spoil it here for you. While in seminary, I interned remotely with the General Board of Church and Society. My focus area was on both domestic and global hunger and poverty.

I remember feeling both excited and overwhelmed. It was a huge area to think about. And I had no idea where to start. It was so helpful to work with a mentor that guided me forward, and I found so much value in the social principles, and in another, lesser known denominational resource, the Book of Resolutions. Looks a little bit like our Book of Discipline, but it is a different resource. These resolutions sometimes offer more specific suggestions on next steps to take for a whole range of current social issues and concerns. That have been brought forward and voted on by our General Conference, which meets every four years.

We don't always get everything right. And it's important for us to constantly examine what God is leading us to next. Let us always remember that we are seeking to live into God's vision for this world, for our Cosmos, and not our own. Responding to the call to follow Jesus is not a simple single action. It requires constant recalculating, to figure out how to respond next. Sometimes changing directions in our work and in our life. Sometimes working around constraints, limitations and obstacles.

However, be assured, God's promise of guidance will be with us always. Giving us clarity in times of unknowing, giving us wisdom in times of uncertainty, and strength and courage to speak boldly, while it gently leads us towards transformation as we seek to follow Christ. And so, let's take a moment now, to experience the spirit of God working in us as we renew our commitments to follow Jesus in the way of love, and in the way of life. Close your eyes. Breathe in with me.

Breathe in and feel the spirit of God. Feel Ruach within you. The life giving and light bringing divine presence of God. Holding you, changing you, leading you. Let go and release all the tension, all the fear, all the hate, all the grief, all the anxiety you have been holding. Remember your connections with one another, with all humanity and all creation. Remember your connection with God. Feel God's guiding hand, leading us towards God's love, God's comfort, hope, strength, joy, wisdom, and peace. Loving God, may we feel your presence always, moving with us, gently guiding us towards paths of just relationships with you, gracious creator and sustainer. And each other as we seek to follow closely in the ways of Jesus. Amen.
