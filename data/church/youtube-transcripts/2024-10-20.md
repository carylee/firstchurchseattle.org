### New Testament Reading 2: Corinthians 12:12-26

**Debra Loacker:**
The second reading is from First Corinthians, chapter 12.

Christ is just like the human body. A body is a unit and has many parts, and all the parts of the body are one body, even though there are many. We were all baptized by one Spirit into one body, whether Jew or Greek, or slave or free, and we were all given one Spirit to drink. Certainly the body isn't one part, but many. If the foot says, "I am not part of the body because I'm not a hand," does that mean it's not part of the body? If the ear says, "I'm not part of the body because I'm not an eye," does that mean it's not part of the body? If the whole body were an eye, what would happen to hearing? If the whole body were an ear, what would happen to the sense of smell? But as it is, God has placed each one of the parts in the body just like God wanted. If all were one and the same body part, what would happen to the body? But as it is, there are many parts, but one body. So the eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you." Or in turn, the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you." Instead, the parts of the body that people think are the weakest are the most necessary. The parts of the body that we think are less honorable are the ones we honor the most. The private parts of our body that aren't presentable are the ones that are given the most dignity. The parts of our body that are presentable don't need this. But God has put the body together, given greater honor to the part with less honor. So that there won't be division in the body, and so that the parts may have mutual concern for each other. If one part suffers, all the parts suffer with it. If one part gets the glory, all the parts celebrate with it. This is the word of life.

## Sermon: Help Wanted - No Exceptions
**Preacher:** Barbara Moreland
**Scripture:** Acts 2:42-47, 4:32-35; Corinthians 12:12-26

Hello, welcome. Come in. My name is Lydia, and I believe your name is Miriam. Is that right? I'm so glad you found us. Sorry about the mess. Things are a little chaotic today. Well, actually, who am I kidding? Chaotic would be a slow day, and we haven't had one of those in a long, long time. But, as you can see, our group of Jesus followers is growing, and it's growing like crazy. Folks are leaving their homes and former professions to come join us here. All kinds of people. We have poor folks who have nothing to bring with them, but they're so willing to work. We also have some rich businessmen who were used to having servants, and while they do offer money, they can't do much of anything else that's really helpful.

We also have a lot of women here who've come on their own, often with children. Some, even some teenagers, boys and girls, who heard Peter and the other disciples in Jerusalem and then decided to follow them here. And you know what? At the end of the day, every single one of these vastly different new people comes needing the same things: a meal, a bed, and answers to their questions. How are we managing all this? Good question. And the answer is, I don't know. Some days are certainly more challenging than others, and I know this sounds crazy, but what keeps us going is two things. First, we are all different, and our differences become our strengths. And the second is that we're all willing to do our part, whatever that is, because we believe in the community that we're building.

Enough of us are, well, no one's perfect. For example, look over there. There, see Peter? He's our preacher, our rock, our leader. But his one practical skill is that he can catch and cook fish. Beyond that, not so much. And the man is completely disorganized and compulsively challenging in his language. We don't let him talk to the authorities. James and John over here, they love to talk about Jesus's words and ideas, but it's really hard to get them to stop talking and help build a shelter that we need. And how helpful is it that those, that group of women over there, can talk about all their wonderful sewing and the things that they've created, and we've got 30 kids running around here with nobody to watch over them. What we actually need is, I think, simple. Well, it's not more talk about the best way or the right way to build our community. It's folks willing to take a chance on something important that's unlike anything they've ever done before. We want individuals who will stand up, take a stand, get to work, and follow through. Whether it's cooking and cleaning, or preaching and healing, or soothing a sick child, or teaching a teenager about real life. More than anything, if we are really going to build this community of Jesus followers, we need people willing to offer their skills, talents, ideas, and energy to make this whole unique experiment work.

I'm telling you, sometimes I feel like putting a sign on the door that says, "Help wanted. All talents needed. This means you."

I think that the, the great thing about the Bible stories is that we can apply them to real life. And I really can imagine that kind of conversation I just showed, shared with you, going on in any modern church, including First Church. What we need is not a whole bunch of people with one set of ideas, because that generally doesn't accomplish enough. And as I look around this room, I realize we've got a lot of different talents, energies, and knowledge, expertise. We have it all here. And the question is, what are we going to do with it?

I'm the worst, I, Elizabeth doesn't want me. Okay? I don't need to be over there in those chairs. Um, I can cook, I can wash dishes, but Dwanna's, no, I'm not going to even approach running a meal like Dwanna can do in her sleep. But if everybody is good at one thing, stuff doesn't happen. It's too complicated. And one of the things that Jesus's disciples teach us is that, yeah, Peter was a loudmouth fisherman who was impulsive, and and James and John were worried about their status, and Jesus loved them anyway. Jesus used them anyway. Whatever they accomplished, we're still the beneficiaries of.

I believe that everyone in this room has gifts and talents and abilities that you haven't yet realized are critical to the life of this community. I sit on the Church Council, and I hear people talk about the finances and the trustees talking about the building, and the and April wanting to pro, create a good program for the children and youth, and and somebody else is figuring out how to pay the bills, and and we've been doing this for a hundred and what, sixty-something years. And we're still here, because we've come together as a community and put all of our efforts together to accomplish God's will. It sounds so simple, and it can be and not so simple at the same time.

I like the scripture that Debra was reading, because it talks about not what happens when things go wrong, but what happens when things go right, when people come together, what what things can happen. It tells me, I don't have to worry about the things that I'm not, because I can focus on the things that I am. I am not the, um, I'm not the one you call when the boiler isn't working and you don't have any hot water. You call my husband for that. But, um, I've held a lot of different jobs in this congregation over the last twenty-something years. As have many of you. And we, we keep going, and we keep accomplishing God's mission, because of our diversity, because of our togetherness. And that's not a bad thing to be able to say. Now, could we try something different? Absolutely. Maybe the the best things are yet to come, because we haven't figured out what they are yet. Um, my husband will tell you it's it's not a good idea to ask me what I really want to do, because I'm going to tell him something that he wasn't prepared for. Maybe we need to just be prepared, be unprepared, and bring all of our energy together and change the world.

I'm one person. Our pastor is one person. We have one more pastor over here. Um, today is Laity Sunday. Well, with no disrespect to the two in the robes over here, or the even retired clergy we have in our congregation, every Sunday is Laity Sunday. How many of them are there, and how many of us? It's our, it's our body. I'm not the voice. I'm maybe the loudmouth, but I'm not the voice. What are you? How do you fit? What part have you not tried yet? Glory be to God. We have such incredible possibilities. The woman in the story ends because she sees the glint in the other woman's eye, and she recognizes that eagerness to be a part of something important, and together they get to work. Amen.

**Congregation:**
Amen.
