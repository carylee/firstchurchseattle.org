## Sermon: The Promise of Encounter
**Preacher:** Pastor Elizabeth Ingram Schindler
**Scripture:** Matthew 17:1-9
Let us pray. Oh God, may the words of my mouth and the meditations of all of our hearts be found pleasing in your sight. For you are our rock and our redeemer. Amen.

This week in our staff meeting, we read this same text from Matthew 17, and I invited everyone to think about any mountaintop experiences that we've had. Those times on either a literal or a metaphorical mountaintop when we've discovered something new or had an encounter with God that has changed our lives. And I imagine many of you have such experiences that you would call mountaintop experiences and I hope that you'll share them with each other and with me. Transfiguration Sunday is the perfect time to tell those stories.

And in our sharing this week, I remembered a time in the summer of 2004 when I was part of a study tour of the Middle East. Thirty seminary students from six schools across the Southeast went with 10 lay people from various churches in that same area to Syria, Jordan, Lebanon, the Sinai Peninsula of Egypt, Israel, Palestine, and Greece, led by a professor of Old Testament. And every day of that experience was incredible because of those travel companions, because of the things that I learned there about the Bible, and because of the places and people that I encountered.

But one of the real highlights was when we visited Mount Sinai. We arrived in the mid-afternoon at Saint Catherine's Monastery, which was built in the 6th century by the Byzantine Emperor Justinian, around what is traditionally understood to be the burning bush, out of which God called Moses in Exodus chapter 3, the beginning of the liberation of the people of Israel. There's still this kind of, it's not really a bush, but this plant that kind of comes out of a wall and they say, this is the bush, and you say, yeah, okay, I mean, I don't know why it wouldn't be, right? But Saint Catherine's is the oldest continuously inhabited monastery in the world. People have been living and worshipping there since the 6th century. And it's surrounded by three peaks, one of which is Mount Sinai.

So we came and visited Saint Catherine's and then we went to this little motel nearby for the night, where my room, which I shared with a roommate, was completely infested with mosquitoes. And some other people got bed bugs, so you know, you get what you get. Um, but I'm quite allergic to mosquitoes and I get these huge welts and I itch for weeks, and so I chose not to stay in that room and instead I stayed up all night in the lobby of the hotel. But it wasn't a long night because at about 2 or 3 in the morning, our group got back together, um, and gathered in the dark, where local guides, speaking a language we could not understand, um, emerged from utter darkness, and shined these little flashlights into our eyes, kind of one at a time, sizing us up, and then they would grab us by the arm and lead us off into the dark, um, to pair us up with the camel that we would be riding up the mountain. And the camels had done the trip so many times that they didn't need any light to see the path, which is good because there was none. It was very, very dark. So they took us about two-thirds or three-quarters of the way up the peak and then let us off for the steepest part of the climb, which we traversed with little tiny flashlights of our own.

And the goal was to arrive at the summit of Mount Sinai while it was still dark, so that we could experience the sunrise from there. The sunrise that woke up the whole desert around us with a rainbow of color. And it was totally worth it. It was worth the sleepless night, the only time in my life that I've stayed up all night long. It was worth the mosquitoes, it was worth that unsettling sorting on to the camels in the dark. The sunrise from the top of Mount Sinai was one of the most significant, magnificent things I've ever experienced.

And part of that was because the whole way up the mountain, I could not stop singing this old worship song from the band Third Day. It came out around 2001. And the song's called "Show Me Your Glory." And it's written about the story in Exodus 33, when Moses and God are talking. They're negotiating about the people of Israel. God's ready to give up on Israel, and Moses is convincing God to give them another chance. And as part of that interaction, Moses asks God to see God's face. And God says, "Nobody can see my face and live. But because we have this special relationship, I will hide you in the cleft of this rock. And I'll pass by, and you Moses can see my back, and that will have to be enough."

So all the way up that mountain, I was praying, God, show me your glory. And God did. I felt like I saw God's face in that sunrise. I felt God's presence there, like I imagine Moses felt it when he received the Ten Commandments in that very spot. I felt as close to God on that mountaintop as I ever had before and ever have since.

And I tell you about that trip to Mount Sinai for two reasons. One is because it is the epitome of a mountaintop experience for me. An encounter with God that changed my life and my faith in profound ways in just a few moments. But I also tell you that story because this is what the readers of Matthew's Gospel would have been thinking about when they heard this story of Jesus going up on a mountain, not Mount Sinai, but a similar peak, with his three closest disciples where they were met by Moses and Elijah. And Jesus turned dazzling white.

Now, just before this, of course, Jesus has been traveling, he's been teaching, we've heard a lot of his teaching already in the last several weeks. He's been performing miracles and healing whole crowds of people. And right before this story of the Transfiguration, Peter has declared that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of the Living God. This is the first time we've heard this language in the Gospel of Matthew. And right after that declaration from Peter, Jesus has told the disciples for the very first time that he will have to suffer and die, but that he'll rise on the third day. So this is the perfect time as we're preparing to enter the season of Lent that we're getting this story.

And six days after that declaration from Peter, and Peter talking about his upcoming suffering for the first time, Jesus goes up on this mountain with Peter, James, and John. And we don't really know what happened when they got there. Maybe they started praying and had a vision while they were praying. Maybe Jesus was teaching them something. Maybe they just arrived at the summit of the mountain and, bam, Jesus changed. We don't know. The story doesn't give us those details. It just says that Jesus was transfigured. That his face shown like the sun and his clothes became dazzling white.

And if that wasn't enough, suddenly, Jesus was joined by Moses and Elijah, who together represent the law and the prophets, showing thus that this is the same God who has been present throughout the whole history of Israel. It tells us that Jesus doesn't replace the God of the Old Testament. He just carries forward the work. It tells us that Christianity doesn't supersede Judaism. It emerges from it. And the story says that Jesus, Moses, and Elijah were talking, and I wish it told us what they were talking about. We don't get this discussion. I want to know whether they were all rolling their eyes together and saying, the disciples, can you even believe they still don't get it? Or if, uh, Jesus and Moses are asking Elijah, what was it like going up to heaven in a chariot? You're the only one who's done that. Or if, um, if Moses and Elijah were asking Jesus, you know, how are you feeling about Good Friday coming up? They had so much they could have been talking about, and we don't get to hear any of it. But it must have been really interesting or inspiring because Peter says, "Hey, let's just stay here." And I love the earnestness of Peter in this. Because we assume he doesn't really understand what's going on. That's often the case with Peter. He's very earnest, but he doesn't really get the point.

But maybe he, he did understand that Jesus is about to go into a time of suffering and death, and he's like, this shining face scenario with Moses and Elijah sounds really good compared to that. So he says, "I'll build three shelters. Three booths, three tents." We're not sure really what he intended or where he would get the materials. But he said, "I'll create three dwellings, one for Jesus, and one for Moses, and one for Elijah, and we can all just stay here."

But before he has even finished making this offering, he gets interrupted. By this bright cloud that comes and overshadows the whole mountain. And a voice from the cloud, who we assume is God speaking, probably in that big Charlton Heston voice, says out of the cloud, "This is my son, the beloved, in whom I am well pleased." These are the same words that Jesus heard at his baptism. But at that time, we didn't know if anybody else heard them. We don't know in the baptism story if that's a community announcement or just a message from the Father to Jesus. And here, it is a community announcement. Everyone gets to hear it. And then there's an added bonus because at the end, God says, "Listen to him." "This is my son, listen to him. Open your ears, try to understand what's going on and why."

And with the cloud and the voice and the bright light, and knowing what they already know about God's history of presence on mountaintops, like how from our reading from Exodus today says that the glory of the Lord was like a devouring fire, it's no wonder that the disciples fell to the ground in fear. I think they may have understood at this point that Jesus wasn't just another prophet, that he wasn't just a great teacher, that he was the Son of God.

But what they didn't understand yet, was that in the person of Jesus, God had shed God's power, God's magnificence to get close to us. That the God of the universe who had been a cloud like devouring fire had put on a body like ours, humbling himself so he could be with us. They didn't know yet that God had set aside God's glory to show us love and gentleness, even to offer a reassuring touch. Maybe that's even the most profound part of this mountaintop experience, of this encounter with God. That we serve a God who not only created all that is, seen and unseen, with just a word, but also one who took on flesh, so that God could put a reassuring hand on our shoulder when we're afraid. So that God could speak to us in words we understand and tell us to get up. Or here more accurately, tell us to arise, which means in this context not only stand up, but also wake up and even rise from the sleep of death. Because this is a God who will not only meet us on the mountaintop in shining light and booming voice and scary cloud, but also one who will heal the diseased and cast out demons with just a touch. And who will reach out that same hand to reassure us and to invite us not only into discipleship but into resurrection.

So we may not fully understand what happened on that mountain. And neither did the disciples. That's part of why Jesus told them not to tell anybody about it for a while. And that's true most of the time with our mountaintop experiences, with our encounters with God, we often don't actually understand what happened until way down the road. And when you have your own mountaintop experiences, they might look different than this one for Peter, James, and John. They might look different from the experience I had on Mount Sinai. Maybe you'll hear the voice of God audibly, but maybe not. Maybe there'll be a bright light for you, and maybe not. Maybe you'll feel a gentle touch. Maybe you'll get interrupted by God when you're telling God your very good plans. Maybe God will invite you to listen. To arise. I don't know what it'll be like for you. But one thing is for sure, and that's that ours is a God who shows up. Who doesn't require any kind of intermediary or translator. Who could claim all the majesty and chooses not to so that we can know God up close. So that we can have encounters of our own, wherever we are. And thanks be to God. Amen.
