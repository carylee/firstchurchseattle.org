## Sermon: Preparing a Humble Heart
**Preacher:** Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** Colossians 3:12-17; Luke 2:41-52

**Jackie Celin:**
Let us pray.

May the words of my mouth and the meditations of all of our hearts be acceptable not to each other, but to you, oh God, our rock and our redeemer. Amen.

One of our favorite movies to watch as a family leading up to Christmas is Home Alone. Many of you know it, I'm sure. Can you picture the mom's reaction on the airplane headed to Paris, as she finally remembers what she has left behind? Her eyes get wide as she leans forward and shouts, "Kevin!" realizing her eight-year-old son is home alone in Chicago.

I can't help but wonder if Mary and Joseph had a similar reaction when they realized their 12-year-old son Jesus was not with them, after already a day into their long journey back home. Did Mary have a Kevin moment? We next see her and Joseph frantically looking around amongst the rest of the caravan to see if anyone else had seen Jesus.

It was common in these biblical times for families to journey to Jerusalem from their homes far away. Jerusalem was the site of the Holy Temple, God's house, and therefore the site of where the holiest of ceremonies and rituals took place. As devout followers of their faith, Mary and Joseph are seen making this journey to Jerusalem on these special occasions. For instance, after Jesus was born, Mary and Joseph traveled from neighboring Bethlehem to the temple in Jerusalem, where Jesus was presented to God and a sacrifice of two turtle doves or young pigeons was offered. Afterwards, the holy family travels home to Nazareth, within Galilee, about 60 miles or so north of Jerusalem. It would have taken them at least 30 hours by foot.

With such a long journey, it's not too surprising that Mary and Joseph were unaware of Jesus's absence among them. Woman and children usually started the journey earlier, since their pace was often slower. The woman, children, and men likely wouldn't have met up until the evening when the caravan camped. Mary probably thought Jesus was with Joseph. Joseph thought Jesus was with Mary. They didn't realize he was missing until they met up with each other later that evening.

Jesus, too, seemed unaware that he had been left behind. In fact, he seems surprised that his parents had spent three days looking for him throughout Jerusalem. He felt it was, he felt that he was in the most obvious place, God's house. Jesus says, "Why were you searching for me? Did you not know that I must be in my Father's house?" These are the first words spoken by Jesus in the Gospel of Luke. In fact, this scripture reading is the rare occasion where we get to see Jesus as a child. The age of 12 is significant, as it meant Jesus was about the age when his instruction in the temple would begin.

Last week, we celebrated with joy at the birth of Emmanuel, God with us, as the word of God became flesh through the Incarnation of Jesus here on Earth. So then, what was Jesus doing in the temple, listening to the teachers and the rabbis, asking them questions? Didn't he understand the word of God more than they did? Jesus exhibits a quiet and devout reverence in this text. Perhaps Jesus is showing us what it is like to be in awe and wonder of hearing the word of God. But I also see Jesus's humbleness in this text. He doesn't assume that he already knows everything there is to learn about the word of God. As Jesus grew as a child, we learn he was filled with wisdom and the favor of God. The text also tells us that he increased in wisdom from this time in the temple. This devout study with the teachers was important preparation work for his public ministry that would begin at the age of 30.

Perhaps Jesus was also wanting to learn more about the rituals, laws, and experiences and expectations of his faith tradition, his context. What was important to his faith community in their present time and place? And how the rabbis taught the faithful followers of their traditions.

As we look ahead to the new year and the challenges in front of us, within our society and as a church, we need to first prepare our hearts for how we will respond in service to the world. Jesus demonstrates for us how to engage in the act of preparation, showing us the characteristics expected of us as Christians that are also reiterated for us in the text today from Paul's letter to the Colossians. Paul describes these Christian qualities: compassion, kindness, humility, meekness, and patience. When we act through God's love, we allow these Christian qualities to guide our hearts in a perfect harmony that leads to the peace of Christ being expressed in all that we do and all that we say.

But to have true compassion, humility, and patience, we must acknowledge that we don't always know everything. We must continuously examine our own privileges and recognize the ways they continue to hold power over others and over creation. We must consider what it would truly mean to give up some of the control that we try to hold on to so tightly.

Later in the service, we will participate in the historical tradition of the Wesley Covenant Renewal Service. This service first began in August of 1755, when John Wesley, the founder of Methodism, adapted a Puritan ritual. Since then, Methodists across the globe have developed a tradition of holding the service on or around the beginning of each new year. It is a time when we each take the opportunity to recommit ourselves to God once again, in the company of each other, and as we look ahead to our plans and visions for the new year.

When we say the words of the Covenant Renewal later in the service, you will hear echoes of these themes of compassion, humility, and patience, as we acknowledge the many different tasks we are called to do in service to Christ. We must not just do the tasks that we want to do, but instead listen for the tasks that Christ asks us to do. It may mean putting aside one task that we really enjoy, in order to focus on something less desirable, so that someone else may have the opportunity to generate new ideas on a task that's new to them. Or it may mean setting aside our own pride to create space to listen to another's perspective, especially if they and their community have historically been silenced and pushed to the margins.

I keep a copy of the Wesley Covenant Prayer taped to the inside cover of my sketchbook. Whenever I am frustrated that something hasn't gone as I've planned or when I feel overlooked for a task or a role, I remember to pull out my sketchbook and read through the covenant I have made with God. Reminding myself, it isn't about me or what I want. I am humbled and my personal pride is laid to rest as I again recommit to following Christ.

January 1st also holds another significance for my family, as it is also Haitian Independence Day. It is the day that we remember the strength, courage, resiliency, innovation, and determination of the Haitian people, who fought against Napoleon's army for 13 years, and freed themselves in 1804 from the sinful, oppressive bonds and chains of slavery. Something most of the world never expected. In his book, Underdog Revolution, Haitian entrepreneur, blogger and author Pierre Stanley Baptiste, defines underdogs as an individual or group with disadvantages, few resources or unfavorable circumstances, compared to another group or individual. He refers to the Haitian Revolution as a prime example of an underdog revolution. He writes this book for both people who experience disadvantages and for people that want to be allies. He explains how important it is for allies to work alongside disadvantage communities, inviting participation into the problem-solving work in ways that allows disadvantage communities to utilize their own creativity, innovation, and resources.

For those of us that come from places of privilege and seek to be allies for the 3.5 billion people in the world living in poverty and extreme poverty, he shares these important words: In trying to accomplish our boldest dreams to move humanity forward, we often forget humanity is made of humans. Humans with stories, pain, failure, desires, weaknesses. Many people and organizations are trying their best to help, and we are grateful to have them as an ally. If you are on such a quest, I truly hope you will put humanity first.

As a faith community, one way we can do that is by preparing a humble heart and listening to the needs and tasks expressed by the people in the organizations that we've built relationships with that are centered on our priority areas as a faith community. And the Christmastide season is a perfect time to refocus ourselves on that work.

Christmastide, or the 12 days of Christmas, is the short season between Christmas and Epiphany. Theologian Howard Thurman writes, as sung by the choir last week, "When the song of the angels is stilled, when the star in the sky is gone, when the kings and princes are home, when the shepherds are back with their flock, the work of Christmas begins." To find the lost, to heal the broken, to feed the hungry, to release the prisoner, to rebuild the nations, to bring peace among others, to make music in the heart.

For several years, we have prepared a work of Christmas document in coordination with the 12 days of Christmas, remembering that Christ Christ taught us to care for the poor, free the oppressed, to love God and to love neighbor. Each day provides an opportunity to study and reflect on the work of justice and compassion of organizations we work with, as we consider a call to further action. Those are available for you in the Narthex.

One of the organizations listed, WAZEN, was recently new to me, and one we learned about through our relationship with the Church Council of Greater Seattle. WAZEN stands for the Washington Immigrant Solidarity Network. It is the largest immigrant-led coalition in the state of Washington, a grassroots organization led by the people most affected by our nation's immigration policies. They run the Deportation Defense Hotline, the only statewide hotline exclusively dedicated to serving immigrants and refugees, particularly undocumented immigrants in the state of Washington. It connects immigrants and refugees with key resources, provides no your rights information, and allows for reporting of suspected immigration enforcement activity. These reports are then used to activate the Rapid Response Network, where immigrants and allies are sent out to record what is taking place, and question ICE and CBP officials trying to detain individuals and families. This innovative extensive network has been developed by the people most affected, the immigrants and refugees themselves. It provides the support that they have determined they need. We are invited into the work as allies, as most of us will truly never know the fear of what it feels like to be live in fear of a knock on our doors.

So then, what would it mean for us to let go of the things that we think we know, and truly listen to the people most affected by the hurt and injustices of our world? In what ways can we practice sitting like the child, Jesus? Listening deeply for the ways we hear God's presence, God's glory, God's words. How can we work on finding our own humility to prepare a humble heart, in order to express Christ's peace through compassion for one another and for the world? Our humility is found in recognizing that God is bigger than we can fully comprehend. Our humility is found in leaving space when it is our time to listen. Our humility is found in acknowledging the ways we have caused harm, whether intentional or not. Our humility is found in letting go of the control we are used to having and wanting. Our humility is found in listening to communities tell us how our resources will best help them, rather than us telling communities what we can do. Our humility is found in inviting communities most affected by systems of oppression and injustice to participate fully in the creative problem-solving process. Our humility is found in amplifying the voices of the most vulnerable, the most marginalized. Our humility is found in recognizing how we see God in the most unexpected places, through the people we meet and the experiences shared with us. As we allow ourselves to be moved by what the people and experiences teach us.

May we always remember to be like that child of Jesus, full of awe and wonder, growing in wisdom, and preparing our humble hearts for a lifetime of ministry to share God's love, peace, and hope to a world in need. Glory be to God. Amen.
