## Sermon: Wilderness: A Place of Healing
[20:56]
**Preacher:** Pastor Kathy
**Scripture:** John 9: 1-41

**Pastor Kathy:**
Thank you, Craig and Sean for that wonderful reading of the ninth chapter of John's Gospel. I may have to change my sermon now.

I'm going to ask Laura to put up a slide before we get into the Gospel story this morning. John's Gospel is not easy to read because it's repetitive. And it's repetitive on purpose. Most of us read the Bible like we read a newspaper, expecting it to be a direct narrative. But John's Gospel was written in a particular style that was familiar to the people of John's time. John used chiasms to make his points throughout his Gospel. A chiasm is a literary device that directs the reader or hearer to the main point of the story by arranging the words or ideas to create a concentric pattern that moves toward the point and then moves away from the point. And so, um, I'm looking at a very far far picture of this, uh, but you see the blue arrow. This is a diagram of the full chapter of, uh, John's Gospel, the ninth chapter. And, um, there's an A and then an A star at the bottom. Next, below the A, there's a B and, um, and a B star is just above the A below that. And where it ends up is D, which is, um, the parents refusal or what is it say? I'm sorry, I can't see. Parents fearing eviction from the temple.

So, there are a number of ways that I have found this this chiasm described for this chapter of John. It's always the same verses broken down, same way that the verses are broken down. But with different headings just depending on the interpreter's focus. In each of the chiasms, the parents' fear of being expelled from the temple is the center of the chiasm. It is the, uh, focus. And that ex being expelled from the community is the point of the arrow. We hardly ever look at that part.

So, with that in mind, let's take a look at the story, remembering that the parents are at the center of this telling. It begins with the disciples asking Jesus, "Who sinned caused the man's blindness, his or his parents?" Jesus replies, "Neither this man nor his parents sinned. He was born blind so that God's work might be revealed in him."

While Jesus states unequivocally that sin is not the cause of blindness, the second half of his response is less clear. It can be just as confusing or even harmful to infer that God might inflict disability on a person in order for that person to become an object blessed for God's glory. I think it's not helpful at all to use a real person's disability to talk about spiritual issues. But that happens all the time in the scriptures. Ancient people equated physical disability with sin and they said so, as evidenced by the disciples' question, "Who sinned? This man or his parents?" Sometimes modern people make the same assumption.

Jesus lays that idea to rest. The story itself has a lot to say about the way that people with disabilities are seen or not seen, and treated or ignored or worse hidden. We'll do better with the original intent of this story if we understand that in the Gospel of John, the miracles that Jesus works are signs that point to Jesus as God's very presence revealed to the world, so that we may know that we are God's beloved children.

This sign initiates a conversation that gets at the heart of what Jesus wants to reveal. That the man who was blind from birth is God's beloved child. So the conversation before and after begs the question of whether this can be true, because Jesus has disrupted assumptions. This story is about understanding, about getting it and not getting it. And the spiritual metaphor is light and dark. The conversation reveals some of the reasons that people choose not to understand or to stay in the dark. The temple leaders are purveyors of religious truth, so a competing source of truth is threatening. The man's parents are so intimidated by power and want so badly to belong to the community that they're willing to deny what they know to be true. And one may wonder, why their blind son has to resort to begging in order to live? Is it because they believe he is blind because of sin?

The author of John hopes that we will be like the man who was born blind. Jesus makes mud to put on his eyes, which could make us remember that Adam was created out of the dust of the ground in the second creation story in Genesis. The man was sent to wash in the pool of Siloam, which means sent. His new life begins with a kind of baptism. The early church already claimed that baptism signified being cleansed, being named as God's beloved, being sealed with the Holy Spirit and marked as Christ's own forever, and being sent into mission.

The man didn't have to know anything other than the truth of his own story. His experience of God's healing presence in the one who changed his life. He went from telling about a man called Jesus, to saying that he must be a prophet, to affirming that the sign proves that Jesus must be sent from God, and finally he addressed Jesus as Lord and worshiped him. This man, and every other person who suddenly experiences the radical love of God through Jesus, is a new being. It's the difference between night and day. Everything is changed. Everything is different.

But in John's Gospel, the Pharisees and even the man's parents continue to wander in the wilderness. The Pharisees because they cannot accept what they cannot imagine, in spite of the evidence in front of them. And the parents because they fear no longer being part of the community. The man's parents are really interesting, because they are afraid to let go of the orthodoxy, the right teaching of the of the synagogue, that sin is at the heart of disability. That they have distanced themselves from their own son, in spite of having experienced him as their son.

Jesus disrupts this thinking. His healing ministry disrupts the worship. And each person in the story finds themselves in some kind of wilderness, unsure of how to make sense of what they are experiencing. What is true? What is not true? How can they perceive differently?

Like each of the persons in this story, we all have experienced being in the wilderness in one way or another. When our lives are disrupted in some way. It may be when we're finding our way through a difficult diagnosis for ourselves or someone we love. It may be a loss of a relationship or a job, or an unexpected move, or being overwhelmed by too many responsibilities, or feeling left out or not belonging. Whether we feel alone, afraid, overwhelmed, unmoored, or lost in any way, Jesus offers us unconditional love and healing.

I want to be clear here. We often take the word healing to mean cure. And sometimes that happens. But it also has a much broader, richer meaning. It means to be made whole. Healing is a visible sign of God's grace, not just for the individual, but for the whole community. Through that grace, God has the power to restore individuals to the community, so that both may may become whole.

The Apostle John's community calls to us down through the centuries to get it. To pay attention. To be open to the work of the Holy Spirit. Like the man in John's Gospel, we can be witnesses to our own stories of the unexpected miracles of God's love. Our job as Christians is to share what we've experienced of God's love and to love others as freely and generously as we have been loved.

I've come to understand that in many cases, healing or wholeness comes slowly by the grace of God. And I've learned to hold on to the promises of stories that point to that change. I've been offering prayer with anointing every Sunday during communion for most of my ministry. My favorite story concerns Greg. A man who arrived in church one morning, holding his infant grandson. He lived across the street from the church in a rental house that changed residents frequently. Greg looked as if his life had been hard. During communion, he knelt for healing prayer. I made the sign of the cross on his forehead with oil and prayed close to his ear. "Gracious and loving God, hold your son Greg in your tender heart. Pour out your Holy Spirit upon him. Give him strength in his body and courage in his spirit. That he may be whole in every way." When he stood up, he was weeping and he did not move.

As communion concluded, we continued he continued to stand transfixed, weeping. At some point during the sending hymn, Greg went back to his seat. One congregant later asked, "What was the deal with the man with the baby?" Do you hear an echo of the questions the Pharisees asked in John's Gospel? The unbelief in what they witnessed, or the fear of the parents in being associated with their son because they saw him as a sinner? Greg brought the wilderness into the church's worship. But another person in the church asked, "Did you see him? Did you see Christ among us?" Two ways of interpreting what we all witnessed. One way being suspect of what was not expected or as judgment. The other way in openness to God doing a new thing.

A few months later, Greg's wife left him. He lost his home and began drinking again. One evening he fell and hit his forehead on the sidewalk. Unable to get up, he caught the attention of a passing motorist who was calling the police when a member of the church drove by. Recognizing Greg, the church member told the other motorist, he'd take care of Greg. And calling him by name, he gently took Greg to a friend's house. On several occasions, another congregant provided a motel for Greg until he could find work. Healing sometimes takes time.

On that first morning in church, Greg was overcome by a prayer that named him as God's beloved. It made Greg visible within the community that learned to recognize and care for him as a beloved child of God. Over time, Greg found his way out of the wilderness through the ministry of Christ's body, the church. The Prophet Ezekiel tells us that the source of the healing of the world is the living water that flows from the altar of God, from the sanctuary, reviving the desert and making fresh all the waters of the world. It is the life-giving river that waters the trees along its banks, producing fruit for food and leaves for healing the nations. The ministry of healing is given to the body of Christ, the baptized people of God, who are water washed and spirit born, sealed by the Holy Spirit and marked as Christ's own forever, to be light for the world. People of God, we have healing in our hands. Thanks be to God. Amen.
