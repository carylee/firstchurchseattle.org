### Stewardship Moment
**Bo Babarinsa:**
I was asked today to give a stewardship reflection. Um, so good morning. Um, in June, during my baptism, I shared what brought me to First United Methodist Church. Today, I share why I stay.

For my first Sunday, I sat on the same side, in the same pew, and at the same time. Form and order are comforting to me. And if there's one thing Methodists love, it's order. So when asked to speak about transforming the world, I'll admit on the surface, I might not seem like a steward of change. But I've learned that change can be a stabilizing force, the quiet kind that builds and binds community. I'll also admit, I'm still reckoning with what's ahead, especially with the pastoral changes. I've asked myself if this is still a place I want to be. And yet, even in that uncertainty, two moments reminded me why this church matters and what belonging here means.

The first came from a simple question. John reached out and asked, "Bo, how do we get young people in the church?" I make a living solving hard problems, but with computer systems, not people. Yet the fact that someone from the congregation thought to ask me, invite me into the work of shaping the church's future, showed a kind of trust that transforms quietly, but deeply. People go where they're invited, but they stay where they're included. That invitation reminded me that I belong here and the church's future is built from small acts of trust and inclusion.

The second moment came in an unexpected phone call. A member reached out to me in crisis. We share a bond, but not one that I thought would ever relate to such an intense exchange. The details are private, but in that moment I answered. Most of us come here seeking guidance, consumers of spiritual care, not providers of it. But that day, I learned that the spirit of the church isn't something that comes here to receive. It's something we already carry. After that call, I realized that everything I came here seeking comfort, faith, and connection, I was able to offer back. They said, "Bo, I had no one else to call." That was deeply meaningful to me because that's why I came to First Church. I had no one else to call.

Even now, in this age of isolation and spiritual dissidents, one call, one small act of connection can transform a life. Transformation doesn't always look grand. Sometimes it's the courage to answer, to make someone feel seen, or simply share breakfast. I'm happy to continue my stewardship here. It's the small touches, the nods of acknowledgement, the familiar faces, and the quiet moments that carry, that care, that carry me through each Sunday. And in the same pew, and at the same time, I'm reminded that change doesn't always mean new. It can mean, it can be transformative what we already have. Thank you.
