**Sermon**
**What We Need Is Good News**
(Pastor Elizabeth)
*Pastor Elizabeth speaks about the nature of news in society, often focusing on scandal or failure, making her opt out of watching TV news. She acknowledges the importance of staying informed through balanced sources, though even then, news often tries to evoke anger or fear. She highlights that the word "Gospel" literally means "good news" or "good tidings" from the Old English "Godspell" and Greek "euangelion." The sermon explores how the Gospel, the stories of Jesus, represents a brand new beginning and the best news ever written, focusing on sharing "good news" rather than "recruitment." Pastor Elizabeth discusses how the Gospel may not always feel like good news to everyone, relating it to the expectations of Jesus's contemporaries who hoped for a political revolution, but instead received teachings of love and healing. She suggests that in our era of "fake news," we often approach scripture looking for verifiable facts or contradictions, when the Gospel's true intent is to present a person: Jesus Christ. She emphasizes that encountering Jesus is meeting "good news incarnate." She challenges listeners to approach scripture with receptivity rather than critique, to meet Jesus in its pages and be transformed. Pastor Elizabeth then proposes a "rubric" from Isaiah's text (Luke 4:18-19) to evaluate if something is truly "good news": Is it good news to the poor? Does it bring freedom to the captive? Does it illuminate truth? Does it set right what is wrong? Is it good news for everyone? She concludes by stating that while sweet animal videos might lift spirits, true good news is found in the person and work of Jesus, who is available, waiting, and hoping for an encounter with each of us, transforming us to share that good news with the world.*

**Passing of the Peace**
(Pastor Elizabeth)
*Pastor Elizabeth explains the meaning and liturgical placement of the Passing of the Peace, emphasizing it as an offering of Christ's peace to one another, reflecting the words of the risen Christ. She clarifies that its placement in the communion liturgy is traditional, aligning with Jesus's teaching about reconciliation before offering gifts at the altar. While acknowledging the challenge of full reconciliation within a short time during worship, she highlights its purpose as a spiritual practice that trains hearts for reconciliation in daily life. She encourages congregants to be at peace with others before partaking in communion and to continue this work throughout the week.*
