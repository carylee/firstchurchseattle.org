## Sermon: Wilderness: A Place of Provision
**Preacher:** Pastor Elizabeth
**Scripture:** Exodus 17:1-7; John 4:5-26, 39-42
[11:00]
**Pastor Elizabeth:**
Thank you, Ben, for that lengthy reading, even though we cut a big part of it out. It's still a very long story and I hope some of you read it at home this week so you got even the parts that we cut.

But it's really odd that Jesus chose to go through Samaria on this journey. It would have been quicker, easier, more socially acceptable, and probably even safer if he had taken the coastal highway to the west or gone down the Jordan Rift Valley on the east. But something made Jesus charge right through this mountainous region, a wilderness, if you will. And the author of John's Gospel says that Jesus has to go this way, but doesn't explain why. And certainly doesn't say what made Jesus stop at Jacob's well at this particular time.

But that's what Jesus does. He sits down at the well right at noon, tired and thirsty, with the sun high overhead, and he's all alone. He hasn't brought anything to eat, so he has sent the disciples into the city to buy some food. He also didn't carry a water bottle. Jesus is clearly not a Boy Scout. He is not prepared. So he has nothing to quench his thirst.

But a Samaritan woman comes to the well to draw water. And Jesus, almost certainly relieved to see any other person there, asks her for a drink. And this is already significant because in those times, Jews and Samaritans did not usually speak to one another, and they certainly did not share water containers. They had been rivals for centuries, with the Jews thinking that the Samaritans had worshipped false gods ever since their return from Assyrian captivity.

Thus, Jews believed that you should worship God in the temple in Jerusalem, but the Samaritans built their own temple on Mount Gerizim, which the Jews had destroyed more than a century before this woman came to the well in our story in John's Gospel. So, for centuries, there had been very little friendly interaction between these cultural groups. And then if you add to that, the fact that this is a woman, a woman with no name, someone with no standing in society. And we see that Jesus, once again, is breaking all the rules.

And the Samaritan woman knows that this interaction is out of bounds too, but she doesn't yet know who Jesus is. She does know that at this time, Jewish men did not usually talk to women who were not their relations. And in fact, they would have tried not to even be alone with a woman who was not their wife, because it could lead to gossip or scandal or temptation. So for Jesus to approach this woman and ask her for a drink of water from her cup was certainly not what the Samaritan woman expected to encounter when she went to the well that day.

But Jesus must have sensed something in her. He didn't just see a Samaritan. He didn't just see a woman. He didn't just see a cup from which he could drink. Maybe Jesus saw a possibility, an openness, a potential connection. He immediately engaged the woman in a deeper conversation, bringing up the concept of living water, which, interpreted literally, means a spring or a river, moving water, as opposed to still, stagnant water.

Jesus told the woman that whoever drinks from the water he gives will never be thirsty again, that it will become in them a spring of water that bubbles up to eternal life.

And I'm afraid that this image might lose a little bit of power for those of us who live in the Pacific Northwest, where water is in abundant supply even in drier seasons. You know that for much of the year our lawns are soggy. Our footsteps squish and squelch, should we venture off the pavement. We never leave the house without a hood, no matter the forecast. Slugs abound here. Moss grows on everything. Even if it's not technically raining outside, our region is wet. And then, when the sun finally comes out in the summer and our lawns dry up, we all congregate at the water. At rivers and lakes, along the bay or the ocean, hiking to waterfalls or springs, marveling at the snow and ice that stay on the mountains year round. We are water people.

But if we lived in the desert, like in modern-day Israel-Palestine, a water well would be a welcome destination. And a spring of living water, moving water, more like an oasis. It would be a sign of life, a place that quenches thirst not only for the moment, but well into the future. This living water, or even a well, would suggest not only refreshment but nourishment, vitality, community, even life itself.

So we probably can't fully understand the power of the living water in a place like this, not as viscerally as the Samaritan woman would have. And especially since I I'm venture to guess that none of us have ever had to draw our water from a well and then carry it in jars or buckets to our homes. Most of us just turn the tap, and out it flows, clean and clear.

But, maybe we can relate to the idea of being parched. Maybe that makes sense to us. Even in our soggy land, we have been dry. Mentally, emotionally, spiritually, relationally, creatively. So perhaps we can understand, at least on a metaphorical level, what it is like to be in the wilderness. To keep going back to the well again and again, the still water that provides for us, but that doesn't last. We might know what it's like to keep getting parched again and again, to drink what doesn't satisfy. When the things that we're pouring into our lives don't actually make us thrive.

When we're in those spaces, in those times when life feels repetitive and unfulfilling, when it just feels too hard. We too might long for living water. I know there've been multiple times in my own life when I've felt that way, and some of them I can remember in pretty stark detail. Times when life has been busy but not full, broad but not deep, with relationships that did not encourage and faith only where I could fit it in around the edges.

There've been times when I've been exhausted, unmoored, overstretched and unfulfilled, working hard without results, spinning my wheels. Can you relate? I've tried to feed myself with things that would not nourish, wasted my energy on projects that did not last, gone through the motions of life without actually living. And when I've stopped to notice what's going on, I've realized I've been drinking as frantically as from a fire hose, but my thirst could not be quenched, because I was going to the wrong source. I was trying to create living water for myself, and it didn't satisfy.

In those times, it was only when I returned to the well of life, gave myself over to the one who created all things, who is the living water, that I began to really live again. Not that everything somehow suddenly became easier, or all my struggles disappeared, but life certainly had more meaning and felt worth living.

Now, over the years, um, folks have added lots of details uh, to the story of the Samaritan woman that aren't actually in the Bible. So, just want to touch on those. Um, people like to refer to her as a woman with low morals uh, or someone who has shared herself with men in an unscrupulous way, because Jesus says that she's had five husbands and the one that she lives with now is not her husband. And for some reason, we really want to discredit this woman. So, we emphasize her questionable morals. And maybe it makes us feel better to think that we follow a Jesus who would condescend to interact with such a woman even if we usually wouldn't. But I have to think that maybe this critique is just patriarchy, plain and simple.

Because the text does not say that the Samaritan woman is a woman of the night. And Jesus doesn't seem to have any issues with her morality or her behavior. There are plenty of individuals in the Gospels to whom Jesus announces forgiveness and set gives instruction to live a different way. But this woman is not one of them. So perhaps she's just a woman who like so many other women of her time and ours has been tossed around by people more powerful than her. Maybe she's a woman who's had really bad luck. Or a woman who is oppressed and used in a misogynistic culture.

Or perhaps, the language of husbands is all symbolic. Stick with me on this. Jesus has already been called the bridegroom in John's Gospel. He's just performed a couple of chapters ago, his first miracle, John calls him signs, at a wedding in the city of Cana. And now he's meeting the Samaritan woman at a well where many Old Testament couples got engaged. So, marriage is on the mind. But it doesn't seem like Jesus is looking for a spouse himself. So maybe what he's talking about here, just maybe, throwing it out as an idea, is not literal literal marriage, like legal, uh physical, emotional, or sexual relationship. But maybe Jesus is talking about to whom we are giving our hearts.

Maybe when he talks to the Samaritan woman about marriage, he's referring to the false gods that the Samaritans have worshipped. And the lack of true biblical covenant under which they are living. Maybe, in this conversation, he's helping her to see not that she needs to change her lifestyle, but that there's a better way to live. Not with a different man, but with a different way to worship. A different way to orient her life. And that would make sense when the woman then asks Jesus about different worship practices of Samaritans and Jews. Otherwise, it's kind of hard to make that jump from "you've had five husbands" to "let's talk about worship."

So the Samaritan woman didn't ask Jesus about her lifestyle or with whom she should live, but she did ask Jesus about proper worship. And something in that question, in that conversation, made Jesus reveal himself to her, using the same language that God used to name Godself to Moses in Exodus chapter 3, when God calls Godself, "I Am." Same words here. And suddenly it becomes clear to the woman that Jesus is not just a prophet, but is God in the flesh. God come to dwell among us. God come to walk our streets and sit by our wells and drink our water and have these conversations with us.

Now, the woman understood a lot better than the disciples did. And maybe that's because they were so caught up in the way they'd always done things. But the woman understood enough to leave her water there by the well and run off to tell her friends. "Come and see," she told them. "Come and see what I've seen. It will change you." No longer was the big question about where the people should worship, Jerusalem or Mount Gerizim, but whom they should worship when the Messiah was there in their midst. They learned so quickly through that encounter with Jesus that this is the one to whom they could give their lives. The one who is the living water that would cause in them and in us a spring of water to bubble up to eternal life.

Now, like with Nicodemus and eternal life that we talked about last week, the living water isn't achieved by doing religion in a particular way, or in a particular place. Instead, it's received by entering into the presence of the one who is life abundant. We find living water not because we've checked all the boxes, but because we've opened ourselves to an encounter with the God who cannot wait to be in conversation with us. And with many others whom we might have written off.

When we're in the wilderness, when we are parched, we might be especially tempted to try to produce living water ourselves. But this story of the Samaritan woman shows us that we're not the creators of our own salvation. Eternal life, abundant life, the life that really is life, is available to us. But it's not ours to create. It's ours to receive. It comes from the depth of love. From our willingness to stop striving and have a conversation with the God who comes to sit with us wherever we are.

Thanks be to God. Amen.
