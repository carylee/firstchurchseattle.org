Pastor Elizabeth

(23:55) I invite you if you brought your Bible with you today, which I encouraged you to do in the E-news, if you read the E-news, it's like a little test, uh, but it's okay, you don't have to raise your hand if you read it, or if you brought your Bible. If you don't see them right away, you can go forward or back a little bit, and you're almost guaranteed to find them.

Now, when people I have known in other contexts, find out that I'm a pastor, one of three things usually happens. Sometimes they tell me about their relationship to church, however distant. Perhaps they grew up going to church, or they say they really should get back to church, or they might tell me that their ex-husband's brother's family is really serious about church. It's as if they think that I can only be friends with people who are somehow connected with church, and so they're trying to make that connection, even if it's kind of a stretch.

The second thing that might happen, if we don't go into this, here's how I have once gone to church before, um if they don't share that relationship, then sometimes people just start confessing their regrets, kind of out of the blue. Um, and they don't usually call these sins per se, um, but they are definitely confessing. And this especially happens, uh it seems, when I'm held captive, like when I'm in the dentist chair, or on an airplane. One minute, we're fasting our seatbelts, and having chitchat that's real polite and kind of vague, and the next, I'm hearing about how they wish they hadn't cheated, or they could have treated their parents better. Uh, and it's a lot. Um, people I guess are just walking around needing to get some stuff off their chests. But most often, uh, what happens is that someone will find out that I'm a pastor after we have been in relationship either for a few minutes or maybe for a few years, depending on the context, and they will look momentarily stricken, and then apologize for their use of bad language, and promise to clean it up in the future.

And I've never seen the doctrine myself, uh, but there's apparently a widespread belief that when a pastor is nearby, you can only think and talk in a certain way. And it doesn't even seem to matter if they have, perhaps, on very rare occasion, particularly if we know each other through some kind of sporting event, heard me use that same language, uh, for which they are now apologizing. They seem to think that I am some kind of saintly tattletale. And that if they use dirty language in my presence, that God will immediately know, and perhaps strike them down.

And maybe that sounds pretty good to you, because I know a lot of us would prefer that the world clean up the language a little bit, when we're around. And it does convey some level of respect for pastors, which I appreciate. But the problem is that it's hard to have a real relationship with someone if they're censoring themselves. If they clam up when I'm around, or if they think they can only use certain words, or say certain things, and they have to hold on to the rest until I'm not around. Then I don't get to know who they really are. And eventually the relationship will stall. It can't really go anywhere, because they don't feel like they can be honest when I'm around. They hold back a part of themselves, and so the relationship eventually fizzles out.

And on the surface, we might think, you know, that's really sad, that someone's bad language kept them from being in an authentic relationship. We might even pity them, or we might think, well, it's a good thing that I never say things like that. But this isn't altogether different from how we tend to behave when we think God is around. I mean, yes, most of us believe that God is everywhere, all the time. But when we're really focused on God's presence, or when we're trying to speak to God, most of us have a sense that there are particular ways we ought to think and talk. Things we can say, and things we can never say. Things we should share, and those we should keep to ourselves.

Many of us are more careful with our words to God, than we might be with our words to our best friend, or our sibling, or even to a child. We have the best intentions, doing it out of respect for God. But when we censor ourselves, we limit our relationship with God. Because we can't have a close relationship with someone to whom we can't or won't speak openly and honestly, showing up as our true and full selves.

So then the question is, how do we find the words to pray openly and honestly before God, to cultivate that deep relationship that our hearts are so longing for? How do we learn the language of prayer? And thankfully, we don't have to look far for instruction. According to Old Testament scholar Ellen Davis, "The single best guide to the spiritual life currently in print is the book of Psalms." It's right there in the middle of our Bibles, a section many of us skip over, between the fun stories of Genesis and Exodus, and the stories about Jesus in the Gospels.

Now, most of us are only familiar with a few of the 150 Psalms. Perhaps we've heard Psalm 23 read at a memorial service, or Psalm 100 cited around Thanksgiving. My fifth-grade class recited it for the Thanksgiving uh assembly at my little Christian school growing up, and I still hear it in a very southern accent in the King James version. We might know a few phrases that we didn't realize are from the Psalms, like, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" which is from Psalm 22. Or, "I lift up my eyes to the hills," which is from Psalm 121. Or, "I am fearfully and wonderfully made," which is from Psalm 139.

But for most of Christian history, the Psalms have been a really important part of spiritual formation. In the fourth century, when the Roman Empire became Christian, many of the most devout disciples moved out into the desert to nurture their faith, and out there, they sustained themselves by learning all 150 Psalms by heart, and reciting them continuously. And Western monks still recite the Psalms in worship, not all 150 every day, but all 150 every week. The Psalms are prayers that have taught and sustained and shaped Jewish and Christian uh adherence for centuries. And they'll nourish us too, teaching us the language of prayer, if we let them.

But when we begin exploring the Psalms in depth, we might find that some of them, the ones we have not heard in church, make us feel a little uncomfortable. Most of the Psalms we are familiar with are Psalms of praise and and Thanksgiving. Their words are pleasant. They are the things that we feel like we should be praying. They fit into Paul's instruction to rejoice in the Lord always, which Ellie just read for us. But there are also Psalms of lament, like Psalm 6, which says, "Be gracious to me, Lord, for I am languishing. Heal me, Lord, for my bones are rattled. I am exhausted with my groaning. I make my bed swim every night. With my tears I melt my mattress. My eye is worn out from vexation, grown old because of all my foes."

I'm languishing, the psalmist says. I'm wasting away. I'm exasperated. My bed is soaked with my tears. How often have we felt that way? And either thought that God must not be present, or that God would be ashamed of our lament. So we hide it, and pretend that everything is fine. The Psalms tell us that when we're languishing, it's only appropriate to take it to God in prayer. And here are words that we can use if we don't have them. Because in the act of naming our laments, our weakness, our yearning, we open ourselves fully and honestly before God, which begins to clear the way for praise later on. It doesn't say we're going to stay languishing forever. The articulation of lament in prayer begins a process of healing, asking God to create the conditions in which it will be possible for us to praise again, even if we can't today. So the Psalms beg us to lament in prayer, if that is what we're feeling. Not to hide it from God, or to pretend like we're fine when we're not.

Or, I invite you to turn over to Psalm 109. This is one of the cursing Psalms. This one begins with a plea for God to listen, because the person who's writing this has a friend that has betrayed them. And here's what he prays. "Oh God," he's addressing this to God, don't forget that. "Appoint someone evil to oppose my enemy. Let an accuser stand at his right hand. When he is tried, let him be found guilty, and may his prayers condemn him. May his days be few. May another take his place in leadership. May his children be fatherless and his wife a widow. May his children be wandering beggars. May they be driven from their ruined homes. May a creditor seize all that he has. May strangers plunder the fruits of his labor. May no one extend kindness to him, or take pity on those fatherless children. May his descendants be cut off. Their names blotted out from the next generation. May the iniquity of his father's be remembered before the Lord. May the sin of his mother never be blotted out. May their sins always remain before the Lord that he may blot out their name from the earth."

Those were some strong feelings! And that's in the Bible. That's Holy Scripture. We probably haven't heard it read in church before, and it might feel surprising that that's in the Bible, but don't try to tell me you have never felt that way. Because all of us feel anger at times, and even if just for a moment, we have all wished the worst on someone who has hurt us. We generally try to banish those thoughts, because we think Christians are supposed to be nice all the time. But here are curses, enshrined in Scripture. Prayers asking God to make someone else's life miserable. And that means that Scripture is telling us to bring even these feelings of anger to God in prayer. To bring even our worst selves into the context of our relationship with God. Because there's no better way to be healed of our hardness of heart. After all, if we hide our feelings from God, how can God transform them? How can God transform us, and make us into people of peace and love, if we don't bring our hearts openly to God?

And there's something bigger at stake here too, because in praying the cursing Psalms at appropriate times, we're also able to name that God too is outraged by betrayal and injustice. By praying the cursing Psalms, we confirm that every believer has a share in the prophetic task of naming and renouncing evil. Even evil in our own community. Even evil that is directed against us. In praying the cursing Psalms, not only do we name that evil, but then we begin to hand our anger over to God, trusting that God will respond, and we don't need to take vengeance into our own hands, which helps us not to repay evil for evil, but to work for good.

And here you thought that the Psalms were outdated and boring, or even irrelevant. And it's true that the language might not feel natural for us. That some of the beauty of the poetry of the Psalms is lost in the translation from Hebrew to English. Or some of it might sound or feel clumsy in our own mouths. But the Psalms are for all of us, for all times. Whether we're lamenting with Psalm 6, or cursing with Psalm 109, or praising God with Psalm 30, which Teresa read for us earlier. Because, of course, so many of the Psalms are Psalms of praise. And Psalm 30 is a classic Thanksgiving Psalm, which tells a story of going through trouble and coming back out of it. The psalmist has gotten through something hard, and he feels like he's got a new lease on life. And so he writes, "Lord, my God, I called to you to help, and you healed me. You rescued me from going into the pit."

So he's not just grateful that the trouble is past. He doesn't just say, "Thank you, God, that I am not now in the pit." He also confesses that God is the one who has saved him. He doesn't assume that his good fortune is something of his own making, which might be something that some of us tend to do. He doesn't pray, "Look at me, God! I'm so awesome! I avoided the pit with my intelligence, and my social connections, and my brute strength." Instead, he gives thanks for God's work in his life, declaring that the proper response is for him to praise God forever. And in sharing that prayer, he reminds us too, to remember when God has rescued us from the pit. To think about all the ways God has worked in our lives, and to respond with praise. And when we do this, the next time we go down to the pit and we need to lament, we also feel more confident that we will come out again. Because we've taught ourselves that that's how it works in our life with God.

Now, I may not have convinced you yet that it would be a great thing for you to memorize all 150 Psalms, and recite them at least weekly in order to cultivate a deep relationship with God. And I I confess to you, I have not memorized uh all the Psalms. I've memorized a few Psalms, um but not not all. But the gift of the Psalms isn't just in memorizing and reciting them, though that's a beautiful way to write them on your heart. The gift of the Psalms for us is that there is one for any way we're currently feeling. There's a Psalm for any experience we're going through, if we'll just look in the book and find it. There's a model for how we can pray, no matter what we're thinking or how we're feeling. We don't have to make up those words. We already have them.

And what's more, is that when we pray these old prayers, we are praying with the saints. We're praying the same words that faithful disciples have prayed for centuries. That monks around the world are praying even right now, surrounding us with these prayers. These are the same prayers that Jesus prayed himself. And in praying them, we join our voices and our hearts with Jesus, drawing even closer to the heart of God. Positioning ourselves inside this circle of saints that offer their own prayers for us, when we aren't sure how to pray on our own. And because we're joining this great cloud of witnesses, because they're the prayers of the whole church in all times and all places, we can also pray them on behalf of others. So, perhaps you like these Psalms of lament, but you are not currently languishing. Well, there's good news. You can pray the Psalms of lament on behalf of those who lament. Perhaps you're not feeling outraged at injustice just now. If so, I'm not sure why, but if you're not, that's okay. You can still pray the cursing Psalms on behalf of those who have been betrayed. Perhaps you're not currently joyful. That's okay. You can still pray the Psalms of praise for those who are. Because all of our prayers get joined together, and God hears them all the same.

Many of us have looked far and wide for the right words for prayer. Many of us believe that while we should pray, or we want to pray, that we just aren't good at it, or we don't have the words. I can't tell you how many times we say, who would like to pray, and people say, oh, I'm not good at that. No, no. Sometimes we even give up on prayer altogether, because we feel certain that our words, even our feelings, or our experiences aren't good enough to bring before God. But the Psalms tell a different story. The rest of Scripture tells us about God, but the Psalms invite us to talk to God. Not just to develop ideas about who God is or how God works in the world, but to enter into that relationship, to enter into conversation, into a spiritual life of depth and meaning. What we need for relationship with God is right here in the middle of our Bibles. We already have all the words, and God wants to hear whichever ones our hearts long to pray. Thanks be to God. Amen.
