## Sermon: What We Need Is All of Us
**Preacher:** Pastor Kathy
**Scripture:** 1 Corinthians 12:12-31

**Pastor Kathy:**
It is appropriate that the adult spirituality class is taking on the topic of loneliness this spring. Just three years ago, in the spring of 2023, US Surgeon General Vivek Murthy declared an epidemic of loneliness in the United States. Over four years, a study at Harvard discovered that people between 30 to 44 years of age were the loneliest group. 29% of people in this age range said they were frequently or always lonely.

Among 18 to 29-year-olds, the rate was 24%. For 45 to 64-year-olds, the rate was 20%. Adults aged 65 and older reported the lowest rate, 10% felt lonely. There were no real gender differences found. Men and women experience similar rates of loneliness, nor were there major differences based on political ideology or race or ethnicity.

Whenever I read statistics about loneliness, I want to take out billboards all over town that say, are you lonely? Try church. Because in the church, God has given us community, an absolute necessity for human beings to thrive. A sense of belonging is vital for human psychological well-being. We need interaction with other human beings to thrive. And this is where we find, learn from, and build community.

But church is not simply community, like work colleagues or PTA or book clubs. This is where we practice co-creating the beloved community with the Holy Spirit. The first story I thought of when Pastor Elizabeth asked me to preach on this passage from 1 Corinthians is the story in the ninth chapter of Luke, of Jesus feeding 5,000 men and who knows how many women and children. When the disciples worried about how the crowd might be growing hungry late in the day, Jesus told the disciples to feed the people.

"But," they argued, "they only had five loaves of bread and two fish among them." So Jesus instructed them to have the crowd sit down in groups of 50. There's a story I like from theologian William Barclay. He tells the story of traveling by train in England. He was seated in a compartment with five other people. As the day wore on, he grew hungry, but he thought it impolite to eat in front of other people, who might not have brought anything to eat. So he sat there. Until a little boy in the compartment pulled a sandwich out of his pack, and unwrapped it, and offered to share it with everyone, breaking off a piece for each person.

Then everyone pulled out what they had brought with them, and an impromptu picnic broke out. In the biblical account, I truly don't know what happened after the disciples broke the crowd into groups of 50. But Barclay suggests that when people sat in groups small enough to see one another's faces, they began to share what they had with others in the group, making sure everyone was fed with food left over.

We live with over 2 million people in King County. So many faces, so many names, so many stories, most of them I think are on the freeway. So many people to serve. So if we're going to make a difference in such a large population, we need to gather in groups small enough to see each other's faces, to get to know each other, and to be known. This is where real community can grow, where we share our stories and care for each other's needs, and learn to build the beloved community.

This is where we're welcomed by name. And here I want to plea for name tags, because you're getting a new pastor in a few weeks, and your new pastor is not going to know your name, so please, name tags. Because new people are going to continue to come to this church. God just keeps inviting new people to this community. Thanks be to God. This is where we are accepted just as we are, from wherever we've come from, or however we've come.

This is where our individual gifts can be shared and celebrated. This is where everyone belongs. Pastor Elizabeth says that at the beginning of every worship service because it's true. Pastor Jackie reminded us of it at the beginning of this service. You belong here. Because we belong in this community, we are invited to share our joys and our concerns so that we can care for one another. I've only been here five short months, but I have seen you in action, looking out for one another, helping each other in times of crisis, and staying connected with members who can no longer attend.

This is where we find not only community, but our sacred identity as the gathered body of Christ. The Apostle Paul uses an organic image of the church as the body of Christ as we heard this morning. Paul equates each one of us with a part of the body and asks how the body can function if any part is either missing or undervalued. He writes, "If the foot would say, 'Because I'm not a hand, I do not belong to the body,' that would not make it any less a part of the body. And if the ear would say, 'Because I'm not an eye, I do not belong to the body,' that would not make it any less a part of the body."

Each of us is important and each of us is God's unique gift to the body. Each of us brings different gifts and geniuses. Each of us brings a different viewpoint and a different lifetime of experience. Different causes tug at our hearts. Diversity is God's gift to the community, so that we can be the whole body of Christ together. Each of our gifts is important for us to work together and to serve the world, and no one gift is more important than another. It's sometimes easy to think that we don't have anything to offer, or that what we have to offer doesn't matter. But that's not true.

Paul asks, "If the whole body were an eye, where would the hearing be? If the whole body were hearing, where would the sense of smell be?" But as it is, God arranged the members of the body, each one of them, as God chose. When one of us isn't here, we don't see as well. Or hear as well. Or serve as well. Each of us using our gifts makes the body of Christ whole, and it is the Holy Spirit that breathes life into the body.

Let's take the metaphor of the body of Christ a step further. Just like the wheat for the bread on the communion table, we are gathered together and kneaded into one loaf, one body. Every Sunday, like the bread, we are blessed and then we are broken, like the bread. Each one of us is broken in some way. And then the body, the loaf, this body is broken apart and given to the world.

The same is true of the cup. We're gathered like the grapes of the vineyard that are pressed into wine that is blessed and poured out to remind us that we are forgiven. Always and forever forgiven. So we are blessed and poured out, after the Benediction, so that we may be agents of forgiveness and mercy for the healing of the world.

There's a part of Paul's metaphor about the body of Christ that used to make me feel uncomfortable. The part where Paul writes about how each part of the body is respected, especially those that seem to be weaker or not respectable. Not language I like. Until I became Aaron's pastor. That's not his real name. In one church that I served, after worship one Sunday, a father brought his young son Aaron to ask me if his son could serve communion.

I thought Aaron was about five years old, and um, I was thinking on my feet, and I don't always do that very well. I chose an age that was enough older that we could get out of this predicament. And I said, um, I usually make children wait until they're seven before they can serve communion. And his father exclaimed, "Aaron, just two more weeks!" So, I sent Aaron home with the words he should say when he held the cup. Two weeks later, that little boy looked each congregant in the eye and spoke the words he'd memorized as he offered the cup, saying, "The cup of new life for you," with dignity and grace.

Aaron and his older brother served communion often from then on. And on Sundays when one of the retired pastors or lay leader wasn't available to lay hands on people who had come for healing prayer during communion, Aaron would walk forward and lay his hands on their shoulders. One Easter morning, he showed up with bunny ears to take his place with the communion servers as he gave each person a piece of chocolate, saying, "For you, because resurrection is sweet."

Our children are an essential part of our community. They need to know that they are God's beloved and that they belong. I think what Paul means in that unfortunate phrase is that every one of us is an essential part of the grace that is the Lord's table. The last thing about all of us being what we need here, um, that I'm going to talk about anyway, is that the beloved community is a place of healing. Where we learn to internalize our identity as God's beloved children. So that we can become the love which we were created to be.

And this beloved community is also a laboratory in which we can practice being peacemakers for a troubled world. God does not invite us into a perfect community. We will not always agree. Or work the same way. Or even be compatible. But we will be beloved by God in our diversity. We are charged by the Holy Spirit to learn to see Jesus in one another, to honor and respect one another, to treat one another as we would treat Jesus.

We are given the gift of the Holy Spirit to help us discern God's will together. In this laboratory of the beloved community, the Holy Spirit calls us to work for justice and peace among ourselves, so that we know how to bring justice and peace to the greater community and to the world. And we're given the gift of each other's sacred stories to reveal the overabundance of God's love and grace. As much as we need to hear the stories of scripture from our biblical forebears, we need to hear each other's stories. They are sacred.

Each of us is a story that God is telling in the world today. What we need is here in the now and not yet beloved community, where God calls us by name, knits us into Christ's body, animates us with the Holy Spirit, and sends us in the world to be God's love and justice and peace in action. Thanks be to God. Amen.
