**Sermon: What We Need Is Bread**
*(16:03) Pastor Elizabeth:* I don't think anyone's actually like beating against the building over here. And it's certainly not thunder. I think they've just replaced those plates on the street again, and it's really loud when something heavy goes over them. So hopefully that won't last long, maybe a week or two. We'll cross our fingers.

There's something, I don't know if this will come as a surprise to you, but there's something a little bit terrifying about preaching. And it's not just the public speaking aspect. Lots of people are afraid of public speaking. But preaching is a little bit different. It's the act of listening for a word from God through the pages of scripture, and discerning what the spirit has to say to a group of people, and then putting that message into words that will adequately express what God wants to say.

In essence, preaching is presuming that one can speak on God's behalf. Which, when you put it that way, sounds a little bit narcissistic and kind of ridiculous. And that's not to mention that we also have to make this message from God interesting enough to hold your attention for 15 or 20 minutes, because how can you hear God's message, even if the words are accurate, if it's so boring that you've checked out before the preacher ever makes her point?

And I share this not to elicit compliments on preaching or to prompt sympathy for work that I feel both called and honored to do, something that I love doing. But to share some of the context that makes communion so especially wonderful for the preacher. It's true that when we have holy communion, we also have a whole separate part of the service, and we have even more of those moments where we worry that we might bumble the words and distract you from your encounter with God. But despite that, the communion liturgy actually comes as a relief.

Because when we celebrate communion, the pastor isn't in charge of the script. What we do at the table was written for us centuries ago. So even if everything else in the service goes wrong, I don't think it's possible for us to mess up communion. Because it isn't about our creativity or our ingenuity or our entertainment. Communion is actually a promise in which a loaf of bread becomes the body of Christ for us. And God shows up, offering us grace.

It all started with the Last Supper, in which the disciples thought they were gathered for the Passover meal, a celebration still carried out today by Jews all over the world, to remember how God rescued the Israelites from slavery by opening up the Red Sea and leading them to freedom. And the word "Passover" refers to the last plague that God brought on the Egyptians through the leadership of Moses, in an effort to convince the Pharaoh to let the Israelites go. And remember that the final plague was a plague of death, in which all the firstborn males would die. But the faithful Israelites were told to sacrifice a lamb and paint their doorframes with its blood. And if they did that, then their firstborn males would be saved. The spirit of death would pass over those homes and not visit them.

So every year, Jews still celebrate the freedom that God brought about through the Exodus, as well as the freedom still to come when God will restore all things. But when he gathered with his disciples for the Passover meal in the Upper Room, Jesus did kind of a strange thing with that liturgy. He said, "Actually, the freedom that you've been waiting for is already here, and it's me." It's a parallel to the story we read a few weeks ago, when Jesus was teaching in the synagogues and he read from the scroll of Isaiah and said, "Today the scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing," and we interpreted that to mean, "I am the good news." Jesus is the good news that we're looking for.

Jesus essentially told the disciples, "There's no need to sacrifice a lamb and put the blood over the door anymore. I'm giving my life for your freedom. My body and my blood." And then Jesus took bread and he gave thanks. He thanked God for feeding us, literally for giving us the food that sustains our bodies. And he thanked God for making him the bread that gives us life. He broke the bread. And he said that his body would be broken too, his blood spilled, that his offering wasn't just a metaphorical or spiritual offering for us, but a physical one, that he was giving his body. And he gave the bread to the disciples and told them to eat it as a way of sharing in this new covenant that God was initiating with the world, a covenant that still stands, inviting us all to participate in God's redemption.

And we just heard Bruce read in 1 Corinthians that the celebration of Holy Communion was already a practice in the early church, rehearsing what God instituted with this simple formula in the Gospels, that we tell the story and we remember the body of Christ. And when we say "remember," we don't just mean "think, think, remember" in our heads, but "re-member," "member again," become members again of the body of Christ. And then we ask the Holy Spirit to do her work and we participate in this action with our bodies, with our hands, our feet, with all of our senses. And we trust that these simple elements, bread and juice, representatives of the very basics that we need to be sustained, nothing fancy or unfamiliar, that these elements become the grace of God for us. And the promise is that no matter how we might fumble the rest of the business of being church, whenever we gather at the table, God shows up.

Now, clearly you could write a book, or several, about the history and the theology and the practice of communion, and many people already have. And we could talk all day about the logistics of the different ways that people celebrate Holy Communion. With wafers or bread, if you use pita or Hawaiian loaf, if you use juice or wine, if you stand or kneel, if you take communion in your seats or if you gather around the sanctuary, or if you line up here, or if you make two lines and just come through, or if you take it daily, or weekly, or monthly, or quarterly. All of these things are different depending on your community of faith. But I'm not sure talking about those details is really helpful.

So instead, I thought it might be good for us this morning, before we come to celebrate communion together, to talk about what we do at the table, and why it's important, and why it's powerful. And so you'll notice that in our liturgy, even though it changes a little bit from week to week, the first thing is always that we tell the story. We remind ourselves that the story of salvation for us didn't begin when we were baptized, or when we first came to church, or when we first took Holy Communion together. That our story didn't even begin with the death and resurrection of Jesus, or the Last Supper that he shared with his friends. Instead, our story starts at the very beginning of time. Our story is the whole history of God. And it began when God breathed life into the world and called it good.

And that story, the story of salvation, includes the story of the Passover, of the liberation of God's people, both out of Egypt and out of every bondage we've been in since. And that story includes our tendency to turn away from God, and God's promise to be present with us anyway, no matter what, all the time. And in retelling the story every time we gather here, we remind ourselves that it's not a story that we made up. And it's not a story we're responsible for. And it's not a story that we can mess up, or alter, or even improve. It's a story that we've been invited into. It's the story of salvation for the whole world.

And because it's a story we can never deserve, one we can't earn, we call that story, the prayer that we pray to tell the story, the Great Thanksgiving. The retelling of the story is itself a prayer of gratitude. So we begin our practice of communion by thanking God for the whole story, and for including us in it, for never giving up on us. And that's why some Christians call it the Eucharist, from the Greek word *eucharisteo*, which means "to give thanks." It has its roots in the Greek word *charis*, which means "grace." So it's a sacrament of grace in which we give thanks for the story that we have been invited into that's not ours, that we could never earn.

And after the Great Thanksgiving, once we have given thanks, then comes the words that we say are the words of institution. And when we say "institution," we don't mean institution like an organization or the words of the church, but these are the words that instituted the practice, that started the practice, the words that began it all. And sometimes I like to sing the words, because I think it helps us to hear them in a different way. But whether we sing them or speak them, the liturgy always includes Jesus's own words from the Last Supper, when he says, "This is my body, given for you. This is my blood, poured out for you."

And during the words of institution, the pastor takes the bread and juice and probably holds them up to bless them and to thank God for them, just like Jesus did at the Last Supper. And then we might break the bread and show you that it's for you, for everyone gathered, that it's for anyone who wants to take part. And by repeating Jesus's own words, we remember that he's giving himself to us, in forgiveness and relationship and liberation. That this whole thing isn't just a little snack time in the middle of the service, it's not just a chance to get up and stretch your legs, but it's actually an offering and a receiving, that it's part of our relationship with God.

And then after we say the words of institution, we pray for the Holy Spirit to come, to rest on everybody that's here, everybody that's gathered for this worship, wherever they are, and on the bread and the juice, to make them somehow the body and blood of Jesus. And you might notice that when we do this, I hold my hands out over you as a way of trying to direct the Holy Spirit onto you, and then over the bread and the juice to do the same thing. And we believe that something happens when we do this, that the Spirit comes in and it changes us in some way. And I don't claim to know how that works. And theologians have been arguing about it for centuries, about what actually happens when we pray this prayer, what actually happens to the bread to make it into the grace of God for us.

But it's something we can't really explain. So in that part we have to lean into the mystery of God, and trust that whatever's happening, the bread and juice can actually be transformed into the body and blood of Jesus for us. And at the same time, we pray that that spirit will come into us and transform us too. Transform our hearts to look more and more like the heart of Jesus, so we can do his work in the world.

And here's a fun fact for you, you can use this at trivia if you want. In the old Latin Mass, this part of the communion liturgy, which is called the Epiclesis, includes the words *hoc est corpus meum*. And that's in Latin, it means "this is my body" in Latin. And the priest always said these words with his back to, he was facing the altar and his back is to the congregation. And so people didn't really know Latin, so he's saying *hoc est corpus meum*, he's probably mumbling because he's done this so many times. And they can't really hear him because he's facing the other way, and it sounds like "hocus pocus." And that's where we get the term "hocus pocus." That's why we say "hocus pocus" when we want something magic to happen. Because we believe that something is happening when the priest is up here saying his words, even if we don't know what it is. That's true, that's where we get the term "hocus pocus." The next time you try to do a magic trick and you say "hocus pocus," you're actually participating in the communion liturgy. Which is really cool. And that's not to say that what happens in communion is magic. It's not magic, it's the Holy Spirit. And those are similar but different. But we do believe that something actually happens when we say these words. We don't know how, but it's something extraordinary that turns this ordinary bread into a sacrament of grace. And how it works is a mystery.

And so after we've prayed the hocus pocus, after we've asked God to do something, to work in these mysterious ways, then it's up to all of us to participate. Whoever wants to. All are welcome. And in our church we come forward to receive. And that's intentional. As a sign that we are responding to God's invitation. God doesn't have to come all the way, God has come a long way to be present here, but we have to do something too. So we get up and we come forward as we can to receive the invitation. We use our whole bodies, because Jesus gave his body. Because Jesus came as a person and not just an idea. And we stand here in the midst of the congregation, surrounded by others, to remind ourselves that we can't do this alone. That we're all together as we re-member the body of Christ, and we need each other. We can't do this at home by ourselves, just one person at a time. We need the community, even if we are worshiping from afar.

And we take the bread and the juice so that we can touch and taste and smell and see and even hear the grace of God. So we can feel it in our bodies and not just think about it with our minds. And ideally, we take the bread and juice from one loaf and one cup, as a prayer for unity and belonging. And we're working toward getting back to one loaf in this congregation, instead of having all these little pre-cut cubes of bread. But however we do it, coming forward and taking these elements is itself a prayer, in which we ask God to transform us from the inside out. In which we believe that if God can change a loaf of bread into grace, then God can change us too.

And through it all, no matter how much we understand, or how much we like the particular customs observed in this church, or the liturgy said by whatever pastor's leading, the underlying gift is in literally communion. Because all of us are seeking communion in one way or another, all the time. Sometimes we try to commune with nature. Sometimes we try to commune with our possessions, sometimes with other people. We seek to know and be known, to love and be loved. This is part of being human. But when we come to this table, we're reminded that we are created for communion with God. That God wants to be in communion with us. That God invites us to be in communion with each other. Together as one body, fed by this one meal.

So we can go all over, looking for connection with the divine. We can search the world hoping for something inspirational to happen for us, wanting to have an epiphany or a mountaintop moment or a flash of lightning that will change our lives forever. That moment when we might feel the presence of God. We might try any practice out there to nudge our souls toward completion or holiness or enlightenment. But when it comes right down to it, maybe all we need is a little piece of bread, and the reassurance that in that bread, God is present some way, somehow. And God promises to show up for us again and again, every time we gather, to nourish and sustain us, so we can be life for the world. We may not know how to explain it, but God keeps showing up. God is in the bread. Thanks be to God. Amen.
