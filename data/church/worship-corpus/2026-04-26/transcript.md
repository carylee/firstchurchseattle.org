**Sermon**
*What We Need Is Here: Welcome Home*
Pastor Elizabeth: I was just thinking neither of my children have ever asked for a goat to celebrate with their friends. I don't know if that means we're doing a great job with parenting or I don't know what that means.

In our First Church Forward team, as we're working toward a strategic planning and talking about the church's mission and vision and goals, we've spent a lot of time over the last couple of months thinking about the state of the church. Our church, this congregation, as well as churches in Seattle and Christian churches in America and even the church universal. And we've used the word unprecedented a number of times. Talking about the times that we're living in and the challenges we face and the culture in which we live.

We've been asking ourselves who we're called to be and what we're called to do, as our country goes to war and rolls back environmental and civil rights protections and attacks immigrants and ignores the vulnerable and degrades democracy often in the name of Jesus. And many of us on our team, in our congregation, in our community, may be lost enough in this current world that we're trying whatever latest thing is showing up in the news or on our social media feeds, wanting to jump into trends, to fight back or to help or to join a cause. Whatever is the new thing.

Others of us may be enticed by consumerism, trying to buy our way to feeling better, or putting our hopes in overworking or stockpiling resources, trying to protect ourselves and those closest to us. Or perhaps we've gone a slightly different direction and we're focused on improving ourselves. Maybe we've started a new hobby, or joined a club or developed a yoga practice, or maybe we're eating more protein and cold plunging and sleep tracking and the like. Sometimes discovering something that feels helpful, but often just kind of thrashing about, trying to find the thing, the activity, the resource, the group that will help us through the living of these days.

And we may even have perceived by now that this is as much a spiritual problem as anything else. And that spirituality itself has also become an industry in which people are constantly trying to sell us things. That there are hundreds of companies and even more individuals who will sell us a gimmick or an oil or a special cushion to sit on or a bell to ring or an app to use or a mantra to say, that will help us to center ourselves and grow spiritually, that we might feel better about the world even if it hasn't changed much in our favor.

And some of us have gone to great lengths, looking for that golden ticket that will instantly transport us to a higher plane on which we can commune with God without effort. That place where we can block out the noise of the world and hear the movement of the Spirit, where God might speak to us directly and tell us how to live.

I confess that I personally have done my share of searching. Trying out the schedule of the Benedictines and the prayers of the Franciscans. Walking the labyrinth and praying with beads or knots, depending on which tradition has caught my eye. And of course, I've bought so many books. So, so many books. Handbooks and workbooks and self-help books and history books and memoirs and systematic theologies and calls to social justice and difficult truth-telling stories that are meant to inspire and transform.

But what I've realized, and what I hope you come to trust in the days ahead, is that everything we need is actually here. That we don't have to search far and wide, that we don't need the latest thing, or to sample a little bit of everything, or even to try something new and different. Because what we need for a deep and rich spiritual life, for communion with God, for finding truth, for the community and resource we need to work for love and peace and justice in the world, everything we need is here. In the church. Even in this church today.

Now, you've probably heard today's gospel lesson before, in some form or another, read to us and sung to us so beautifully by the choir. It's a story we often refer to as the story of the prodigal son. A story of a searcher. Someone who wanted to try the latest thing, who wanted to get in on the new trends, to travel the world, to be enlightened. Someone who was looking to satisfy that longing deep inside, and went searching for it.

He asked his father if he could have his inheritance early, which was basically like saying, "Dad, I'm getting impatient waiting for you to die. And I'm wondering if you can just give me the money now." It's a request that probably wouldn't go over super well in any era, but at that time was even worse than it would be now. Because the younger son in the family would have been obligated to take care of his father in his old age. After all, there were no nursing homes then, no long-term care insurance. And by asking for his inheritance and going away, this younger son would be abandoning that obligation, abandoning his father without a caregiver.

In that cultural context too, sons didn't usually grow up and move away like they do now. Instead, the transfer of property from one generation down to the next was the very most important asset a family had. So to ask for his inheritance like this son did, was to ask his father to divide up the family's land now, and sell part of it off to a stranger, giving away something the family would have otherwise held onto for generations, land they would have relied on.

And so, we don't know why the father in this story gives the son what he asks for. The law actually said that he could have beat or disowned him just for asking for his inheritance. We think some of our laws are strange, there were, there were some interesting ones then too. And surely the father's feelings were hurt by his son's request. I know mine would have been. But for reasons we don't know, the father gave him the money and set him off on his big adventure. Perhaps because he too understood that longing to find himself, to be inspired, to experience something new, to make a difference in the world. Maybe the father too had wanted to go off in search of enlightenment and had never had the chance. So he blessed his younger son and sent him on the way.

Of course, what the son found out in the world wasn't spiritual depth or his life's vocation or a way to make a difference in the world. He did not encounter lifelong friends or a worthy cause to devote himself to. He didn't find the key to self-fulfillment or lasting change. Instead, he wasted his inheritance on wild living. Some of the translations say he squandered it, which is such a good word. He threw it away, searching for something that could never be bought. And then to add to the drama to the story, a famine came over the land, and the son had nothing to eat. So he got a job feeding pigs, unclean animals that Jews were not supposed to touch. And then somewhere in between the barn and the feeding trough and the grumbling of his empty stomach, the son came to his senses and realized that what he needed was at home. That it had already been there before he went off wandering.

So he decided to humble himself and go back. And he practiced his apology all the way back home, planning to ask his father if he could work in his own home as a servant. But you know how it goes, that all the days that the son had been away, squandering his fortune, the father had been watching for his return. Maybe it was just a broken heart that made the father keep his eye on the road, just hoping, praying that his son might come back. Or maybe the father knew that what the son really needed had been there all along, but that he had to discover it for himself, and that that might take a while.

Either way, before the son could get all the way home, the father ran out to greet him, and cut off his apology, and told the slaves to kill the fatted calf and bring out the royal treatment, and invite the neighbors to a party. Because his lost son had been found. The son would not become a servant in his father's household. Instead, he would be honored and cherished, adored as one who truly belongs. And that doesn't mean that home was perfect for this family, for this father-son duo or trio. It wasn't perfect before the younger son went away, and it certainly wasn't after he came back.

The story tells us about the older son, who's angry that his brother got celebrated for squandering his inheritance, while his much more responsible brother was home working double time. And that older brother had every right to pitch a fit and say, "That's not fair." Because it wasn't. And the father had a lot of tough parenting left to do with both of these sons. The repair of those relationships would take a lot of hard work, and a lot of time. And the economic situation in the household was likely worse than it had been before too. They were down to about two-thirds of their property. And nobody knew how it would play out when the father eventually died and the younger son had no inheritance, no land, no way to support himself. Home for this family was far from perfect, far from ideal. But it was enough. It was good. It was exactly what they needed.

And I think the church is a lot like that. This is a community that will never be perfect, because it's made up of imperfect humans, who don't always keep our cool and sometimes have trouble getting along, and sometimes pitch fits, and sometimes even take our money and go home. Even though we promise in our vows of membership to be the best community we can be, it'll never be perfect here. We'll always hurt one another. Some will always give more, and some will always take more. The church will never have every program you want it to have, and you might hate the new banner or your neighbor's favorite hymn or the coffee we serve in the narthex. And someone might miscommunicate something, and somebody else might not show up when you wanted them to. No church is perfect. And some have even done more harm than good, we have to acknowledge that.

But for reasons we may never understand, when God made us, God arranged us into communities. And these we call churches. God said, "These are, these churches are the body of Christ." We are Christ's body for the world. We are designed to be together in this way, as imperfect as we are. Saint Augustine said that God is our native country, the one from whom we came and to whom we belong, that our hearts are restless until they find their rest in God. And I think that is the restlessness that makes us go looking for the latest and greatest, that makes us jump onto all those trends. We want to find answers, yes, but we're really looking for God. And maybe we go out there to find God, because in here feels so ordinary and unremarkable, and at the same time so challenging. We do the same things week after week, praying it will mold our hearts in the right way. We're not catching the latest trends in here, but trusting the practices of millennia that are still worthwhile. And it's hard work being in community together with people we didn't necessarily choose sitting next to us. Looking honestly at our lives and asking who God calls us to be. We love for there to be a magic solution to faith and life, a product we could buy or a place we could go to solve all the world's problems on our own. And yet, we were made to be together. And when we gather, what we need is here. God is here. The good news is here. The rhythms of our faith are here. You are here. Your gifts and your wisdom and that of your neighbors, the community is here. Everything we need for the living of these days is here in the church.

It's not necessarily fancy or flashy or new. But if you're searching for life with God, for depth and meaning, for vocation, for relationship, for ways to pursue love and peace and justice in the world, you can find it here. And maybe the best part of all of it is that all you have to do is show up. There are no prerequisites. There is no dress code. You don't have to have it all figured out. In fact, you don't really have to have anything figured out. If you've already squandered your life's savings on wild living, well, you can show up empty-handed, and you will still be fed. If you're in the middle of a pity party because life isn't fair, you're still invited, and we'll wait patiently until you're ready to come inside. The journey won't be perfect, and at times it won't be easy. We'll all make mistakes along the way. But no matter who you are or where you've come from, what you've experienced and what you bring with you, God has had God's own eye on the road all this time, just waiting to run out and greet you, to cut off your apology, and bring out the royal treatment, to say, "Welcome home. Welcome home. You're exactly where you belong, and everything you need is here." Thanks be to God. Amen.
