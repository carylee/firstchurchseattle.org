## Sermon: Love's Many Faces
**Preacher:** Barbara Moreland
**Scripture:** Exodus 2:1-10, 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

[29:45]
Good morning.

Today is Mother's Day. Bet you didn't know that.

It's a time to honor the women who gave us life, who put up with our two-year-old. Yes. It's a time to honor the ones who put up with our two-year-old tantrums, survived our teenage drama, applauded our first wobbly steps into quote real adulthood.

Lots of memories.

Some of us are fortunate in that our relationship with our mothers was strong and honest and loving and supportive. Probably not perfect, probably not without conflict, but we were given a solid foundation on which we could grow and become more or less competent, functional, productive individuals.

For others, Mother's Day is a bit more complicated. For some, the memory of losses can be quite painful. The day can bring a mixture of sadness combined with gratitude for time shared over the years. But for most of us, Mother's Day is a time to reflect and remember and to realize that we are more like her than we ever thought we would be.

As most of you know, I call myself a word person. I believe words are incredibly powerful and they must be used carefully and with respect. So I want to pause for a disclaimer and a word of explanation. I'm going to be using the words mother and mothering a lot in the next few minutes. But I am not necessarily referring to the woman who gave birth to you or even to a female person.

Mothers, as we're going to be thinking about it, is more about relationship and less about biology. The face that you see when you use this term will be special to you and only to you.

What I call mothering, a verb, is an active empowering force that is not restricted to any particular gender or physical body. Mothering is a relationship, built on connections between real people in the real world in real time. It involves being present and attentive, showing compassion and engaging with the other person. Mothering says, I hear you. I see your pain. I'm sorry you're hurt. Let me help. These mothering words can bring healing and connection. But mothering always includes taking action, offering support and providing protection.

Because the real power of mothering is love. Love that moves, love that is fierce and decisive, love that takes chances, love that results in a full life for the beloved. Mothering is one of love's many faces.

I have discovered, not surprisingly, probably to most of you, that many of my favorite Bible stories involve strong women who get stuff done. Our first scripture about the story of Moses's birth is just that kind of story. It's a story of women who found solutions to a real, immediate problem. You know, none of those women were given names in that story. Although the mother is named in a genealogical list in Exodus, and from stories later on, we know the sister's name is Miriam. But these unnamed women told an incredible story. Because the problem that they face is that the Pharaoh has decided that all male babies born to the captive Hebrew people are to be killed. The mother defies the order, nurses her baby in secret for as long as she can, and then develops an action plan. She sets him out in a basket in a way that he will be discovered. And then sets her the her the baby's sister in place to watch over. The sister bravely approaches the ruler's daughter with a plan to keep the mother's baby connection intact.

In giving her baby a chance to survive, the mother has saved his life. But imagine the conflict between terror and love that a mother has to make, has to face in making that sacrifice. She has to take such an incredible chance in order to save her son.

To put the decision she faces into a modern context, think about a Russian mother who tells her son to leave the country so he's not drafted into a war machine military. Or a Haitian family who faces sending their daughter alone across the border to protect her from the horrors at work of war at home. How strong does that love have to be?

Mothering means doing all you can, including breaking the rules and taking a chance. And in the end, mothering love steps out in faith, let's go and prays there's somebody on the other side to help.

Breaking the rules is another one of love's many faces. I heard a conversation not long ago that came back to me when I was reading the Moses scripture. It was in response to that story about the deportation of a mother and her young son, and the child had cancer and they were going to send him back, but they weren't going to let him take his cancer drugs with him. And the person that I heard talking was actually criticizing and denouncing parents who put their children at risk by breaking the law.

I would argue that the persons who are to be held accountable for the health of that child are not the um, not the parents. It's the authorities who believe that their own lives and their own children are more worthy of security and medical support than anyone else.

The truth is that you and I should be standing up with the strength of mothering love and confronting those people who deny others humanity.

I imagine in the time that I've been speaking, you have been picturing someone who has given you that fierce, empowering, laughter-filled, world-challenging, mothering love. And if you haven't, think for a minute.

I want you to bring that face firmly into focus. Hear the voice of that person in your ear. Remember all the ways that person has loved you, supported you, taught you, endured you. Feel the acceptance and grace, the comfort that that person gives you. And put that face in front of you. Because now we're going to acknowledge those, those persons. We're going to read the first section of the scriptures that we heard just a minute ago. And they'll be up on the screen. And where you see the word love, I want you to substitute that person's name. And later on it will say it instead of love. But you'll know the spot. So, um, can we get those, can we get that slide up.

So I want to read this together, and where you see the word love, or in the latter part it, use that person's name. Join me.

If I speak in the tongues of humans and of angels, but do not have [name], I am a noisy gong and a clanging symbol. If I have prophetic powers and understand all mysteries and all knowledge, and have all faith so as to remain mountains, but do not have [name], I am nothing. If I give away all my possessions, and if I hand over my body so that I may boast, but do not have [name], I gain nothing.
