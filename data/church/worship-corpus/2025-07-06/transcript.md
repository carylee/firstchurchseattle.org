### Sermon: The Unstoppable Expanding Peace of God
**Preacher:** Rev. Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** Luke 10:1-11, 16-20

Let us pray.

Let the words of my mouth and the meditations of our heart be acceptable not to each other, but to you, oh Lord. Amen.

If you've ever seen a fractal image, you may find it quite fascinating. The infinitesimal, seemingly smaller shapes are duplicated within themselves to make the same shape, but larger, with a process that can often continue on to infinity.

Fractals are found throughout our natural world: in trees, plants, shells, frost, in ecosystems like our coral reefs, and even in our yummy vegetables, showing us how the smallest elements work together to make up the whole. Similarly, the simplest actions we take in our daily lives are an important part of a larger network and how we respond to and care for one another. No action we take is too small, as each of us can make a positive impact on the world when we lead an active Christian life centered on God's peace and love.

In the Gospel reading we heard today, Jesus appoints and sends out 70 persons to all the places he himself intended to go. But before Jesus sends out these 70, he first sends out the 12 named disciples on a similar mission: to proclaim the kingdom of God and to heal. They had been traveling alongside Jesus, learning from him as he performed miracles and taught the good news message. So it wasn't too surprising when he sends them out on their own. Perhaps Jesus is trying to prepare them for when they will for when he will no longer be with them.

However, the 70 that are sent out hadn't previously been mentioned, and they aren't included in the other Gospels. We wonder, who who are they? They aren't named or described. We don't know if they are men, women, non-binary persons, or children. We don't know if they are cisgender or transgender. We don't know their ethnicity, their culture, their language, their education level, or their life stories. And yet Jesus places trust in them to share an important message and to proclaim the coming of the kingdom of God.

The number 70 may hold some significance. It might be a reference to the 70 elders of Israel found in Numbers, that Moses appoints to help him lead in the wilderness. Or it might be a reference to all the nations of the time, believed to be about 70. In either case, it feels representational, and it offers us an opportunity to connect at a personal level with the text. We also recognize that the author of Luke also wrote the book of Acts, writing them together in relationship to one another. Therefore, we can consider these stories of being sent out as part of a larger sequence, which includes Jesus calling the first calling first the 12 disciples, the disciples then following Jesus as they listen and observe his teachings, Jesus then sending out the 12 disciples on their own, Jesus then sending out the 70 in pairs to places he intended to go. Later, Jesus appearing to the 12 at the end of the Gospel, after his death and resurrection, telling them to proclaim repentance and forgiveness in Jesus's name to all the nations. And then in Acts, the 12 and all gathered in Jerusalem, being baptized with the Holy Spirit at Pentecost. This is the beginning of the early church, where more will continue to be sent out in their own way across time.

Now, we know a lot more about the 12 disciples than we do about these 70 persons, including how the disciples were each called by Jesus, and how they gave up all they knew to follow him. While we can recognize the ways we are each individually and uniquely called to follow Jesus, sometimes it may be difficult for us to connect personally with the with how the disciples left everything behind: their vocations, their families, and our lives of comfort and enjoyment. This is true even for those of us that accept a lifetime call to ministry.

However, while we may sometimes struggle to see ourselves in the disciples' story, this passage offers us an opportunity to see ourselves in the 70. The 70 helps us recognize that all God's people, each one of us, are sent out, even ordinary everyday people like ourselves. We're sent out to share the good news message through our words and through our actions. The sequence speaks more speaks to more than just an expansive of Christianity over time and space. It's also about the powerful message that is sent out with the followers and helpers of Jesus.

Jesus provides a little more instruction for the 70 than he did for the 12 initially. He describes the importance of offering peace first, and gives instructions also for receiving peace. He also tells them to proclaim that the kingdom of God has come near to you. This peace and the kingdom of God, or kindom of God as we often say, go hand in hand.

We here at First Church will often use kindom in place of kingdom to move us away from empire language. Language that emphasizes kings and power. Instead, kindom refers to our interconnectedness with God, with creation, and with one another, recognizing that we are all God's beloved. The kindom of God will not be fully realized until every human and creature on Earth is valued and appreciated and can live free of discrimination and oppression. Therefore, the 70 are offering a holistic message of peace.

We use a worship resource here called enfleshed. It provides us with liturgy, um, that we often use in our call to worship and other elements in our service. It also provides us with commentary that's focused on collective liberation, and it's written by individuals who often find themselves on the margins of Christianity. The commentator for this week, Nicola Torbett, describes the type of peace used here in this passage, saying, "The Hebrew word Luke was almost certainly referencing would have been shalom, which means not just an absence of negative things like strife and violence, but the presence of wholeness, well-being, and right relationships with all other beings."

When we understand peace in this way, we can appreciate the importance of working to build these right relationships with all beings to help bring forth the kindom of God here on Earth. You may have experienced this type of peace before, especially when offered through the kindness and compassion of a stranger in times of need.

My brother is visiting with his family today, and boy, could we tell you of some road trips road trip stories from our time as kids. For some reason, we seem to have the most car trouble at Thanksgiving. One of those times, we received an amazing, unexpected kindness from a stranger ourselves. We were about halfway into a 12-hour trip, almost at the Ohio-Pennsylvania state border, when our dad realized that something was wrong with one of our tires. He pulled off to the side of the highway and found that most of our lug nuts were broken. Our wheel was hanging on by just a thread.

Luckily, we found a hotel right off the exit for the night. But the next day was Thanksgiving, and we knew it would be nearly impossible to find anything open in the small town, let alone any way to get there. For you young ones here, that was well before the time of Uber and Lyft. Our dad decided to walk down the road a little ways to see if he could find anyone to help. He took my brother and myself with him so that he might be less intimidating to a stranger. Nearby, an older man was hosing off his tractor. We stopped to explain what had happened, and not only did this man call up his friend to open up his auto store for us, but he drove us there in his pickup.

We were able to get what we needed and make it to our family late on Thanksgiving evening. The kindness and compassion this man offered us acknowledged our humanness and interconnected relationship, even though we were strangers to one another. Perhaps you have a similar story of how a stranger shared with you the most unexpected kindness, or perhaps you were the stranger providing help.

What would it mean for us to truly offer God's holistic peace to one another? Are we doing that today? Will that same kindness and peace be offered to individuals and communities that look different from us, that talk differently from us? Are we truly offering the same level of peace to the strangers in our midst, to the immigrants, refugees, and asylum seekers? To our Black and Brown siblings, our queer community, our transgender and non-binary friends and loved ones, and to differently abled bodies? Are we recognizing the beloved sacredness of each other? Do we seek out the well-being for each individual first before considering our own desires and comforts?

I dare say, when we spend more collectively to build cold, drab walls of concrete that deny people of their human rights and dignity, then we spend to ensure everyone has access to health care they need. We are not. And when we spend more collectively on weapons of war and destruction, rather than tools of plow shares and pruning hooks, to make sure that all are fed, we are not. I could go on and on, as I'm sure most of you could as well.

Friends, we seem to be living in a time where our society has stopped seeing each other as human. Our society dehumanizes one another, criminalizes one another, blames one another, and tries to hold on to control that really isn't ours to have. We focus too much on ourselves. We are afraid to let go and let Jesus truly lead us. We have become obsessed with money and with taking land for our own uses, for making ourselves wealthy, for having the easiest access to the resources that we want. We then destroy our planet to extract those resources, leaving devastating holes in our natural world that cause further harm to the already most vulnerable communities in our midst and in our world. We take and take and take, and ignore the harm that we are doing, ignore the ways we are breaking our internal relational connectedness with each other.

And so friends, we need to reorient our society to God's holistic peace. That works to repair the holes left in the fabric of our interconnectedness, to put us back into right relationship with God and each other. We can each play a part, even when we might not be the ones making the decisions.

Speak up and speak out. The harm being caused to each other is not in line with the teachings of Jesus, as he said, "Whenever you enter a town, and they do not welcome you, go out into the streets and say, 'Even the dust of your town that clings to our feet, we wipe off in protest to you.'"

We need healing of who we are as a nation. We cannot truly celebrate our independence until all the oppressed have been set free. It is time to accept God's invitation to be sent out. Go out with a willing heart in faith, even when you might not know what's ahead or how you will be received.

Just last week, we attended our Annual Conference's Commissioning and Ordination Service. As each group of clergy and laity accepted their appointments that were set by the bishop for the year, we sang a verse of "I'm going to live so God can use me." Just as we sang earlier today. I invite you to remember those words and consider how you are going to live each day so God can use you to continue expanding God's holistic message of peace. What actions of kindness and compassion will you share with your family, with your friends, as well as the strangers you may never meet? Friends, even when we become so overwhelmed with the current state of our world, let us remember that God's peace and love always wins. This is God's world, and I assure you that God's peace is unstoppable. God will continue to use each and every one of us to further the fulfillment of the kingdom of God here on Earth. Some days it may feel impossible to make an impact, but remember those fractal images, where the smallest components help form larger and infinite components. We are but one piece in this sequence. Our tiniest actions of good combine with the tiniest actions of good from one another to form and create the most beautiful image of an ever expanding network of God's peace. Are you ready to be sent out?

All praise be to God. Amen.
