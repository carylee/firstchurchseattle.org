## Sermon: Interdependence
**Preacher:** Rev. Laura Baumgartner
**Scripture:** 1 Corinthians 12:12-27

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
Good morning. It is a joy to be with you in worship today. I want to say thank you for welcoming me here and thank you for allowing Pastor Wanji to be at Haller Lake and to participate in this pulpit swap this morning. Again, I'm Pastor Laura Baumgartner. My pronouns are she, her. And I am so glad to be worshipping with you today as part of the body of Christ.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
I want to begin with a poem called The Sun by Mary Oliver. Have you ever seen anything in your life more wonderful? Then the way the sun every evening relaxed and easy floats toward the horizon. And into the clouds or the hills or the rumpled sea and is gone. And how it slides again out of the blackness, every morning on the other side of the world like a red flower. Streaming upward on its heavenly oils. Say on a morning in early summer at its perfect imperial distance. And have you ever felt for anything such wild love? Do you think there is anywhere in any language a word billowing enough for the pleasure that fills you as the sun reaches out as it warms you as you stand there, empty-handed. Or have you too turned from this world. Have you gone too crazy for power for things.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
This week in Seattle was a sunny week, and of all the days Tuesday was a very warm and sunny day. I had a friend in town and we decided that we would go to the beach at the end of the day. After dinner, we packed up swimsuits and towels and headed out as the sun began to recede beyond the horizon. As the last vestiges of light made their way west, we dipped our toes in the water. As the light faded from the sky, we knelt in the water that had been warmed all day by the sun's rays. As darkness began to envelop us, we paddled around, cooling our skin and feeling the support of the buoyancy of the lake. As all the other beachgoers abandoned us to the comfort of their cars and homes, we stayed watching the stars come out, and marvelled at how warm it was still on a September evening in Seattle, that we could lay on the beach drying after sundown. As we lay there, the sun went on shining its light on other parts of the earth, and other stars went on shining their light where we could see them. We were tiny players, yet connected within the system of the planets and stars that God had put together and set in motion.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
We were dependent on the water to hold us up, and on the air to have been warmed all day, keeping us comfortable. We were dependent on the plants that had been part of our dinner to give us energy, and on the people who had prepared it and brought it to our table for sustenance. We were alone on the beach, but we were not isolated or independent of others or of God. Our scripture this morning tells of this connection between members of the body of Christ. Paul writes to the church in Corinth that they might remember their connection one to the other. He admonishes them to remember that the one they think of as less honorable or weaker is actually indispensable. And he does this by comparing the church to a human body. Where would the body be, he asks, if one part could decide it had no need for the others. Instead, he explains that God has put things together and set them in motion in such a way that if one suffers, all suffer with it, and if one member is honored, all rejoice together with it. So it is with the church. We bear each other's pain. We share each other's joys. We are connected, one person to another, one committee to another perhaps, this morning, one congregation to another. We rely on one another. We depend on one another. We are interdependent. All of us are interdependent, put together and set in motion by God's love and mercy, grace, and power. All of us are interdependent, put together and set in motion by the movement of the Holy Spirit among us, enlivening us to do what is possible through her nudging. All of us are interdependent, put together and set in motion by the risen one, who moves with us through this life, who knows what it is to walk the solid ground of this planet, to feel the water hold him up, to cool his skin in the gathering darkness of an evening, and who knows the sorrow and pain of loss that is part of this life too.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
We are interdependent, and also completely dependent on God. In addition to the analogy of the human body that Paul wrote about, I would liken us, interdependent people in the church, to the interdependent planetary system in which we reside. I would liken us, the interdependent people in the church, very similar to the fish and the herons and the beavers and the salmon and the humans, the sky, the sea, the air, and the soil. We are interdependent parts of a whole. No one component of the system of that which sustains life in this place is more important than the others. When we might imagine that one part is weaker or less respectable, we find that it is actually indispensable and deserving of our great respect. Much the way that the church is dependent on God's love and grace, mercy and power to keep us going. The planetary system is dependent on energy from the sun to provide what is needed to sustain life. The sun's light provides energy for plants to grow, which produce fruit, which give food for all humans, animals, and other animals beyond humans. The sun's light is also responsible for fuels that gather below the ground from remnants of those who grew on the land or ate the fruits of the soil, and over many years, those remnants eventually become fossil fuels. What we can otherwise know them as is just concentrated solar energy. Beyond even that, the sun's energy is responsible for the movement of water around our planet and air for rain and storms, rivers and glaciers, Gulf streams, and all the life that is carried in them. The sun is the energy source for almost everything on our planet.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
Environmental scientists describe the planet as like a wheel with paddles sticking out around it, the kind that you might see on an old-fashioned windmill or a grain mill or paddle boat. The planet is set up to be kept in motion by that which lands on those paddles and pushes it around its axle. That is the sun. It spins that wheel, keeping the whole planet functioning. So if the church is like an interdependent planet, then the sun is like the source of God's love for the church. This sun, our sun, this great star that provides and provides and provides some more for those who live on the planet, is a source of energy for all our lives, and the lives of all those who are here with us, the way that God is the source of love for all our lives, and the lives of all of those who share our common home as well. It might not seem like it all the time the way we live. We in this country have a love affair going on with fossil fuels. And we forget that fossil fuels are sunlight concentrated by millions of years underground by natural processes. We forget that when we burn a gallon of gasoline in our cars, it is the equivalent of 33.7 kilowatt-hours of ancient sunlight. In case it's been a little while since you were in a science class, a kilowatt-hour is one of the ways to measure energy, how it's transferred or converted. 33.7 kilowatt-hours is the amount of energy that is released by one gallon of gasoline when it's burned. And it's the same amount of energy that would be necessary to light a 100-watt light bulb for 14 days straight, 24 hours a day. I'm not suggesting that you should leave your 100-watt light bulbs on 24 hours a day, 14 days straight, but if you did, it would be the same amount of energy as in one gallon of gasoline. Fossil fuels sometimes might seem like shortcuts to energy that doesn't require solar power. But in fact, fossil fuels are concentrated solar power that has been stockpiled by the earth for millions of years. And it's just in the last 100 or 200 years that it has become possible for humans to harvest and release it in much shorter time frames.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
Throughout this season of creation, this worship series that we are doing in many of our United Methodist churches across the connection, we've come back to the book The Serviceberry by Robin Wall Kimmerer for more understandings and analogies to help us describe the natural world, and the ways that indigenous peoples have lived as interconnected, interdependent components in the systems of the natural world. In The Serviceberry, Kimmerer compares the flow of energy from sun to earth, powering all that happens here, and the flow of love among humans, sourced from God's love for us, to the flow of a breastfeeding mother's milk for a baby. Kimmerer points out that as a parent would never try to sell milk to their infant child. So we cannot try to sell the things that God provides for us. God's love or the sunlight. These are gifts so that life can continue. God's love is a gift given so that people can continue. Sunlight is a gift given so that the planet can continue. These are not commodities to be bought or sold. Kimmerer goes on to name some of the strategies that can help people to embody the interdependent ways of the natural world. In our reading for this morning, she pointed out that specialization avoids scarcity, and that diversity is an antidote to competition, and the difficulties or sometimes even violence that scarcity might inspire. So, we look to the diverse ways that people have developed to thrive on this planet, the many options that we have for finding the energy we need, converting it, or transferring it where we need it, knowing that originally, almost all of it came from the sun. Some of that diversity might include harnessing solar energy with passive solar designs, keeping the sun's warmth in where it's needed, and preventing it from entering structures where it would be too warm. Also, converting solar energy to electrical with a photovoltaic cell, creating plants that produce electrical energy through a hydroelectric dam where solar energy is pushing that water around the water cycle. Or allowing the solar energy to push the air through the turbines of a wind mill. There is a diversity of ways to locate the energy we need, and that diversity is an antidote to the scarcity that we sometimes imagine in our energy structures.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
Remembering Paul's point about the church, reminds us that even the weakest or least desirable of the components in an interconnected, interdependent, integrated energy policy is worthy of respect and consideration. Countries around the world, other than our own, are leading the way to this understanding. I'll share just a couple of examples. Pakistan has recently achieved 20% of its electrical generation from photovoltaic cells, mostly installed by individuals and communities of faith after devastating floods removed much of the infrastructure that they had relied on for their power before. And Chile, just this year, surpassed 70% of its power generation from renewable sources. Moving away from fossil fuels to more renewable energy sources is a way to honor the gifts that have been given freely and in abundance, to use them for the benefit of the interdependent web of life. Today we celebrate the gifts of the sun, freely given in abundance, enabling life to flourish here and near here and far from here. Ministry partners around this country including Third Act and Green Faith and 350, churches and denominations, synagogues and mosques, as well as many beyond the religious community have set aside this weekend to call attention to the potential for solar power for life to thrive on this planet in abundance. After worship today, I invite you to check out the displays in the narthex about the ways to turn the corner here toward more sustainable ways of life, and to honor this gift that has been freely given. I encourage you also to thank the members of your climate justice group who have made the displays out there possible.

**Rev. Laura Baumgartner:**
And beyond today, beyond the solar power displays that are here in our midst, I also encourage you to find ways to embody the principle of interdependence, empowered by the free-flowing gifts of sunlight and God's love in your life. Perhaps that will look like a trip to the beach, where you can be in God's presence, supported by the presence of God in the water, warmed by the presence of God in the air, noticing the ways that God's Spirit is moving in the changing brightness or cover of the skies. Perhaps it means sharing in a diversity of doing things, of ways of approaching challenges, knowing that diversity is an antidote to scarcity. Perhaps embodying interdependence looks like welcoming different understandings and perspectives, even following the leadership of those we might once have understood to be weaker or less respectable, rather than assuming that the ones who have power now also have the wisdom that is needed for the future. Whatever embodying interdependence looks like for you in this moment, we are at a time when we can no longer assume that the ways we've always done things will be fruitful today or tomorrow. Let us learn from the traditions and the ways of life that yield abundance. Abundant love, abundant grace, abundant joy, because we cannot thrive alone. As Kimmerer reminds us, all thriving is mutual. God has given us each other and has made us interdependent with those around us. May we know God's love in abundance, and may we be part of the systems that keep God's love moving through all of creation. Amen.
