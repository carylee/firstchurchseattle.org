## Sermon: Shouting Out, Calling From, Following Forward
**Preacher:** Rev. Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** Mark 10:46-52

Please join me in prayer.

May the words of my mouth and the meditations of all our hearts be acceptable not to each other, but to you, oh God, our rock and our redeemer. Amen.

This passage in Mark is one of the healing narratives found throughout the gospels. However, it's also important for us to name the harm that has been done to the disability community through certain interpretations of the healing narratives that have perpetuated ableism in our society. The Center for Disability Rights describes ableism as a set of beliefs or practices that devalue and discriminate against people with physical, intellectual, or psychiatric disabilities.

When we ignore someone or their disability, or if we somehow suggest someone is not complete unless they have been cured of their disability, we are devaluing another human being and practicing ableism. God has made humankind in the image and likeness of God, and that includes persons with physical, intellectual, and psychiatric disabilities. Theologian Sarah Melcher writes, "If human disability is an unsurprising aspect of being human, it could imply that God, too, shares that aspect of disability, since humanity represents the image of God. The possibility is implied that God shares in the experience of disability."

Theologian Nancy Eiesland puts this perspective for us, puts this in perspective for us when she describes how one day she had a life-changing epiphany as she visioned God's self using a sip-puff wheelchair. There is more to the story of Bartimaeus than just a healing narrative. It is also a call story as his faith led him to become a follower of Jesus. We can examine the story from all different facets to observe how it relates to our contemporary context, specifically to understanding our own vocational calling.

Often we might associate vocational calling only with ordained ministry. However, vocational calling is something that applies to all Christians. Most Christians are not called to ordained ministry, but we are all called, laity and clergy alike, to be ministers as we practice Christ's ministry of out-reaching love in all aspects of our lives. Discerning how we make a living or our careers and where we find meaning in what we do may be a large part of that, but there is so much more to our vocational calling. Our vocation is also relational, as we discern how we are called into our roles as spouses, parents, children, sibling, and friends. Further, we are also called into our relationships with our faith community, all of creation, and even the Earth.

Kathleen Cahalan, author of *The Stories We Live*, *Finding God's Calling All Around Us*, explains how important it is to understand vocation, not just as a noun, but also as a verb and as a preposition, because our vocations throughout our lives are fluid and dynamic, changing with us as we change. We also likely have multiple vocational callings at any given time through our many relationships. She describes vocation through eight prepositions: I am called **by** God, **to** follow, **as** I am, **from** grief, **for** service, **in** suffering, **through** others, **within** God.

And so let us examine how Bartimaeus found his vocational calling in this story to follow Jesus. The passage begins by describing Bartimaeus as a blind beggar. He recognizes that a large crowd is passing by him, and he hears that they are with Jesus. He begins to shout out to Jesus for mercy, reaching out to Jesus in an act of prayer. The process of discerning our vocational calling begins with prayer, asking Jesus for guidance, for strength, for wisdom, for courage, and mercy. We may find ourselves doing this especially through large transitions in our life, as we move into retirement or become a parent for the first time. When we graduate from college and begin searching for our first professional job. Or when a loved one has experienced a significant health challenge, and we find ourselves unexpectedly in a caregiving role. Or maybe you feel ready to reflect on your spiritual gifts and how to best use them in your faith community, as Barbara asked us to consider last week.

Whatever the reason you find yourself looking deeper into your different vocational callings, prayer is a great place to start. Whether we cry out in desperation, or pray silently for guidance amongst uncertainty, our Christian discipleship to follow Jesus begins internally through our spiritual relationship with God. "Take heart, get up. Jesus is calling you."

Jesus responds. Jesus heard Bartimaeus, even across all the noise, even as others tried to silence him and keep him down. Jesus did not come to Earth to make the rich more wealthy or the powerful more powerful. Jesus came to care for the poor, to bring voice to those that have been pushed to the margins, and to teach us how to follow in his example. Jesus shows us this in this text, that no one is to be silenced and that no one is to be considered less than anyone else. When Jesus heard Bartimaeus shouting out, he stopped and stood still. Jesus responds by calling Bartimaeus from the margins, reminding him that he too is a child of God, worthy of God's unconditional love, the same as everyone else. Jesus calls Bartimaeus to a new place. Bartimaeus can leave behind his life as a beggar in the margins and be led to something new.

As we seek guidance from Jesus on how to live out our vocational calling in different seasons in aspects of our lives, we could know that Jesus desires to lead us to something new that provides a flourishing for all of creation. Sometimes this involves teaching us how to recognize where oppression exists in our world and how to end it. The disciples needed to learn this important lesson as well. "Take heart, get up, Jesus is calling you."

The disciples learn. The disciples and the others in the crowd didn't consider Bartimaeus worthy enough to speak to Jesus. They continued to push Bartimaeus to the margins by participating in the ongoing oppression of the disability community in their society. They failed to understand the true message and teachings of Jesus. However, Jesus did not give up on the disciples. He engaged them in the work of justice. Instead of calling up Bartimaeus directly, Jesus asked the disciples to bring Bartimaeus to him. The disciples went to Bartimaeus and said that phrase, "Take heart, get up, he is calling you." They learned of the true hope that Jesus brings to the oppressed, and that it is wrong to silence anyone.

This was part of recognizing their own vocational calling. Sometimes it takes action to recognize the ways that we are called. The disciple showed us how the oppressors can learn how to do better through a willingness to recognize where we have been wrong and have done harm before. This past weekend, clergy from across the conference, including myself and Pastor Wongee, gathered with leaders of the organization Mending Wings on the Yakama Reservation. Mending Wings is a native-run organization that seeks to mend the wings of their youth so that they can fly again. We became active listeners as we heard the personal stories shared by leaders and young adults of the Yakama Tribes, as they described to us the harm done by us, Westerners and Methodists. Harm that wasn't that far in the distant past. We heard difficult stories of violence, wars, and lies. We learned how zero treaties between the U.S. and Indigenous nations across the country have been kept. And we listened as they shared how the Methodist boarding schools in Yakama created lasting generational trauma for so many families. This is just the first step in recognizing how we have participated as oppressors of Indigenous peoples. We hope to bring a team of youth and adults to one of their students learning about missions events next summer to continue understanding the harm done, and to contribute to building a reconciling relationship between Methodists and the Yakama Tribes.

Part of seeking our vocational calling is doing the hard work of examining our own privileges in society and recognizing the ways they continue to hold others in oppression through acts of discrimination, racism, and colonialization, including all of creation and the Earth, not just human beings. "Take heart, get up, Jesus is calling you."

Bartimaeus follows. When the disciples went and told Bartimaeus that Jesus called him, he jumped up and threw off his cloak. This was a significant piece of clothing in this biblical time because it identified him as a beggar. But even before he met Jesus, he discarded that cloak without missing a beat, as he had faith that Jesus was calling him to something else. This text is the last event of Jesus's public ministry in the Gospel of Mark, and provides a transition from a large section about discipleship to Jesus's trial, death, and resurrection. The last line of this passage says that Bartimaeus followed him on the way. As he joins with the disciples and the crowd following Jesus into Jerusalem, he shows us what it means to be a true Jesus follower.

Have you felt the ways that Jesus has called you into something new or different, whether it is in your career or in your relationships? Are you ready to be bold like Bartimaeus, and throwing off your cloak and choosing to follow in the ways and steps of Jesus? "Take heart, get up, Jesus is calling you."

And so I ask you, where do you see yourself in this story? Are there parts of Bartimaeus's story that resonate with you? Perhaps you have been in prayer with Jesus seeking a change in some aspect of your life or clarity in your own vocational calling. Or maybe you have felt marginalized in your communities and are shouting out just to be heard. Maybe you see yourself in the disciples and the crowd that participated in ongoing oppression, but with a desire to learn how to do better, to truly follow Jesus. Likely, it's a little bit of both.

Our larger connection across the United Methodist Church has many ways to commit more intentionally to how Jesus calls you to connect your faith with your church, your world, and with one another. Some of these programs include the Global Ministries Earthkeepers that provides collaborative space for developing environmental projects in your communities, incorporating topics of ecotheology, anti-racism, and community organizing. Or United Methodist women, now United Women in Faith, Deaconess and Home Missioner programs for lay people that have felt called by God to engage with a full-time vocation in ministries of love, justice and service. Or the Board of Discipleship's Lay Servant Ministries programs to develop, prepare and engage principled Christian leaders for the mission of making disciples for the transformation of the world, including certified lay servant, certified lay speaker, and certified lay minister programs. Or Global Ministries missionaries, global mission fellows, and individual mission volunteer programs that serve in partnership organizations and communities around the world anywhere from two months to three years or more. This is not an exhaustive list, but are some ways to engage and set apart existing programs that provide focus for you on some aspect of your vocational calling to be a follower of Jesus.

And while this sermon isn't specifically about ordained ministry, if that is an area you would like to discern, Pastor Wongee, an ordained elder called to a lifetime of ministry in word, sacrament, order and service, and myself, a provisional deacon, called to a lifetime ministry of word, service, compassion and justice would be more than happy to talk with you. As we saw in the passage, just as Jesus engaged the disciples to help Bartimaeus respond to his call, so too does Jesus engage each of us to help one another discern our vocational callings. And so I invite you, open your hearts to receive where God is calling you next. Not just on the work you do to make a living, but also in the way that you show up in your job. For instance, perhaps you feel called to start a new diversity, equity, and inclusion program at your company, to encourage your colleagues to examine their privileges and to work to eliminate discrimination and racism in your field. And then also discern how God is calling you in all of your relationships of your daily lives, and in your faith community, and through all the ways you're asked to live in the footsteps of Jesus out in the world. Go to Jesus in prayer, shouting out what you are searching for, consider where Jesus is calling you from. Will you be ready to follow Jesus forward into what is next? "Take heart, get up. Jesus is calling you." All glory be to God. Amen.
