### Sermon: The Promise of Blessing
**Preacher:** Rev. Elizabeth Ingram Schindler
**Scripture:** Matthew 5:1-20

**Pastor Elizabeth:**
Let us pray.

Oh God, may the words of my mouth and the meditations of all of our hearts be found pleasing in your sight. For you are our rock and our redeemer.

Amen.

I have a love-hate relationship with the word blessed.

I mean, on the one hand, I want to be blessed. I want to bless other people. One of my favorite things to do as a pastor is to offer someone a blessing. Plus, I am from the Deep South. So, I like to say, "Bless your heart." As a way of absolving myself from being judgy when I say something about someone that could be construed as unkind, like, for example, "She's not the sharpest tool in the shed, bless her heart." See, doesn't that sound caring and compassionate? Bless her.

And also, Jesus is obviously into blessing people. So, if I want to be like Jesus, then blessing is going to be a part of it. But I also think that we've gotten kind of lazy with our use of the word, and we often use it incorrectly. And that's not just a matter of semantics. It actually leads to some really problematic understandings of who God is and how God works in the world.

A few years ago, the church I was serving at the time, um, at that church, I did a month-long stewardship series called #blessed. And the uh, intention was that it would help people to look at what it means to bless, to be blessed and to bless others, and what it does not mean. And I suggested then, and I still believe that almost everything we might see like on social media with the hashtag #blessed, is probably not a blessing at all. It's usually an achievement. Uh, and to call it a blessing is just kind of a humble brag. For example, someone might write, um, "Our son got into his first choice college, #blessed." Or, "Sunset view from our new vacation home, #blessed."

But those things aren't blessings. Achievements? Sure. Nice things? Absolutely. But to call those blessings is to suggest that God gave them to you. That God was intentional about putting you in a new vacation home while someone else sleeps on the street. That God micromanaged your kid's college applications, but not the kid next door who prayed just as hard.

Too often, when we look at success or victory or wealth, and we call it a blessing, if we're honest, we might have to admit that it's actually greed or gluttony, or we might have stepped on someone else on the way to the top. And if we look at what Jesus says about blessing from our reading today in Matthew 5, it doesn't match up with our social media feeds at all.

So, I think we need to be careful when we say that we are blessed, or when we ask God for a blessing for someone else. Even if it is, say, our favorite football team, just for a random example. And we need to make sure that our understanding of blessing matches what Jesus means when he offers one.

So, let's look more closely at this set of blessings that we have in Matthew 5, uh, known as the Beatitudes. And this is the very beginning of what we know as the Sermon on the Mount. And Matthew didn't call it that. Jesus didn't call it that. Somebody else decided much later it's called the Sermon on the Mount. But that's probably how you know it. And to this point in Matthew's Gospel, the story has been very exciting. We've had Jesus's genealogy and birth and the visit of the Magi, and then he's had to flee Egypt to escape Herod. Uh, we've had the preaching of John the Baptist in the wilderness and Jesus's baptism, and then he goes straight into the wilderness to do battle with the devil, which we're going to hear about in a couple of weeks, uh, at the start of the season of Lent. So, it's been super exciting story. And then we get to Chapter 5, and Matthew tells us that Jesus goes up on a mountainside to teach the disciples and anyone else who's close enough to hear, and we call it a sermon, uh, which might honestly make you want to skip over this part and get back to the action, uh, especially when you see that it's three chapters long. But we're going to look at it anyway.

And it's interesting to me that this is basically Jesus's inaugural address. He hasn't said anything to the people to this point. It's the very beginning of his ministry, like his coming out sermon, his convention speech. It's the first time we hear from him. And so, we might expect him to start with his platform, with an explanation of what he's going to do, or what his life will be about. He might lay out his core values, or what kind of administration he plans to have. Or he could get in line with Moses, who went up on a similar mountainside, and he could begin his teaching with the very basics of how we are to live, like an update on the Ten Commandments. But instead, he starts with these sweet words that we have likely seen cross-stitched onto a pillow.

He says, "Blessed are the poor in spirit. Blessed are they who mourn. Blessed are the meek. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness. Blessed are the merciful. Blessed are the pure in heart. Blessed are the peacemakers. Blessed are those who are persecuted because of righteousness. Blessed you." See what I did there? That was perfect placement. "Blessed are you when people insult you, persecute you, and falsely say all kinds of evil against you because of me. Blessed are you."

And we might begin to wonder by the end of this list what Jesus is really talking about. Because for one thing, these are not the people that we would normally describe as blessed. There are no new vacation homes in this list. But we also might wonder whether Jesus, by naming these people, is asking us to try to be all of these things, so that we too can reap the rewards that he names in the Beatitudes. In other words, we might wonder whether these blessings are meant to be prescriptive for us.

Or, we might wonder if Jesus has already seen all of these people in the very short time he's been in ministry so far. Maybe he's already seen the meek and the merciful and the peacemakers, and now he's simply telling the crowd that these people are indeed blessed, that they are favored by God, which would make the Beatitudes descriptive instead of prescriptive. He's just naming what is.

But there might also be a third option. And so, I wonder if these blessings might actually be, not prescriptive or descriptive, but performative. Meaning, when Jesus says blessed are you, maybe something actually happens. Maybe something changes because of his words, which would make a ton of sense, because this is the same God that literally spoke the world into being. His words have power.

So, maybe when he says, "Blessed are you," something changes and the people are blessed. But what even does that mean?

The English word blessing comes to us from an Old English word, which means to sanctify or consecrate with blood. We don't usually think about that, right? When we say, "Bless you." We generally think of a blessing as kind of a vaguely positive abstract thing. But the original meaning had to do with the very force of life, which is blood. So, to bless someone then was to invoke divine favor on them and connect them to the source of life, so that they could live.

Put differently, but I think related, a professor once told me that the best way to pray for someone, if you don't know the right words, is to hold them gently in God's gaze, so that God might notice and care for them. And that's an image of one meaning of blessing, entrusting to God's care, holding in God's light, connecting to the source of all life.

Usually, when we offer a blessing to someone else, it comes in a pretty standard form. We say, "Bless you," when someone sneezes, by which we usually mean, "I wish you well," or, "I hope you're okay," or even just, "I notice you, there, sneezing." We may not mean anything at all when we say, "Bless you." Most of us don't intend to really bless people, to connect them to God in this way. Or sometimes, we might think of blessing as adding a little bit of holiness to someone or something, to make them a little more sacred.

But one of my favorite preachers and authors, Barbara Brown Taylor, reminds us that we never need to confer holiness on another person or thing, because it's already holy simply because God made it. You are already holy before anyone has blessed you. This place is already holy before we've said the first prayer in it. The trees outside are already holy. We don't need to designate them as such, or say a special litany to make them so. So, Taylor says that offering a blessing isn't giving holiness to something. It's simply a way of seeing. It's recognizing the holiness that's already there. Paying attention to a person or an object, so that we see it as it is, and pronounce it good, rather than simply passing it by, or judging how useful it might be to us. We don't make things holy when we bless them. We just notice that they already are.

So, in that sense, the Beatitudes are simply a way for Jesus to show us God's presence in places we might not usually look for it. He looks at the poor in spirit and the meek and says, "There is holiness. These people are beloved. I'm with them." And we might prefer power and wealth, but Jesus says, "Look, there's my image in the persecuted one, in the hungry for righteousness, in the peacemaker." It's like Jesus is saying to his followers, right at the beginning of this ministry, when he hasn't taught them anything else yet. He says, "I know this is going to be hard. I know that you'll probably take some hard knocks because of me. I know that it looks like you're on the losing side when you turn the other cheek and pray for your enemy. But you're blessed. Holy. Beloved. And I'm with you. And what's more, there's hope for you, because the kingdom is coming, the new kingdom, that comes with the person and work of Jesus, and you're already part of it."

It's like he's saying, "These days might be really hard, but I'm holding you up in the gaze of God. I'm naming you as beloved right here, and just by speaking, in the naming, your belovedness becomes even fuller, richer, deeper. In the blessing, you're drawn even deeper into the heart of God because you begin to see it for yourself."

And that's the promise of the Beatitudes. That whether you identify with the poor in spirit or the mourning, with the hungry for righteousness or the peacemaker, Jesus sees you and names you as blessed. Whether are you are named by this culture as worthy and powerful or unworthy and powerless, Jesus sees you and names you as blessed. He holds us all in the light of God, and invites us into the kingdom, where we will see each other's holiness right on the surface. We won't have to search for it. Where we can readily name each other as beloved.

But, and this is important to hear, the Sermon on the Mount doesn't end with the blessing. It just starts there. The very next thing that Jesus says is, "You are the salt of the Earth, and you are the light of the world." Not you should be salt and light, or you could be, or would you please try harder to be, but you are. It's in your identity. It's who you already are. And it's not just for your own sake. You aren't blessed just because it's nice for you. Though it is nice. You're actually blessed for the sake of the world. You're called to share that blessing with others. In our belovedness, we're called to share the light, to take care of the Earth, to witness to this new kingdom that's already on the way.

God will always love us just as we are. That is true. God will always see the holiness in us and try to point it out for us. We will always be blessed, and praise God for that. But we can't get too comfortable in the blessing. We can't stop just knowing we are loved, because Jesus has work for us to do. And it's the transformation of the whole world.

Amen.
