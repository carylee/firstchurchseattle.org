**Preacher:** Rev. Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** Ecclesiastes 3:1-13; Matthew 2:13-23

Before we move into our time of sermon, we received word early this morning from Judy McNeal that Bradley Sharf has passed away.
I remember Bradley fondly as the first person that I met that whose hand I shook as I walked through the doors of First Church at our old building at Fifth and Marion, over 20 years ago.

Amen.
Let's imagine together for a minute.
We see two young parents, swiftly and quietly packing up their meager belongings as their young child sleeps soundly in the corner of the room.
They have just experienced the most joyous celebration as excited visitors came to greet their new child with beautiful gifts.
It wasn't too long ago that they had already made a difficult journey, trying to find a place to give birth in an unfamiliar city.
And now they find they must ready themselves again for further arduous travel.
This time with a sense of urgency for the safety of their child.
They grow weary thinking about the rough terrain they will need to cross on foot, the hills and valleys they must climb, and the challenges of the climate.
How will their young child handle it?
Will they be able to find water when they need it, a safe place to camp for the night?
Will they have enough food?
Will they make it to the border in time to escape the cruel hands of King Herod?
Herod's jurisdiction ended at the Egyptian border about 40 miles away from Bethlehem.
But this likely wasn't the Holy Family's final destination.
We're unsure of where they ended up in Egypt, but one possibility was the Nile River Delta area, anywhere between 400 to 600 miles away.
Egypt was governed by the Roman Empire during this time.
The city of Alexandria, on the western part of the River Delta, became the Greek capital of Egypt in 331 BC.
It had a large Jewish presence as many Jews headed to the city to escape Babylonian captivity, starting all the way back in the sixth century BC.
A journey to this area could have taken the Holy Family several weeks, especially considering they were traveling with a very young Jesus.
The story of the Holy Family's journey reminds me of Abraham's travel story.
In Genesis chapter 12, Abram, who would later be called Abraham, set out on his own journey of unknowns as God said to him, "Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you."
It was Abraham's deep faith commitment that prompted his obedience.
Leaving most everything except his wife Sarai, who would later be called Sarah, his nephew Lot, and some possessions, he headed for a place he did not know, uncertain of what he would experience on the way there, he put all his trust in God.
Mary and Joseph did the same, not once, but twice.
God spoke to them too through an angel that appeared to Joseph with a warning, saying, "Get up, take the child and his mother and flee to Egypt and remain there until I tell you."
And so they left, not knowing what the journey would be like or how long they would stay, but going because of their faith in God.
Still in Bethlehem at this time, I wonder if they were expecting to travel back to Nazareth after the census was complete.
Instead, they had to leave their community behind, everything familiar, and the possibility of seeing their family anytime soon.
In this passage we see how the Holy, how the Holy Family became refugees as they were forced to emigrate to another country to flee the violence King Herod was about to unleash.
Several years later after Herod died, it was time for Mary, Joseph, and Jesus to journey back to Israel.
The angel of the Lord again appeared to Joseph, this time saying, "Get up, take the child and his mother and go to the land of Israel, for those who are seeking the child's life are dead."
And so they went, following God's instructions once again.
Initially, headed towards Judah, they made an adjustment in their route mid-course in response to a new concern.
After Joseph received further wisdom in a dream.
Instead, they went to Nazareth in Galilee where they finally made their home.
Mary and Joseph lived through a transitional space, a liminal space, throughout much of this time, showing us what it means to allow ourselves to be led by our faith, pivoting as needed when plans change and when the unforeseen occurs.
When we trust in God, we adapt and we adjust as needed, not without concern, unease or worry, but knowing too that God moves alongside us through it all.
Sometimes we are called to follow God into the unknown, experiencing that transitional liminal space in different seasons of our own lives.
And as a congregation, we are currently in that space now, journeying into the unknown together, after having just said goodbye to Pastor Wanji this past week, as she takes renewal leave, and as we get ready to welcome our new interim pastors, Reverend Elizabeth Schindler and Reverend Kathy Morse, for the next six months.
And as we wait to hear who will be with us after that.
It can be an anxious and sometimes unsettling space to face uncertainty, but we can trust that God will help us guide us through these changes and lead us into new possibilities that we might not have ever imagined.
One thing is certain, change is constant.
We all know that.
And we all experience many different periods of transitions and uncertainties throughout our lives.
Perhaps maybe our health, our job situations, our families, our financial concerns, or our states of mind.
Or maybe we hold concern for what will happen next in our society or to our planet.
Just like Abraham, Mary, and Joseph, we have the opportunity to follow God into that void of unknowns, and trust that we will end up moving towards what God desires and intends for us and for our world.
The story of Abraham's journey is so foundational, not only to the Holy Family's migration story, but also to our lives as contemporary Christians.
It sets in place an important understanding of covenant between God and God's faithful followers.
A covenant is like a promise.
God's promise with us, and our promise with God, binding us together as we commit to being in relationship with God.
God's promise to Abraham was that he would become the ancestor of a multitude of nations, at which time God changed his name to Abraham, meaning, "Father of many nations."
God made several covenants in the Old Testament, with Noah after the great flood, with Moses on Mount Sinai, and with King David.
In the New Testament, Christians recognize a new covenant, which is the covenant of the saving work of Jesus Christ through the grace of God, and the response Christians make in their profession of faith and baptism.
In our worship service today, we will have the opportunity to participate in the Wesley Covenant Prayer together, as a way to recommit ourselves to our faith, remembering the obligations we have made to God to be followers of Jesus.
The Wesley Covenant Prayer is often part of a much larger Covenant Renewal Service.
The first of which John Wesley, the founder of Methodism, held on Monday, August 11th of 1755.
Its roots date back even further, to Puritan Richard Alene's 1663 book, "A Vindication of Godliness in the Greater Strictness and Spirituality of It."
Wesley often conducted the service whenever he visited Methodist societies throughout England.
But traditionally, in London, these services were held on New Year's Day.
The heart of the service is the covenant prayer, a version of which is included in your bulletin, and that you are encouraged to sign and take with you as you renew your own commitment to your faith a little bit later today.
In United Methodism today, many churches incorporate the Wesley Covenant Service around the New Year.
And may hold what's called a Watch Night Service.
A typical Watch Night Service may be about three hours long with scripture readings and hymn singing, but don't worry.
I promise we won't be here that long today.
Watch Night also holds holds much significance in the African American tradition, which also refers to it as Freedom's Eve, since the Emancipation Proclamation went into effect on January 1st, 1863, finally freeing the enslaved people in our country.
Haiti too celebrates obtaining their independence on January 1st, 1804, after a nation of slaves revolted against their colonizers and eventually gained their freedom.
Watch Night speaks then to freedom, to hope, to liberation.
It's a time to consider what God might have in store for us this next year.
Our Ecclesiastes text today is often part of Watch Night Services, giving us the opportunity to reflect on how our lives always involve change, and how God's love and grace are always present with us in those transitions.
For Wesley, a commitment to our faith involves both works of personal piety and works of mercy.
Personal piety includes acts of devotion and worship, including prayer, studying scripture, attending worship consistently, and receiving communion regularly, helping us develop a personal relationship with God.
Works of mercy are the ways we relate to the world around us.
It includes acts of compassion and justice, like visiting someone that is sick, providing food to someone that is hungry, and working for change in our world to to decrease harm occurring to persons and creatures suffering under our unjust systems.
While we listen to the words of the Wesley Covenant Prayer today, think about the ways God is speaking to you, that you haven't explored before.
What is God intending and desiring for you, and how can you renew your commitment to your faith this new year?
Part of listening to what God intends for us, means understanding the shortcomings of our current way of life as a collective people.
Therefore, we shouldn't ignore the harsh warning that we hear also in today's Gospel reading.
Mary and Joseph needed to flee Bethlehem because King Herod ordered a massacre of the innocents, commanding that all children two years of age or younger, in and around Bethlehem, be killed, all because he feared that one of them, little baby Jesus, would one day become a threat to his power.
This is difficult to read in scripture.
And while it seems outrageous to consider that any political leader today would do the same, we must not lose sight of all the children in our contemporary world that are being harmed, who are dying, because of structural violence and terrible injustices taking place.
We must mourn this great loss, weep as Rachel wept, and seek to do better.
Consider, for instance, all of the children in war-torn areas like Palestine, Ukraine, Sudan, and more.
Those children that have lost their lives, or have lost parents and family members to take care of them.
Consider the children facing hunger across the world, and the children living in unsafe and unsanitary conditions, without the means to be treated for simple illnesses, let alone complex conditions.
UNICEF, the United Nations Children's Fund, describes how over one billion, one billion, children worldwide experience some type of emotional, physical, or sexual violence each year, with tens of thousands dying as a result of conflict and other humanitarian emergencies.
For instance, they say, over 400 million children live in countries affected by violent conflict.
Over 150 children, 150 million children, are forced into dangerous child labor conditions.
And climate disasters like severe drought are causing an increase in the amount of young girls who are at risk of being forced into child marriage.
In our own country, one of the wealthiest nations on the planet, 14 million children face food insecurity, according to Feeding America.
Hearing how many children are experiencing a similar fate as those children did under the rule of King Herod, we can recognize that seeking transformation for our world is not only important.
It is necessary and urgent.
As we consider what commitments we will make in our faith this year, let us remember that every single commitment makes a difference.
The Christmas Tide season, those 12 days between Christmas and Epiphany, can often feel like their own liminal space.
We're worn down from the hustle and bustle leading up to Christmas.
We seek rest from all the gatherings and celebrations.
We start to feel anxious about busy schedules restarting soon in the new year.
And we wonder, what's next?
Friends, as we journey together in these next few months, in the midst of transition, let us not be fearful, afraid of any challenges ahead of us.
Instead, let us embrace change by trusting in God, remembering that the tasks in front of us for our world remain the same.
Let us consider this tiny, beautiful little baby that God provided to this hurting world.
Whose cosmic Divine love holds the power to topple fear, intimidation, and violent ways.
Let us recommit ourselves today to our faith, through works of personal piety, mercy, compassion, and justice for our world, remembering that the birth of Christ brings us hope for a brighter future, and that we too can be a part of making that transformation happen.
And so I leave you with these words of Howard Thurman's well-known poem, The Work of Christmas, to consider the tasks at hand as we commit to being servants of Jesus Christ.
When the song of the angels is stilled, when the star in the sky is gone, when the kings and princes are home, when the shepherds are back with their flock, the work of Christmas begins.
To find the lost, to heal the broken, to feed the hungry, to release the prisoner, to rebuild the nations, to be to bring peace among others, to make music in the heart.
All glory be to God.
