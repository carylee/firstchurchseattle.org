## Sermon: My Flesh
**Preacher:** Rev. Wongee Joh
**Scripture:** John 6:35, 41-51 (NRSVUE)

[00:19:15]
Several years ago, our church launched a spiritual practices series. It was a 10-week program where participants could explore different ways of connecting to God and building spiritual resilience. The series covered practices like meditation, prayer, contemplative silence, and many others. It was an opportunity to deepen our faith and strengthen our spiritual journey.

In one of the workshops during the series, we explored what is called "body prayer." This practice involves using our physical bodies, including gestures and postures, to express our praise, devotion, and gratitude to God. It was a new experience for many of us, and I remember feeling a bit awkward at first.

I mean, how often do we use our bodies in worship beyond sitting or standing or kneeling? The leader of the workshop encouraged us to be open-minded and to let go of any inhibitions. We started with simple movements, like raising our hands in praise or bowing in humility, and as we moved and prayed, a sense of liberation began to fill the room. The initial awkwardness slowly gave way to a profound sense of connection and joy.

For me, body prayer was a powerful reminder of the deep connection between our physical and spiritual selves. It challenged my preconceived notions of what prayer should look like and opened up new avenues for expressing my faith.

This experience got me thinking about the idea of the body in worship and how often it's overlooked or even dismissed. In many spiritual traditions, there's often a tendency to prioritize the mind or the spirit over the body, as if the body were something separate or even an impediment to spiritual growth. But what if we saw the body as an integral part of our spiritual journey, a vessel through which we can experience and express our faith more fully?

In today's Gospel reading, Jesus declares, "I am the bread of life. Whoever comes to me will never be hungry, and whoever believes in me will never be thirsty." These words are an invitation to a deeper understanding of what it means to be nourished by God, not just spiritually, but holistically.

The Jewish people in John's Gospel found Jesus's words scandalous. "How can this man give us his flesh to eat?" they grumbled. To them, the idea of eating flesh was not only repulsive but also went against their dietary laws. They understood Jesus's words in a literal, physical sense, and they were horrified.

But Jesus's statement was not merely about physical consumption. He was speaking metaphorically, inviting them to a deeper understanding of his identity and his mission. He was the bread of life, the source of eternal nourishment. He was revealing his deepest self to them, offering his very essence as sustenance for their souls.

This narrative holds a powerful truth for us today. The human body is often misunderstood, mistreated, and even despised, and this has roots in early Christianity. One of the earliest challenges facing the church was Gnosticism, a movement that taught that the material world, including the human body, was inherently evil and that salvation could only be achieved through secret knowledge.

To combat this heresy, early Christian leaders emphasized the goodness of creation and the importance of the Incarnation, the belief that God became human in the person of Jesus Christ. The Nicene Creed, a foundational statement of Christian faith, affirms this truth by declaring, "We believe in one God, the Father Almighty, Maker of heaven and earth, and of all things visible and invisible." This includes our bodies.

The early church also celebrated sacraments like baptism and communion, which use physical elements like water, bread, and wine to convey spiritual truths. These rituals remind us that God is not only present in the spiritual realm but also in the material world, inviting us to encounter the sacred through our physical senses.

Even today, we find ourselves grappling with the same questions about the body. We live in a society that often promotes unrealistic beauty standards, leading to body image issues and self-esteem struggles. The media bombards us with messages that tell us our bodies are not good enough, that we need to change them, enhance them, or hide them. We are constantly pressured to conform to an ideal that is often unattainable, leaving us feeling inadequate and ashamed.

Moreover, our bodies are often subjected to judgment and discrimination based on race, gender, ability, and other factors. People of color, LGBTQ individuals, and those with disabilities often face systemic oppression and violence that targets their bodies. These experiences can lead to deep trauma and a sense of alienation from their own bodies.

In the face of these challenges, Jesus's declaration, "I am the bread of life. The bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh," becomes a radical statement of affirmation and liberation. It reminds us that our bodies are not commodities to be judged or manipulated, but sacred vessels through which we experience life and connect with God.

In his book *Body and Soul: A Meditation on the Christian Life*, the theologian Miroslav Volf writes, "The body is the site of our deepest yearnings, our most profound joys, and our most excruciating pains. It is the place where God meets us, where Christ is born, dies, and is raised in us." Volf's words remind us that our bodies are not mere containers for our souls, but integral parts of our spiritual journey. They are sites of transformation, where God's grace can touch us and empower us to live more fully.

So how can we reclaim the sacredness of our bodies and integrate them more fully into our spiritual lives?

First, we can practice embodied prayer. This means engaging our physical senses in worship, whether through movement, breathwork, or even simply being present in our bodies. As in the body prayer workshop, we can lift our hands in praise, bow in reverence, or dance in celebration. These practices help us to connect with God in a deeper, more holistic way, reminding us that our bodies are an integral part of our spiritual journey.

Second, we can challenge societal norms that demean or devalue certain bodies. This means actively resisting the pressure to conform to unrealistic beauty standards and embracing our bodies in all their diversity. We can celebrate our unique shapes, sizes, and abilities, recognizing that each body is a unique expression of God's creative love. By doing so, we not only liberate ourselves but also create a more inclusive and affirming community for all.

Third, we can advocate for justice and healing for all bodies. This means speaking out against discrimination and violence that targets certain bodies, and working towards a world where all bodies are treated with dignity and respect. It means creating safe and inclusive spaces where everyone feels valued and affirmed, regardless of their physical appearance or abilities.

Friends, the bread of life is not just a metaphor. It is a profound truth that invites us to embrace our bodies as sacred vessels, temples of the Holy Spirit. It calls us to live in a way that honors our bodies, celebrates our diversity, and advocates for justice for all bodies. May we, like Jesus, become bread for the world, offering our flesh, our lives, our very beings, as a gift of love and sustenance for all.

Amen.
