## Sermon: Luminescent Presence
[06:01]
**Rev. Wongee Joh:**
If you ask your biological or adoptive or foster, or those who had a parenting role in your life, they may be able to share with you some details of your birth. I'm not saying that you have a perfect birth story, but there is a story.

Jesus's birth story was not perfect with a perfect baby shower, a perfect wedding before the birth, or with parents of the right social, economic or political standing, or what not. Jesus was not born with best medical care or even in a hospital. Even those who were present were not respectable, like dotting grandparents or surrounded by families and friends or siblings.

Our Christmas cards of the nativity scene can hardly do justice to convey the reality and the mystery of the incarnation, word made flesh, God coming to us as human in our most vulnerable human state as a baby. No one these days gasps at the nativity story for what it actually reveals. It is a scandal that God comes as a vulnerable newborn baby, born a foreigner and a child refugee fleeing political dictators of his day.

Mary and Joseph did not travel for vacation as we often do in our holidays and culture of Christmas time. It's so easy to forget those who travel without choice and are actually fleeing literally for their lives. We are told the Holy family traveled to Bethlehem to be documented for census registration for tax purposes. Nothing merry and bright about that, no singing fa la las all the way. The holy family had no choice but to obey the oppressive rulers of their day. Rulers that were capable of infanticide who ordered babies to death out of fear. Those were the realities of the historical time into which Jesus was born and still even today, infants are born into war and into the atrocities of genocide.

We are told that there were some shepherds present, and shepherds in those days were socially more comparable to our day laborers and migrant workers. The Gospel of Luke is clear that the good news is for the poor, the oppressed, the marginalized, and that is captured in our readings tonight. So how do we listen to the good news with our 21st century privileges? How are we poor, oppressed, in the margins, spiritually, materially, intellectually, physically, emotionally? Are we in need? With what need of good news do we come tonight? Are we and our world in need of the Light of the World, or in another expression, luminescent presence? We can experience luminescent presence when we are accepting that we are people then and now sitting in bleakness.

None of us sits alone. We are indeed in need of love incarnate into our intersectional realities. We are not disconnected from each other and the happenings around the world or in our next door neighbors. The angels in tonight's story do not appear to the powerful kings and people of the day, but to the shepherds watching over their flock in their ordinary work. That is what makes the incarnation extraordinary that God would come to the ordinariness of our lives, into our humanity as is. As it is with our brokenness, our illnesses, our diseases, our abuse of power and war, our broken relationships, our addictions and lostness, us may be far from home without refuge. Into this messy and complicated world, God comes to dwell, to make a home, is born in human form as one of us. That is the good news that God would love us as we are. God would love the whole cosmos and our interconnectedness.

Through Advent, we have arrived into the Holy of Holy nights, to the birth of Jesus, to receive the gift of God into our hearts, our homes, our lives, our world. German theologian Martin Luther reminded us that the angels say, "To you, to you is born this day." And in the context of the historical story, those willing to listen were the lowly shepherds in the field. They heard the good news. So to us listeners tonight in 2024, on the Eve of 2025 breaking upon us, the same good news is spoken, "To you, to you is born this day."

Martin Luther reminds us that quote, "What good would it do me if Jesus were born a thousand times and if this were sung to me every day with the loveliest of airs, if I should not hear that there was something in it for me, and that it should be my own." To you, turn to one another. Say, "To you." To you, my friends. To you, a stranger. To you, visiting. To you who come to Christmas Eve, searching on this one holy night, is born a Savior. Into our ordinary and less than perfect lives, to us, are we in need of a savior? Light in the world, luminescent presence, with and for us, the good news is for you. Yes. To me. Yes. Yes, for the cosmos. It is both uniquely particular, you the scripture says, and it is universally cosmic, salvation for the cosmos.

Let me conclude with a different story, probably very familiar to some on Christmas Eve. It has become part of our traditions as we sing Silent Night tonight and light our candles, and it reminds us this tradition, that it was into a less than perfect night that people gathered like us. The story says that a few days before Christmas Eve in 1818, in a church called St. Nicholas in Oberndorf, Austria, the organist informed the pastor that the aging organ was broken, and could not be fixed by midnight service. The organist said he could play the guitar, I guess guitar was a less than the organ, that night if the pastor thought that was appropriate. The pastor agreed to the guitar and that he had written a simple poem and the musician might set it to music. So that night for the first time in Germany, Silent Night was sung. It was probably not noticed by many that night. We sing it here tonight. The birth of Jesus was unnoticed by money in Jesus's time. New things being done was not noticed in Germany that night. Nor did many notice other than the shepherds, who listened, who listened to the good news that said it was for you, for them, for us.

The hymn captures the spirit of this night. What first time? What new thing? What birth is happening for you tonight? To you, yes, you, the gift of luminescent presence, this gift of love is for you. This gift. And as we pass the light to one another, a reminder that we are called to pass and share our light, our ordinary light, our ordinary love, our everyday acts of service, ourselves and perfect as it may be, is our gift. We are called to rejoice and share and pass on. As we light our candles, let us bless one another in our world with these words by the prophet and contemplative theologian Howard Thurman. Quote, "I will light candles this Christmas. Candles of joy, despite all the sadness. Candles of hope where despair keeps watch. Candles of peace for tempest tossed days. Candles of grace to ease heavy burdens. Candles of love to inspire all my living. Candles that will burn all the year long." May the love of God, luminescent presence, be born in you and me, in us, in our cosmos, anew, and again, and may it be God's gift in the bleakness, the gift of Christ, the gift of one another. Amen. May it be so.
