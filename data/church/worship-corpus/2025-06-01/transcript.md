## Sermon: The Power of Love
**Preacher:** Rev. Wongee Joh
**Scripture:** John 17:20-23, John 17:23-26

It's hard to believe it's almost a year. Pride month is finally here. Last time I was here was actually in one of our pictures in the video today. Ben and I are standing here, um, with our hands stretched out for the blessing for the Pride month. I cannot believe it's been a year. Um, today we have a special year-end celebrations where, which we're doing incorporated into the worship service because not everybody stays for the Church Conference, um, after the service and luncheon today. Everyone is invited, but we wanted to share it for folks who cannot stay after worship service. So as doing my pastor Dory, I will offer my shortest sermon of the year. It will be more linear and three-point.

Um, what time is it? It's Pride month. It's year-end Ministries and we are at the last Sunday of Eastertide. Next Sunday is Pentecost where we will celebrate baptisms and becoming new members and the liberation from all that binds us that is not of love to become the church to incarnate that love in the world. And I just want to share that as part of our baptism and Pentecost, we will be sharing our covenants and we will be reaffirming our faith and we will vow together to resist evil and injustice and oppression in all the shape and forms that appears to us, especially in this day and time.

And so on today's scripture reading, I just want to highlight a few things. One is that often this scripture has been abused. It has been used to justify and sanctify fake unity, unity without conflict, unity for the sake of keeping peace, which is not true peace. Unity at the expense of the most marginalized and oppressed. This scripture reading is talking about unity in Christ. And so we need to focus and ground in that our understanding of Christ. This unity, especially right now, it's very easy to use the word unity as if it means sameness or uniformity, uh as if unity doesn't involve the embracing of diversity and difference. As if unity is putting under erasure some rights as the rights for everybody and excluding some. So I just want to highlight that the unity that is spoken of in today's reading is very different from the unity in which Christ caused us.

Just a quick word, um, the Gospel of John is a Gospel of love. It's not considered one of the synoptic Gospels. Uh, the writer of John is a theopoetic person and uh often referred to as Gospel of love. And so in today's reading, uh, love is talked about five times out of the six verses. It is this emphasis on love, love that is transgressive, love that crosses boundaries, love that is, was, and will be. This is often referred to as the high priestly prayer because Jesus is offering a intercessory prayer, one of the roles in the priesthood. And in this intercessory prayer, and this is one of my personal core foundations for what we do, who we are, what we do, which is part of our Church Conference, who we are, who we are, uh, what we do and how we do it. And part of my personal core scriptures that undergirts, um, how I show up in the world with love in the midst of suffering, in the midst of injustices, in the midst of oppression, to say love is alive. Love is the exclamation point. Love is who Christ is, what the church is about is this truth.

The scripture tells us that Jesus prayed for you and I. Jesus prayed for you and I. I heard some funny comment from a pastor saying, do you, would you rather talk to Jesus or would you rather Jesus prayed for you? I read that in some commentary. It's not a trick question, it's not, I don't like either/ors, you know. Um, but Jesus prayed for us. Come on, folks. Jesus prayed for you. Jesus prayed for you. What more do we need? What more do we need? Jesus prayed for us. Jesus loved us. Jesus cares about us. That is the powerful spirit of love in and through us as the Church of Jesus, who for so loved the world, prayed for us in this moment.

Um, it is also the scripture reading people refer to as Jesus's glory and Jesus's cross. Because often as we kind of justify unity and we make uh, you know, Hallmark cards out of love, we often think love is an either/or thing. This happy ending versus love showing up as we do today in the world. As what's happened in Seattle this past week, how we are called to show up, to say not only who we are and who we are and what we are and what, how we do it and all that, but as people in this community living in Seattle in this community, we show up saying, this is who Seattle is, right? Why we're having these types of, uh, welcoming for all people, for all rights, for the sake of unity. We have to be careful about how this type of ideas and language is used and that's not how Jesus is using it as he's speaking to the disciples and all of us who are witnesses to not only Christ's love, but disciple's love, and all those who will be witnesses to our love, both the glory and the cross. Love is costly. Love does not justify suffering, but love arrives in and in the midst of suffering to be the presence that can offer reconciliation, healing, justice, peace.

Love is alive. Love is not dead. Jesus is God's love incarnate. And today at year-end celebrations is a time for us to remember to celebrate love in our relationships together, in our relationship with our community partners, in our relationship of being present in the city of Seattle. Um, to know that First Church isn't just First Church in Seattle, but we have been first in the heart of Jesus in the time Jesus praised this prayer in Gethsemane before the crucifixion, before the resurrection, before this week, which is Ascension week. Um, and as we arrive here, I invite us to celebrate love incarnate in all our reports, on the people presenting, um, today at worship and down, uh, afterwards in Church Conference here. Um, it is love we celebrate and love is alive. Um, happy Pride month. And welcome to all of us in the prayer of Jesus, uh, in love with us that we may be in love with God and for, with and for the world. May it be so. Amen.
