## Sermon: Living the Question
**Preacher:** Rev. Wongee Joh
**Scripture:** Mark 8:27-38

Let me begin by setting the context of this reading. Um, it's not the same as today's very domesticated Christianity. Um, so we're looking at first century, 67 to 70 common era in which Mark is written and the readers existing in that, uh, first century. Um, the world is not what they thought it would be. Jesus has died. Uh, Jesus really didn't show up the way they thought he would as the Messiah. Their expectations are squashed. They're living under the shadows of war. The Jerusalem Temple has been completely destroyed. Uh, the savior, the king, the new king, if you want to say, uh, has not come to fruition.

And so into this context, we have this story here in which clearly for the people listening to, uh, this reading in Mark, Jesus is not the winner, the victor. They don't have the victory. So, what is it that the Gospel of Mark is wanting the people to remember about this once upon a time dream of a beloved community, a different way?

I hear existential type questions in this reading today. Um, Jesus asking Peter, "Who do they say I am?" And then, "Who do you say that I am?"

I've had existential questions, and anybody who follows Jesus in any form of discipleship, I think has a question. You have a question. You're living that question. Uh, when I was, uh, in college, I read Viktor Frankl's book. Some of you may have read it. It was, you know, millions and millions, you know, best-seller, uh, Man's Search for Meaning, or if you want to use inclusive language, you know, Person's Search for Meaning. And he's a Holocaust survivor. In the book, he tells about his wife dies in the concentration camps, and what gets him through that time, that shadows of that time is that he practices bringing up the image of his beloved, his wife. And so his witness is that, uh, love, being remembered in love saves him, and gives him the meaning in life and his life after surviving the concentration camps. And he has this wonderful, uh, quote, I like to share. He says, "Ultimately, man, person, should not ask what the meaning of her life is, but rather, she must recognize that it is she who is asked."

So, like Peter, and the readers who are reading this in the shadows of destruction, who is this Messiah? Who is this Jesus? We are asked the question that Peter is asked, "Who do they say I am? Who do you say that I am?" Discipleship is ultimately the outcome of our understanding of who we say Jesus is, who we say he is not. Who do we say Jesus is? Who do we say Jesus is not? Ultimately, discipleship is living out of that understanding.

Question for us: in the 21st century, do you believe in the narrative that we live in a dog eat, dog eat dog world? Because this is one narrative that we live in a dog eat dog world. That's, you know, that's really, I'm sorry to my dogs and my cat. Okay? I mean, it's, uh, it's a, it's a saying that we've had and it's this understanding to dehumanize, to other somebody. We saw it last week, the Syrophoenician, uh, woman and her name child dogs. Even the dogs get crumbs off the table. Even in that empire, this dehumanizing of people as if being a dog is the worst thing in creation, right? Um, but we took it to another level this past week. Not only is it a dog eat dog world, it's a pet eat pet world. Okay? So, um, I, it's a mechanism. Okay? It's a dehumanizing mechanism. It's a racist mechanism. Uh, it is a tool of the empire if you want to say. It is a tool to sustain this power of the center. By naming anyone outside humanity would be like a dog eat dog living in that world. That's not our world.

When I was growing up, I heard this: Korean Americans eat dogs. And, you know, in the day of Instagram, it's interesting that there's all this humorous response to this thing. And I feel bad. I feel brokenhearted that, uh, children should never have to develop a sense of humor to survive and overcome this kind of hatred and racism. No child of God should have to develop such tools and skills to survive such dehumanizing rhetoric.

It's a way to pit one person to another. And it buys into that whole binary, the whole dualism, us and them, you know, the center and the margins.

Good news is, we have an alternate narrative. Who do they say we are? Dog eat dog world, people. Who do you say that we are? We are people of two fishes and five loaves. We are people of abundance with leftover. We are people of not violence against violence. We are not people of the Roman, the symbol of the cross, capital punishment, crucifixion. We are people of the empty cross.

I will set a, I have set a cup. My husband brought that back for me. I like to invite my husband to bring me back communion sets from different places in the world that he travels. And him and my daughter Samantha went to Haiti for a United Methodist mission trip. And I know Jackie spent a whole year after her call to ministry in Haiti. And, uh, he brought me back this carved, um, I asked for communion sets and he brought this one back that says, "Haiti" on it. A place at the table. This is God's beloved community.

I'm hearing this news that in Springfield, people are going to Haitian restaurants, supporting the Haitian community. Uh, for who they are, and, you know, I've read in the news for what they contribute. But for who they are.

I also hear on the news that there's billboard signs in Ohio and different places. Very white supremacist ideology, very, uh, nationalistic, including Christians, nationalistic Christians, who have forgotten the good news as it was given in that first century. Jesus flips the tables. He offers us and has always offered us, uh, alternative narrative, a place at the table, an empty cross.

The cost of discipleship. Who do, who do you say, who do you say that I am? Who do they say I am?

Here's a definition of privilege: if you've never had to live in a dog eat dog world, that's privilege.

Here's another definition of privilege: if you know grace, to choose Jesus's narrative, to deny ourselves participating in the hegemony, the dominance of this dog eat dog world, and for us to rally, let's kick off this new church here, rally for what it means to live our true discipleship of who we say Jesus is. Jesus is not just welcoming of US citizens. In Jesus's kingdom, all beloved, including dogs, including pets, are welcome in this another way possible.

What are you rallying for this year? Let's rally behind Jesus the Christ, the Messiah. Let us rally behind the good news, the message of another way possible. May it be so. Amen.
