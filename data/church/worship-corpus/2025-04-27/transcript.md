Good morning to all of you. The Climate Justice Working Group has developed the Climate Church Covenant that you'll you'll hear today. We'll see it shortly to express the priorities and values of First Church in relation to the crisis of climate change. The covenant was adopted by the Church Council this month and it's presented today as a prayer of the people.

Originally, Wally Shafer was going to do the introduction and I when I was asked to do it, I've come late to the or let the tail end of the Climate Justice group. I was very pleased to do so and I had some ideas on what I might say. But then I saw, I read what what Wally was going to say and it's so good. I think I I'm just going to read it. Why is this important? We need to frame the climate climate crisis as a spiritual spiritual issue as well as a social justice issue in which we need to actively defend God's creation and call ourselves to action together as a faith community. The Climate Justice team was inspired to write this covenant by Reverend Jim Antal, our Earth Day guest preacher a year ago and the author of the book Climate Church, Climate World, how people of faith must work for change.

Reverend Antal makes a fairly convincing case that climate change is the greatest moral challenge of today. Similar to the moral standards that churches advocated in the movement to abolish slavery and in the Civil Rights movement, we need to set a moral standard based on a spiritual viewpoint that we are destroying God's creation and that we are worsening injustice suffered by those who have the fewest resources to defend themselves, including humans and all other creatures. Why a covenant? This is a promise, a pledge to ourselves, to nature and to God. It means that First Church is unified and serious in this endeavor. The Climate Justice team started with Antal's thoughts and wording and then with reworking and editing, we made it our own, First Church Climate Covenant. We expect this to be to supplement our individual actions and Earth pledges, helping to answer the question, what can an individual do to affect climate change? The answer, quit acting as an individual and start working together with others. The Climate Church Covenant is a parallel statement to the Land Acknowledgement both recognizing the sacredness of all God's creation and both represent acts of repentance, since all of us here are contributors to the current climate crisis in every unsustainable way, in the very unsustainable way we have been living. Thus, the Climate Church Covenant is appropriate for the recent Lenten season and appropriate for our Earth Day celebration. And appropriate for our current political times when our Earth and environment clearly need defense. The overall goal is to brand First Church as a climate church. And since I have forgotten my paper,

This is uh the climate covenant. In covenant with God and with all of creation, as a climate conscious church, the members of First United Methodist Church of Seattle pledge ourselves to be responsive to our creating call from God, informed by the realities of the most recent climate science, focused on our common good, including all creatures and on treating future generations as we would treat ourselves. Driven by prophetic imagination as we envision a livable future and a just world in which we see every living being as a reflection of God's majesty. Unafraid to hasten a just transition to a sustainable way of living that honors the interdependence of all creation. Generous in using our gifts and assets in solidarity with those disproportionately impacted by the effects of climate change to build a more resilient community. Prayerfully and courageously in using our collective power to animate the conscience of the nation and to take action in the Sailor C region, our state, our country and our global community, as we join with people throughout the world to restore God's glorious creation.

## Sermon: Climate Justice from the Frontlines of the Climate Crisis
**Preacher:** Roishetta Ozane
**Scripture:** John 20:19-23, 30-31
[08:29]
Good morning, beloved community. I come to you today with a heart full of passion and a spirit fueled by the urgency of our time. My name is Roishetta O'Zane. I am an environmental justice advocate, the founder and director and CEO of the Vessel Project of Louisiana, the co-director of the Gulf Fossil Finance Hub, a mom of six, and a grandmother of one. And I'm coming to you from Sulphur, Louisiana. As I think about this beautiful congregation, I can imagine faces that are eager and ready to learn, and that we are all woven together by the threads of resilience, strength and hope. Together, we are more than just neighbors. We are a family bound by a commitment to our community and our planet. And as we gather here, let us remember the wise words of Amelia Earhart, "The most difficult thing is the decision to act. The rest is merely tenacity."

Our decision to stand up for our homes, our children, and our future is the first step on this challenging journey. And as we step forward, we must do so with the fierce tenacity of our ancestors who fought for justice and equality before us. Ancestors like my relative Fannie Lou Hamer, who once said that three are better three people are better than no people. And I can imagine that there are more than three people out in the audience today. Let us recognize that our collective voices are powerful. We are not alone in our struggle. We are united in our resolve. We are here to speak for those who have been silenced, to advocate for those, um, who who feel like they can't advocate for themselves, and to advocate for our the health of our families and to protect the beautiful planet that we all share. Especially states like Louisiana. A state that dances with the rhythm of jazz and breathes life through its vibrant culture. Yet as I sit before you today, in the midst of this beauty, our state is faced with a harsh reality. Our air is thick with the fumes of chemical polluters. Our wetlands are disappearing at an alarming rate, losing a football field every hour. Our groundwater is receding and with it, our access to clean drinking water. The health of our community is under siege with above average poverty rates, cancer rates, and toxic pollution weighing heavily upon us.

This is not just a a statistic. It's our lived experience. The Bible reminds us in Proverbs 31:8-9, "Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves, for the rights of all who are destitute. Speak up and judge fairly. Defend the rights of the poor and needy." Today, we must speak up for our families, our neighborhoods, and for our future. We are called to be the voice of the voiceless, to challenge the powerful interest of those that seek to exploit our communities while disregarding our health and safety. They don't care about us. So we must care about each other. Here in Southwest Louisiana, where communities of color are bearing the brunt of this environmental injustice, we find ourselves on the front lines of the climate crisis. With proposed methane polluting gas export terminals threatening our air and water. And we must not remain silent about it. We cannot allow our communities to be deemed sacrifice zones any longer. Our lives are not expendable as they would like them to be. We are not to be just thrown away for the profit of a few. Our friends are hurting. Our neighbors are suffering. Our children are dying. Yet, my friends, we must remember that we are not the criminals in this narrative. We are the protectors, the mothers, the fathers, the grandparents, striving to ensure a better future for our children. The grandparents safeguarding the legacy of our families. And the advocates fighting for the earth that sustain us all. It is time for those who profit from pollution to be held accountable for their climate crimes.

As we lift our voices, let us do so with conviction and hope. Let us stand firm in our tenacity, knowing that we are not alone. Together, we can create a wave of change that echoes through our communities and beyond. Let us be the changemakers, the champions of justice, and the stewards of our planet. We must take care of those who cannot or feel like they cannot take care of themselves. We must continue to stand up. We must continue to fight and we must do so together. In closing, let us remember the words of Isaiah 1:17. "Learn to do right. Seek justice. Defend the oppressed." Today, we commit to this call. We will seek justice for our families, our communities, our beloved Louisiana, and this entire planet. Because there is no plan B. We must make polluters pay. And we must continue to fight for environmental justice for all. Thank you all today for listening, for being here, for giving of yourselves and your time. I look forward to working with each of you. May you all be blessed on your journey. Have a good day. Thank you.
