## Sermon: And the Stones Shall Speak!
**Preacher:** Rev. Jackie Celin
**Scripture:** Luke 19:28-40

**Pastor Jackie:** Today is Palm Sunday. And the first day of Passion Week, the holiest week in the Christian tradition. During this week, we reflect on Jesus's last week as we recall how he turned over the tables in the temple, which had become overrun with greed. We'll remember his last meal with his disciples before he was betrayed by one of his own. We'll experience the darkness and pain of Good Friday, when we mourn Jesus's death on the cross, and we'll sit in the stillness of Holy Saturday, while we wait in anticipation for the Easter morning resurrection of Christ.

Later this week, we hope you'll join us for a Maundy Thursday service in Fellowship Hall, and our Good Friday service with Queen Anne United Methodist Church. More on that later.

Palm Sunday is the day Jesus triumphantly enters Jerusalem with his disciples after a long journey. Jesus's entrance acts as a counter procession, in protest to the oppressive Roman governments of his time. The governing body which will ultimately cruelly crucify him just a short while later. Each year at the time of Passover, Roman rulers would come to Jerusalem to showcase the Roman Empire's mighty power, to intimidate the people in the land and to suppress or squash any thoughts or attempts at testing Roman authority.

And so, while the Roman rulers entered from the west, from Caesarea Maritima, the great city on the Mediterranean Sea built by his father, Herod the Great. Jesus entered from the east, from the sacred sacred place of his people, the Mount of Olives.

And while Herod entered on decorated military horses surrounded by his soldiers, Jesus entered on a borrowed humble colt. A young donkey dressed with nothing but a few simple cloaks. He was surrounded by his disciples. And while Herod enters in a show of the Roman Empire's power and wealth, Jesus enters Jerusalem as an expression of the peace and love of the kingdom of God. The young donkey was often seen as a sign of peace rather than war.

Jesus's procession placed him opposite of the political and military power, but it also aligned him with the socially and economically oppressed people of his day. The Palm Sunday story of Jesus entering Jerusalem is found in all four Gospels. The narrative is especially very similar amongst the three synoptic Gospels of Matthew, Mark, and Luke. This year, as part of the lectionary cycle C, we study Luke's narrative, which contains an added element not found in the other Gospel accounts.

In Luke, the story ends with the religious authorities challenging Jesus about his counter procession, telling him to have his disciples stop what they are doing. Perhaps they are fearful that Jesus's procession will draw too much attention from the violent Roman rulers that could lead to disastrous things for their whole community. Or perhaps they are worried about losing some of their own privileges as leaders of their community with its placement under Roman rule during this time.

Whatever their motivation, Jesus answers by saying, "I tell you, if these were silent, the stones would shout out." What a stunning statement. The stones? The stones would shout? Certainly, the stones couldn't really come to life. But there is something remarkable in considering the most inanimate object of God's beloved created world becoming the most animated.

Rock is foundational. It is sturdy and unshakable. Earlier in the Gospel, the parable of the wise and foolish builders, Jesus describes how the wise will build their houses on a foundation of rock, while the foolish will build an unstable house with no foundation at all. And in Ephesians, Paul describes how the early church, the apostles and prophets, are the foundation of the Christian church and Christ himself is the cornerstone that directs and guides us in the way to the kingdom of God.

Of course, Jesus isn't trying to say that the stones themselves will literally speak. He's emphasizing a point. He's reminding us of the interconnectedness of the cosmos, how all parts will shift and groan as we continue to cause harm to one another and to other parts of creation. When one is silenced, another will shout out, and sometimes it might be in the least expected ways. The truth of God's divine love will always be revealed. No matter how much the oppressor tries to silence anything or anyone that threatens its power. The good news will be proclaimed, the redeeming and transformative power of God's unconditional and uncontrollingly love will always shout out and shine through.

The Palm Sunday narrative comes at the end of a long travel narrative in Luke. Beginning in chapter 9 and ending here in chapter 19. The long journey from Galilee to Jerusalem with his disciples, reminds me of the long arduous journey that many migrants and immigrants take, as they leave their homes and everything they've ever known. And yet, when they arrive at their destination, they are often met with ever increasing hardships.

Reverend Cruz Edwin Santos, the Director of Hispanic and Latinx ministries in our conference, writes, "Today, immigrant families face unprecedented levels of fear, frustration, and brokenness. The persecution they experience comes in many forms. Legal and political barriers. The immigration system is complex, often leaving families in legal limbo for years. Economic hardships. Many immigrants come to the US seeking success, but are met with exploitation, low wages, and limited job opportunities. Social and cultural rejection. The expectation that the immigrants must assimilate, rather than be embraced for their diverse identities, creates alienation and eraser. Family separation. Harsh immigration policies continue to separate children from their parents, causing lasting trauma." He says, "We must advocate for these families and walk alongside them with integrity and love."

The United Nations Office of the High Commissioner explains how many migrants are vulnerable to human rights violations, including a denial of civil and political rights, such as arbitrary detention, torture, or a lack of due process, as well as economic, social and cultural rights. The denial of migrants' rights is often closely linked to discriminatory laws and to deep-seated attitudes of prejudice. Often, the most vulnerable in our societies are dehumanized and silenced, and we see it happening in alarming ways in our society today.

Let us listen for the cries of the voiceless. And Jesus said, "I tell you, if these were silent, the stones would shout out." If the voices of the marginalized are suppressed, their souls will cry out. If humanity and creation are harmed, the people, the community will roar. And in this moment, here and now, it is time, friends, all the stone shall speak.

And so, I invite you now, to take a moment and close your eyes. Feel the ways your soul is intertwined with the rest of humanity, with creation. Feel it reach out beyond your body, to sense the suffering of others all around you. Listen to the cries, to the voices, to the tears of the many hidden from sight. Listen. To the sounds of suffering, of a man, wrongfully placed in a crowded and cruel foreign prison. Listen. To the silent scream of a woman, being whisked away without warning to detention by masked men. Listen. To the cries of the young war orphan, lying next to the lifeless bodies of his parents, without knowledge of how to survive on his own. Listen. To the parched lips of the thirsty, with no access to clean water, who are forbidden from collecting even rainwater to quench their thirst. Listen. To the rumbling stomachs of a family, a world away, who no longer have access to the food that they once relied on to survive day-by-day.

Listen to the ways the earth is crying out in pain. Through the through the fracturing ice caps, to the dry crackling flames of the wildfires. Listen. Listen. Listen. What else is crying out to you?

These are the cries of the world. The stones are speaking in whatever way they can. God's beloved creation is making itself be heard. Every person, every creature is worthy of life, and God's love will not be silenced. The good news is that God's divine love will transform our world to a place where all creation can thrive. And we are called into the responsibility of proclaiming this good news. And so friends, the time is now. To reach into that inner part of ourselves that is scared and timid.

The church must speak when confronted with such astonishing injustices. We all must speak. And all the stone shall speak. Just last week, we gathered with many others to participate in the nationwide non-violent Hands Off event. To demand a stop to harmful public policies. Hundreds of thousands of people across the nation participated, showing how the stone speak when others have been silenced. We carried many of those signs today in our processional. You see him up here in the chancel.

Remembering Jesus's bold visible statements against the oppressive ruling systems of his day. I wore my General Board of Church and Society shirt, which boldly states what we stand for. Let me read it to you.

"United Methodist stand for the dignity of all people, climate justice, racial justice, non-proliferation of weapons. We stand for migrants and refugees, eradicating poverty, education for all. We stand for maternal and child health, economic justice, gun violence prevention, criminal justice reform, indigenous rights, religious freedom, fair trade, sustainability. We stand for women and children, environmental justice, LGBTQ rights, healthy food for all, civil rights, domestic violence prevention, addiction care, mental health, human trafficking prevention, abolishing the death death penalty. Ultimately, we stand for justice."

Surely, there is something in there that speaks to you. And so, when you leave here today, make the commitment to just do something. Something, anything, anything non-violent. And let us remind ourselves that when we come from places of privilege, whether because of our race, ethnicity, gender, sexual orientation, level of education, citizenship, the language we speak, or the religion we practice, we have a responsibility to listen for the voices being oppressed and to speak out against the oppressor. And so in this time here and now, we must live into Jesus's commandment of loving our neighbors by making the effort to get to know our neighbors. To learn the challenges that our neighbors are truly facing. To understand the ways our systems of governance have tried to silence them again and again.

So go out and meet the people in your communities, volunteer with organizations that are overwhelmed and in the trenches right now. Read and educate yourselves. And stand in solidarity with people and communities being harmed. If the voices of the vulnerable and marginalized are suppressed, listen for the ways the stones are crying, speaking, singing, and shouting, and let yourself be one of them. May God give us strength for the long days ahead, courage to stand up to injustice, wisdom to live as Jesus lived, and may we be led in the love, peace, and compassion of the Holy Spirit. All glory and praise to you, Holy One. Amen.
