## Sermon: Be Free of Our Afflictions
**Preacher:** Joey Lopez, Tara Miller
**Scripture:** Wisdom of Solomon 1:13-15, 2:23-24, Mark 5: 25-34

**Tara:**
War is incompatible with Christian teaching.

**Joey:**
Hold on, Tara. Wrong incompatibility clause.

**Tara:**
Hold on, bear with me for a moment. Um, I was raised a child of the military, and from an early age, I was faced with the marriage of US Christianity with militarism and patriotism. And that was really confusing for me. I heard the church teaching me about the inherent worthiness of all living beings, but there's a contradiction of this teaching with an exception that that could happen in relationship to war.

9/11 happened when I was 11 years old, and that marriage of church and militarism and patriotism became even more rooted in the community that I was living in and the stories that would come from me, from my parents. And so, when I was a teenager, my grandparents took me to this uh, country music concert, and there was a moment where the singer was about to sing "Proud to Be an American." But before he did that, he shouted slurs against Arabs and Muslims. And my cousin and I turned to each other, and we decided in that moment, absolutely not, and we sat down. We weren't going to stand for that song. The whole way home our grandparents yelled at us the entire time. Once we got home, our parents yelled at us the entire time. But we knew we had made the right decision. This and many other stories inform my work and how I, how I understand my life and how I understand the way I want to live. This confirmed a deep commitment to the inherent worthiness of all living beings. And that's been one of the core values I've taken into addressing all other systems of oppression. And it's a core struggle I've continued to experience as the church fails to live fully into this teaching.

**Joey:**
Since Tara started with the other incompatibility clause, I guess I should spend a little time on the one that is no more. I want to share with you a story. In 2011, I put myself forward as a delegate to the General Conference. I was a young adult in Western North Carolina. And we were voting on our delegates, and we had just finished filling our lay delegate slate, and we were about to elect on a elect our reserve delegates. And someone stood up and said, "I just want to name to the body that we have yet to elect any young people." And she listed all the young people on on the, um, who had made themselves available, but she didn't say my name. And then she said, "We've uh, elected no Latina people." And she listed all of the Latina people who made themselves available, and she didn't say my name. And then she said, "But there's one del one person who's made themselves available, who is a young Latino. His name's Joey Lopez." And then on the next ballot, I was the first reserve delegate, first elected reserve delegate.

But quickly in that time leading up to General Conference, I learned why why it took someone calling attention to the lack of diversity in our delegation. The leaders in my conference, the folks who had been to General Conference for generations, did not want the church to change, which meant they wanted people on their delegation that looked like them and that thought like them. I heard things leading up to that General Conference, like, "You're too young to understand the complexities of our church's structure," or, "That's not important for us to discuss at this time." Fast forward to General Conference. We had just taken the vote on whether or not we would remove the incompatibility clause around human sexuality from our discipline, and it failed. And I found myself weeping. And I realized later that my weeping wasn't just about how the church had continued to live in broken covenant with me as a queer person, but I was also weeping about the brokenness between the church and Black people, and indigenous people, and other communities of color, of young people.

Today we have on the table a chalice and a paten from the 2000 General Conference. Sharon, Pastor Sharon Moe, and Pastor Jen Bollerjack, in an act of public witness, smashed this chalice after the vote was taken on human sexuality. And it's here as a reminder of the broken covenant we still live into. Despite the changes in our discipline, repair and healing has yet to happen. We have not found a way to heal our covenant. I left that General Conference in 2012 with a deepened commitment to the long journey of repairing our broken covenant. Not just with my queer and trans siblings, but also with Black, indigenous, and people of color communities in our church.

**Tara:**
Now that you've gotten to know a little bit about us and the values that ground our vocations, let's connect to the text, to Pride and Now, and to our role as people of faith to do justice. We know from personal experience, and we believe through our shared values, that those closest to the problem are closest to the solution. In the Mark text, we heard the story of the bleeding woman. She knew what she needed to be healed, and when she received that, Jesus affirmed her faith. Jesus said, "Your faith has saved you. Go in peace and be free of your affliction." This story is sandwiched between another story of Jesus journeying to meet an ailing child, and the father had come to Jesus to ask for help. When Jesus arrived to the home, those there ridiculed him. They were convinced that the child was dead. But Jesus took the people who believed that life was possible, and he woke her up. The child's parents and the child, those who were closest to the problem, believed, and their faith is what saved her. But those ridiculing Jesus were further furthest from the problem, and they were not directly impacted. They could not see a hope for a future that was not obvious to them.

In the Wisdom of Solomon text, it's made clear that God created all things to be alive, and that's contrasted with those who align themselves with the adversary, befriending death. Those who align themselves with the adversary, those who have the power to easily make change and refuse to, see no other choice but accept death's outcome, like those who ridiculed Jesus over the sleeping child. It is clear to us that the adversary is white supremacy, it's colonialism, it's capitalism, it's militarism, and it's policing. And it's all of the ways that oppression reinforces the disposability of all living beings, ultimately leading to death.

**Joey:**
So it's Pride Sunday, so we should talk a little bit about the history of the queer liberation movement. What do you think? The queer liberation movement, from its beginning, started with an intersectional approach to justice. This movement began and informed by the successes of the Civil Rights Movement, the Black Panther Party, feminism, and anti-war. We earmark this time of year to celebrate pride because of the Stonewall riots in New York, where Black and brown gender diverse people threw bricks through windows at the Stonewall Inn. The riot was a direct action, a protest, an effort to stop the over-policing of queer spaces, especially the policing of queer and gender diverse Black and brown bodies. Building on the momentum of these riots, our movement started to shift away from the Black and brown and gender diverse people who built it. Those closest to the problem. Instead of creating a new world dedicated to life, those with economic and passing privilege within the LGBTQ+ community, specifically white, gay, and lesbian, cisgender people, bartered with death, trading parts of our community for the illusion of fitting in. That is until the AIDS epidemic began sweeping across the US. Our community realized the limitations of fitting in, and shifted back to an intersectional political analysis, demanding things like healthcare for all, marriage and domestic partnership rights, and economic justice. However, again, as our movement for marriage progressed, so did our dealings with death. By the 90s, those who were getting infected and dying from HIV and AIDS were Black and brown people and gender diverse people. Marriage couldn't be about addressing death or deficits. So we traded that in for a new story that spotlighted parenthood and family values, specifically spotlighting white, wealthy, cisgender gay and lesbian couples. We moved away over and over from those closest to the problem in our community, leading us to a that would have led us to a collective liberation, and left open the door for continued harm and death to that part of our community most vulnerable. A legacy that still follows us today.

**Tara:**
The ritual for new membership into the United Methodist Church, asks us to renounce all that is evil, and profess our faith and loyalty to following the ways of Jesus. Members of the church respond by affirming our commitment to the same, and to surround these persons with a community of love and forgiveness, that they may grow in their trust of God and be found faithful in their service to others. It says we will pray for them that they will be true disciples who walk in the way that leads to life. When we profess this covenant, we build in confession for when we fall short. When we fail to honor this covenant in its entirety, and a commitment to repair and reconciliation. While the recent actions of the General Conference created a path toward repentance, repair, and reconciliation, for the 52 years we have lived in broken covenant with LGBTQ people. But it took us 52 years to correct the language in the Book of Discipline, and it has been so much longer that Black, indigenous, and people of color have faced a church of a broken covenant. How long before our church chooses life, creating a path toward repentance, repair, and reconciliation with BIPOC communities? If we are going to peace back the broken parts of our covenant. If we are going to move toward healing and reconciliation. If we are going to talk about Pride now, then we must have a broader, more expansive commitment to liberation.

**Joey:**
Everyone take a breath with me.

We spent most of our time this morning talking about where we and where our movement has fallen short. And now I want to shift us around how we cannot continue to do that moving forward. So I want to start with a story. It's a Sufi tale, a story of a locksmith. Once there lived a locksmith who was unjustly accused of crimes and was sentenced to prison. After a while, his wife went to the king and begged that she might at least give him a prayer rug so he could observe his five daily prayers. The king granted her request. Much later, the man escaped from prison, and when people asked him how he got out, he explained that after years of doing his prayers for deliverance from the prison, he began to see what was right in front of his nose. His wife had woven into the prayer the pattern of the lock that imprisoned him. He realized that he had all the information he needed to escape. So he began to make friends with his guards, persuading them that they all would have a better life if they escaped the prison together. The guards agreed, since they realized they were in prison too. They also wished to escape. So the locksmith and his guards decided on the following plan. They would bring him pieces of metal, and he would fashion useful items from those pieces of metal to sell in the marketplace. Together they would amass resources for their escape. And from the strongest piece of metal they could acquire, the locksmith would fashion a key. One night, when everything had been prepared, the locksmith and his guards unlocked the prison and walked out into the cool night. The locksmith was reunited with his loving wife. His former guards became his friends, and everyone lived in harmony.
