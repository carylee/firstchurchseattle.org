**Anthony:** Yeah, so now that we've heard a little bit about the context of what the situation is in the Philippines, the current the current political, the current economic and social situation. We're going to talk more specifically about what this International People's Tribunal or IPT is.

And so, as mentioned previously, it's a non-governmental judicial body convened by two alliances on the international stage, the Friends of the Filipino People in Struggle, as well as the International Alliance of Democratic Lawyers. I-Chirp was actually not one of the principal, thank you. I-Chirp was one of not one of the principal organizations that convened it, but we endorsed and supported in putting it on. As mentioned, it was convened last May in Belgium with attendees coming in from all over the world to document and shine light on the aforementioned human rights situation in the Philippines, including a large number of people coming directly from the Philippines to share their experiences at the hands of the government.

We heard from a number of expert witnesses on international humanitarian law, war crimes, and the political situation in the Philippines, and the direct testimony from victims of these grave abuses. Over the two days of the tribunal, a panel of independent journalists, independent jurors, including Lennox Hinds, who is Nelson Mandela's United Nations and US attorney, heard testimony and argument from the prosecution on the abuses that have taken place under the US-backed regimes of the former President Rodrigo Duterte and current President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.

This tribunal, while unconventional, as it takes place outside of the purview of formal international and national governing bodies, is not unprecedented. It follows in line of a number of tribunals, both on the Philippines specifically, making it the fourth one, but also throughout different crises and struggles the world over, dating all the way back to the first International People's Tribunal on the Vietnam War. Because of the judicial system in the within the Philippines that Nica mentioned, which is wrought with political corruption, intimidation, and outright threats. The Filipino people are not able to seek justice within their own legal system. In this way, the IPT serves to bring a spotlight to the pressing nature of the situation in the Philippines and provides one step closer for justice for the Filipino people by exposing the powerful system and individuals that are at the forefront of this.

And so an important aspect of the tribunal itself, and for us to really understand the gravity with which these charges and this investigation took place, is international humanitarian law. International humanitarian law or IHL, as we'll refer to it throughout the remainder of this sermon, are basic principles that no entity, whether it be state or non-state group, can legally disregard. The primary basis of IHL comes from the Geneva Conventions in 1949, following World War II. These laws draw a distinction between combatants and civilians, as well as military objectives and civilian objectives. They require the attacks must be proportionate and that precautions are taken to minimize civilian harm, and all persons, civilians and combatants alike, must be treated humanely and with due regard for their dignity. In other words, through these well-entrenched principles, IHL imposes clear obligations which the conflicting parties cannot overlook or evade. Beyond the Geneva Conventions mentioned here, there have been additional treaties and agreements that have been signed on the international stage, including the protocols one, two, and three of the Geneva Conventions, which entrenched the right of struggle for oppressed people to resist foreign domination and local exploitation, which is exactly the case in the Philippines. Additionally, the Rome Statute here established the International Criminal Court, which we'll speak a bit more on later as well.

From there, with our understanding of IHL, we can get into some of the testimony that we heard. And some of those that spoke directly. The first, the first testimony we'll be speaking to one of two today, will be on Brandon Lee. Brandon Lee is a Chinese-American human rights advocate, a graduate of San Francisco State University, who first moved to the Philippines in 2011, inspired by the vibrant movement for national democracy, the very one that also brought me into doing international solidarity work with the Filipinos.

Brandon eventually became a paralegal, a media correspondent, and a human rights volunteer with the Ifugao peasant movement in Luzon, the northernmost island chain in the Philippines, working to support indigenous communities in fights against militarization of their ancestral land and also against corporate land grabs by multinational mining operations. Brandon testified that in 2014, after attending a court hearing where the Philippine National Police were being tried for corruption, Ifugao peasant movement human rights officer William Bugatti was assassinated on his way home. That same year, Brandon Lee assumed that very role of human rights officer, which led to years of escalating harassment by the Philippine National Police, the Armed Forces of the Philippines, and eventually an assassination attempt on his life, made outside of his home in Luzon in August 2019.
