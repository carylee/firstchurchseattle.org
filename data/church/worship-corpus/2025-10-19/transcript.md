## Sermon: "Who, me!?"
**Preacher:** Barbara Moreland
**Scripture:** Esther 4:1-3, 10-14 (NRSVue), John 4 - excerpts (NRSVue)

**Barbara:** Will you pray with me? May the words of my mouth and the meditation of all of our hearts be acceptable in your sight, oh God, our Rock and our Redeemer. Amen.

I want to begin by bringing up one more scripture for us to consider. It's from the beginning of Psalm 139. In talking to God, the writer says, "I praise you, for I am fearfully and wonderfully made. Wonderful are your works, my soul knows it well." The word fearfully here does not refer to being scared, but to awe-inspiring experience and reverence, the kind of beauty that takes your breath away. And wonderfully highlights the distinct and marvelous complexity of the human form. It emphasizes that each person is intricately crafted by God from the beginning, and everyone is a masterpiece of God's creative ability.

The masterpiece begins with our physical bodies, but extends to all the complex qualities and abilities and talents the creator instills into each individual, into a pattern that is specific to that person. That is, we are all alike and at the same time, each of us is unique. I am and each of you are a one-of-a-kind, awe-inspiring, deliberate, holy creation of the one great creator. Feel good about that.

And while recognizing this makes me feel grateful and blessed, it also makes me nervous. It makes me wonder what I'm supposed to do with the gift of being me. After all, God may have made me from a perfect blueprint, but I know that the final product has some serious glitches in it. So how do I honor the gifts of the creator that have been placed within me? How do I be all that the creator wants me to be?

And here's where the experience of the women in our scriptures can give us some insight. We have the stories of two very different women. The first, who is a Jew, who was adopted by an upright Jewish man, Mordecai, and was protected and advised by him. We know her name. It's Esther. She was beautiful, intelligent, sophisticated, and when necessary, cunning. She was even given, she was given an opportunity to join the king's household and was soon favored beyond all the other young women. Eventually, after the king had gotten rid of his current wife because he didn't think she obeyed very well, he eventually chose Esther to be his new queen.

And Mordecai, her adopted father, eventually learns about a plot to kill Jews in the country where they are, and he confronts Esther with the task of protecting the Jewish people, and it's a responsibility she is not eager to take on. Mordecai tells her that her elite status and royal position have made it, have been given to her for just such a time as this. So eventually, Esther agrees. She sets in motion a complicated, devious plan to expose a traitor, to protect her father, and to save the lives of the Jewish people. Queen Esther uses her knowledge of the royal household, her ability to convince others that she is trustworthy, her persuasiveness and a few half-truths to get others to fall into her plans. She takes full advantage of her skills, her intelligence, her knowledge of the people, and a little bit of deceptiveness. Sounds pretty human to me. And she is successful. Esther is a woman of privilege, who uses that privilege to accomplish her mission, that is to save her father and the people of her community.

The second woman's situation is the direct opposite of Esther's. She is a Samaritan, a member of the ethnic group that proper Jews did not interact with. She is not given a name. We call her the woman at the well. She is most likely a social outcast, or at least a marginal person because she has to come in the middle of the day to draw her water, when there's not anyone else around. She's not intelligent, but she's not sophisticated either, and likely has little or no first-hand knowledge of the world beyond her small village. When she encounters Jesus, she's intrigued by the fact that first of all, he willingly approaches her, and then his strange talk about water that quenches thirst forever. When she begins to recognize him as a prophet, she runs back to the village to tell her neighbors, the same people who have likely shunned her in the past. And she brings them to this new Jewish stranger. Many of them become convinced that Jesus is the Messiah. Her life has been changed, as well as those of the people around her.

Both of these women are successful in handling the situation facing them. And neither could have done what the other one did. They used what they were given, their instincts and personality, the knowledge gained from personal experience, their grasp of the specific opportunity facing them. They took all of that and chose to accomplish something important and personal that also had very significant effects on other people. Each one did something the other woman could not do.

And that's another beautiful thing about God's creativity. There are no exact duplicates. Each person is unique, and therefore, each is not simply present in their own world, but awfully a critical and necessary piece in other people's stories. Your particular combination of talents, gifts, abilities, insights, experience, and wisdom is unique to you. And using all of those gifts, offering them as we turn to God and to each other, I think that's what the masterpiece-making creator requires of us. The real question God asks is not, "Why aren't you like so-and-so?" It's, "What are you doing with what you have been given?" And too often our answer is, um, what, who, me? I don't think so.

Today is Laity Sunday in the United Methodist Church. It's a day when we acknowledge and celebrate the contributions and the gifts of the non-clergy persons in our congregations. Laity covers the vast majority of the women, the men and women looking at me right now. The members of the choir, the people you greet as you came in, the person who set the altar table, the ones who are setting up our Ministry Fair downstairs, those who prepared, cooked, and served breakfast to the homeless this morning, and cleans up afterwards. I acknowledge, celebrate and give thanks for all of you who work so hard doing your part to make First Church function.

I think of Laity Sunday as kind of like having a birthday. You're around the same people every day, at home, at work, at school, and these people are basically, uh, pleasant and maybe even kind. But on your birthday, you get lots of attention, even cake, and a party, and maybe some presents, because it's your day. But what they're really celebrating is that almost every day, you're a decent person, a giving soul, a hard worker, or a kind helper. They are acknowledging and celebrating you.

Let's go back to those two women we started with. One was a sophisticated, practical, insightful woman, who had been on both sides of the social and political power structures. Esther used her experience and knowledge of how things really worked in the world to save people. Her actions were complex and a little manipulative, but not violent in her pursuit of justice. The other woman had no connections, no social capital, and probably no experience with life beyond her small village. Yet she quickly understood that Jesus was unique, maybe a prophet, and that he was responding to her differently than everyone else did. And she acted immediately to share this man she had encountered with her neighbors, who then came to believe in him as well, because when he heard who me, she answered.

Yes, today is Laity Sunday, and I realize that we make the general distinction between clergy and Laity, with clergy being those who have received a specific call, excuse me, have gone through a particular discernment process and extensive training, which I think our pastors would agree with. They have been given specific duties and privileges within the structure of the church. And I know we have deep gratitude for the clergy who have are serving First Church now and for many who have served First Church in the past.

But I believe everyone is called to serve. Based on what gifts we've been given, what talents we've developed, what life experiences we've been through. Each of us has a personal obligation to wrestle with the questions, "Who am I and what do I have to offer my God and my neighbor?" And your answers are equally personal, but very different from mine. At the beginning, I asked a question. "How do I be all the creator intends me to be?" Well, let's go back to our two women whose stories we've heard, and see what we learned from them. Each woman took action. Neither could have done what the other one did. Each one contributed her talents, knowledge, experience, herself, to the situation at hand, and by doing so, contributed to the greater good.

I think that's a pretty good example of how Laity works in a church, and in the world. Each individual contributes to the community, to God, by being who they are and using the strengths they've been given in a way that most aligns with God's purpose. They might ask the question, "Who me?" But the answer is almost always, "Here am I, send me."

When I look into the faces of my First Church community, I see disciples, lay and clergy, who are fearfully and wonderfully made by the great creator. People who respond to such a time as this, and we've all said that recently, in different ways to honor God, to support our church, to serve the community, and to hopefully impact the world. I thank God that you are busy being you. Amen.
