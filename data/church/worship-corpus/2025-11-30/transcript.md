## The Hanging of the Greens
[17:35]
**Barbara Moreland:**
Excuse me. Over 2,000 years ago, the story goes that a group of sleepy shepherds were watching over the their sheep on a starlit hillside in Palestine. It was a still, uneventful night until suddenly the darkness was filled with a strange light. The stillness was broken by angel voices singing, "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace, goodwill to all." A baby had been born, who they proclaimed to be the promised Messiah.

This event became so important and central to the Christian faith that our ancestors thought a time of preparation was necessary before we could celebrate the birth of Jesus. Advent, from the Latin word "Adventus," meaning "coming," is the season of expectant waiting that we prepare for today. Both the seasons of Advent and Christmas have customs developed through many centuries and many countries. Old customs have been refined and renewed, and new ones added.

Our worship service on this first Sunday of Advent invites us to deck the halls with greens and bells and glowing candles. And it urges us to hear the songs of the season and offer scripture and gifts as promises of reawakening life and a reawakening earth in spring. We invite you, if you are listening at home, to decorate in at home, along with us, to bring to mind your decorations of today or in the past, as we consider each element of the hanging of the greens. This morning we will prepare our sanctuary and our homes for this holy season, but this is simply an outward reminder of our inward preparations. As we work together today to prepare our church with these holy symbols, may our working together and hearing God's word proclaimed together prepare you and me to accept the gift of Christ again this Christmas.

### The Trees
**Tom Bruhns:**
The trees. Advent begins in the northern countries in the dead of winter. And yet, it begins the church year. The first mystery we are asked to engage in is waiting. We enter the dormant waiting time of winter. Nature seems asleep, but all that is becoming is hidden from our view. The evergreens that bear our Christmas decorations are symbols of that eternal life that may go dormant, but does not die during the winter months. From the German dramas of the Middle Ages, we bring into our homes the evergreen, representing the tree of life.

### The Star
**Ann Shaffer:**
The star. The star is placed in a high position for Advent worship. It is central in our thoughts as we recall that it was under the light of stars that the first announcement of the birth of Jesus was made to the Judean shepherds. It was the light of a star which guided the visitors from the East to play to the place where Jesus was born. The Gospel of Matthew tells us, "And lo, the star which they saw, in the East, went before them, till it came and stood over where the young child was." "When they saw the star, they rejoiced with exceeding great joy." In some lands, including Spain, Italy, and Russia, the people would wait until the first star appeared in the sky on Christmas Eve before beginning their celebrations. Our sanctuary is lit by the lights on the trees and by the light of the star.

God's justice is full of generosity, being faithful and self-controlled. Jesus lived before us the justice of God, bringing hope to our world. Instead of a justice filled with wrath, Jesus overflowed with forgiveness. Instead of a justice filled with anger, Jesus patiently calmed life's storms. Instead of a justice filled with retribution, Jesus proclaimed, "Neither do I condemn you." As an act of restorative justice, Jesus left his seat of honor, humbled himself, taking a towel and basin and knelt down to bless those who would fail their Lord. Remember, this is how Jesus sees justice. When Jesus blessed the Passover bread, Jesus did not forget those who were still being oppressed. Jesus broke the bread and distributed it to all who were present, inviting them to take and eat. This is my body, broken and given for you. Remember, this is how Jesus sees justice. When Jesus blessed the Passover wine, Jesus did not forget those whose hearts were still hardened and uncaring. Jesus poured the wine and distributed it to all who were present, inviting them to take and drink. This is the cup of my blood, poured out for you, in recognition of God's eternal covenant of grace that promises the forgiveness of every sin. Remember, this is how Jesus sees justice. We want to view justice like Jesus. We want to live justice as Christ did. So we make this proclamation of the Gospel. Christ's justice is unstoppable. Christ has died. Christ is risen. Christ will come again. Righteous hope, unstoppable justice, use these gifts of bread and wine to become for us the body and blood of Christ, the promise of hope. Righteous hope, unstoppable justice, use our lives hungering and thirsting for righteousness to become for others your church, the physical body and devoted bride of Christ, the promise of hope. Righteous hope, unstoppable justice, use this Advent season to open the hearts of your people. Call them to a new level of faithfulness and make them a beacon of hope in their community, in our community.
