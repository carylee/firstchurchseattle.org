## Sermon: What's Good?
**Preacher:** Sophia Agtarap
**Scripture:** Micah 6:1-8

[31:47]
He has told you, oh mortal, what is good. And what does the Lord require of you, but to do justice and to love kindness and to walk humbly with your God. What's good, First Church. When I was asked by your former pastor Jeremy, if I'd be interested in sharing a word with you all today, my reaction was of course, an enthusiastic yes. After all, this was a congregation, if some of you might remember, many years ago, I think about 10, as folks were reminding me earlier, that this was a congregation that has been dear to me as I nurtured my own call to ministry as a lay person who explored and then left the ordination process and eventually seminary, what it would look like to, to say yes, right, to the ways that God was calling me in some way to ministry. And I will add that it doesn't always have to be ordination in the United Methodist Church, right? It can be as a lay person who is called to serve in all the ways that they have the capacity to do.

And so it was this congregation who allowed me to find a home where my gifts of hospitality and communication and outreach, and where I invited others to develop a practice of curiosity and wonder as a natural part of our faith journeys. And so as I started to prepare what I would share with you this morning, I of course had many questions, some of them being what text had others preached on, what themes were emerging and, Jackie wrote back, "You're the first in the series." Oh, and you'll be following Jeremy's last service with us, which was a little heavy, so something uplifting would be nice. So no, no pressure. And yet, I hope to find this balance when I saw the title of this series, I hope to find a balance between, you know, for a land Maria energy frolicking through the woods, because naturally The Sound of Music came to mind, and a good word that invites some conversation and application for our text from the prophet Micah and from the Gospel of Mark for this morning. Will you pray with me?

Holy one, meet us where we are, how we are and who we are. Tilling us the fertile soil where seeds of justice and mercy and kindness, the size of a mustard seed, might take root. And may the words of my mouth and the meditation of all of our hearts be acceptable to you, our rock and our redeemer. Amen. There were a few passages in scripture that I memorized growing up. Um, so you don't really want me on your Bible trivia team despite being a pastor's kid and, um, one who spent many days of the week at church related activities. But, if there is one passage that continues to be a conversation partner in my life, it is Micah chapter 6 verse 8. The Lord has told you, oh mortal, what is good. And what does the Lord require of you, but to do justice and to love kindness and to walk humbly with your God. We don't have time to go through all of Micah, but I invite you not to just start at verse 8, right, but queue it up to the beginning, which we heard earlier, at the top of chapter 6, where the foundation and mountains are called on to serve as jury to Israel's actions and maybe even malpractice, if you will. This is no small claims court business, but a trial that involves all of creation and the cosmos to bear witness. And if you want to go back even further to chapter 3, we can get an idea of the widespread religiosity and spectacle that is commonplace among religious leaders of the time. The grand standing and the posturing, the upholding of inequity, the misuse of power, the inclination to self-preservation and arrogance rather than to collective care and liberation. They said all the right things and went through all of the motions of what good religious leaders were to do.

And we don't have a full list of all of those transgressions, but there are a few that were called out in earlier chapters. Coveting fields and homes and seizing them. Violence on the poor. Taking bribes and selling out for money. You would think that we were scrolling through our timelines on social media and news, right, that these are echoes of what we are seeing and experiencing today. It's not too much of a stretch to draw some parallels to contemporary issues. To occupation, to land theft, to assault on those who are low income, low wealth, those who do not earn a living wage. Undocumented kin. God is taking God's people to trial for breaking their covenant. And the folks are so guilty. They don't even try to defend themselves. And so they just go right to, okay, what do you want us to do? What's your favorite sacrifice? How can we make this better? As they start rattling off the various sacrificial offerings to make up for their actions and transgressions.

One question I often ask when I'm involved in any sort of planning is, what does, fill in the blank, look, sound and feel like? And I started to ask, and what could it taste like? So I asked a question once, what does justice look, sound and feel like? And taste like? Someone said, it tastes like a really high-end chocolate bar. A good mouthfeel, something that is satiating and delicious. So I asked this question because I believe it opens us up to imagination beyond binaries and ways that we have preferred to do things out of comfort, out of control, out of convenience, out of habit, and even what privileges us. I invite us to think about what the desired thing or outcome could look like. What could it sound like, what it feel like, what it would taste like, and work backwards to say, okay, what then do we need to do for us to get there? For us to experience that thing we say we want or need or know could be possible.

We can rattle off a lot of things, right? A healthy congregation, healthy cities, healthy budgets. A full life. What is the radical imagining that can happen and that can then guide us and our communities to places that we know can be possible, even if they don't yet exist in their fullness. Perhaps the people in this passage could have been helped by asking some of these questions. I'm in school now, um, working on my doctorate in education and last year made a shift to focus on the intersections of equity, inclusion, justice, diversity and sustainability because I believe those pieces are interlocking. I'm reminded of words from educator and environmental activist who you will soon host, Bill McKibben, who wrote this about these interlocking systems, and I hope is a good word for us this morning. The job of people who care about the future, which is another way of saying environmentalists, is to let everyone breathe easier. But that simply can't happen without all kinds of change. Some of it looks like solar panels for rooftops. Some of it looks like radically reimagined police forces. But all of it is hitched together. Who and what are we hitching ourselves to, as we seek to do and be what the Lord requires?

The COVID-19 pandemic that we are still in has taught us a lot about our interdependence and just how disconnected we are from one another. From our bodies, our spirits, and our communities. Some people heard about the idea of mutual aid for the first time as we sought to show up for one another in different ways, in ways that we hadn't yet explored or felt the necessity to. And nature has a thing or two to teach us. After all, these wise and ancient systems have been figuring out what support and mutual thriving looks like in practice for many, many years. Much research shows that trees have a way of communicating with one another. If you are a Lord of the Rings fan, I picture the ents, who are the ancient shepherds and protectors of the forest. Maybe there's something we can learn from our forest kin about how we will choose to be together.

Dr. Ayana Elizabeth Johnson and Dr. Katherine Wilkinson in the book All We Can Save write this. By recognizing at last the ubiquity of sharing and chaperoning, by acknowledging the fact that communal traits are quite natural, we get to see ourselves anew. We can return to our role as nurturers, each a helper among helpers in this planetary story of collaborative healing. So, if the Lord requires us to do justice and to love kindness and to walk humbly with our God, what would that look, sound and feel and taste like as we chaperone and steward God's kingdom on earth as it is in heaven? I want you to take just a moment to like actually sit with that. What are the sounds, what are the feelings, what are the smells that come to mind as you think of what the fullness of God's kingdom here on earth could look like, could feel like, could taste like.

What would justice look like when a superfund site that has not been cared for in the south part of your city where mostly black and brown residents live? What would justice look like when our policies and our practices are out of alignment with who we say we are and who we want to be? What would justice look like when our Oceana siblings' nations will soon be underwater due to rising sea levels? What does justice look like when it feels inconvenient?

A few weeks ago I presented at a conference on race and ethnicity where we were led by our Kanaka EV or our Native Hawaiian partners. They reminded us of Aloha Aina. A love of land and practice that accompanies and encompasses the Hawaiian worldview of reciprocal and familiar relationships between people and land where in mountains even are kin. And so I invite us to look at this text in Micah and in Mark with that lens of right relationship to land and our natural environments and to one another. What could the kingdom of God look like if we believe that all of creation were our relations? What would justice and mercy feel like if it were not rooted in extractive practices or transaction but in a deep connection and commitment to one another's thriving and joy? There is no list of approved actions to get to doing this justice, loving kindness, and walking humbly work. But we have some guidance. And that is to love God and neighbor. So what could it look like and sound like to commit daily to walking humbly with God? I don't know how many of you like to take walks. I know when we first went on lockdown, I ended up taking all of my meetings on Zoom like on my phone while I was walking around the neighborhood. It allowed me, of course, to, to get out of my seat, right? Cause you could easily spend 10 hours a day just kind of sitting there, moving back and forth to refill coffee and water and come back and the sun rises and sets and you're still in the same spot. And so what would that practice look like to literally and physically walk with God? Through your city, through your home, through your neighborhood, through your place of work or worship? What could it look and sound like to commit daily to walking humbly with God? And I'm not talking about going to all the meetings, volunteering for all the things, which are part of this too. But who are the people of God called to be in this moment?

John Wesley, the founder of this denomination called United Methodists once wrote, I'm not afraid that the people called Methodists should ever cease to exist either in Europe or America. But I'm afraid lest they should only exist as a dead sect, having the form of religion without the power. And this undoubtedly will be the case unless they hold fast both the doctrine, spirit and discipline with which they first set out. Do we have the form of religion without the transformative power that is a response to the ways that the Holy Spirit is moving in and through us and out into the world? Even amidst our unfaithfulness, God reminds us through generations of their faithfulness throughout the ages. But God already made it plain how to live and what to do. What God is looking for in each of us. It's quite simple. Do what is fair and just to your neighbor. Be compassionate and loyal in your love and don't take yourself too seriously. Take God seriously. This message translation offers the same Micah 6:8 text. But perhaps in a way that we can keep returning to, right? As we reflect on what it means to walk humbly with God. God has made it plain in what God is looking for in each one of us, and it's quite simple, the text says, to do what is fair and just to our neighbor. And I would extend this to all of our kin, people and environment, all the critters, all the flora and fauna, and to take God seriously. May it be so. Amen.
