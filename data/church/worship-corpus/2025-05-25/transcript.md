## Sermon: "We Must Become a Triple Braided Cord"
**Preacher:** Tara Miller
**Scripture:** Matthew 13:1-8, 18-23
[29:33]
Good morning. The phrase triple braided cord has been a running joke of my friend group over the past few years. I never thought I would actually be preaching on that topic and I texted them this morning, they're like, oh my gosh, guys, we're doing this. When I first heard that phrase, it was in the context of a wedding where the pastor said that the three elements of the cord were the bride, the groom and Jesus in the middle. My two friends and I grew up in the Bible belt. So this description took us back to the purity culture days, you do my use this? Oh, sorry, okay. This description took us back to the purity culture days of the 90s and the early 2000s, where a teacher would tap between you and your middle school boyfriend who you'd only just begun to hold hands with and remind you to save some room for Jesus. I could preach a lot of sermons on the trauma of purity culture, but I will save that for another day.

As I was looking for scripture verses to tie into what I wanted to share today, ones that emphasize the power of community and collective action, I was surprised to find that exact verse and how very differently it's used in Ecclesiastes than how it was presented to us at this wedding. The chapter starts by naming the overwhelming weight of oppression, of seeing power continue to be hoarded by the few, and then wielded against the people they oppress. Does that sound familiar? It feels hopeless, the oppressed have no comfort. I didn't include it in the reading but the author goes on to say that the dead are happier than the living and even happier still are those who were never born. That's how bad things have gotten. The author shares about how people are constantly working and can't enjoy their lives. Is anyone feeling that right now?

Then it ends with this series of ways that two are better than one and a three strand cord or a triple braided cord is not quickly broken. Together our work is more effective. Together we lift each other up when one is taken down. Together we weather the storm. Together we fight back. I'm sure many of you have heard the success story of Tent City 4 over the past few weeks. This was an incredible example of how knitting the community's efforts together, holding firm to their values, refusing to stand down, and honestly refusing to be gaslit, put the shame back on the city and county officials to the point where they conceded to Tent City 4's demands.

Share and Wheel have operated self-managed shelters for decades, currently including two tent cities, and they have continued to assert that having little, their word is their currency. They sign contracts with faith communities or public land for as long as the community agrees. When it's time to leave, they leave. They don't ask for an extension, they honor the time they've been given and they move on. What happened over the past few weeks is that despite Tent City 4 following all of the steps required in order to prepare to sign a lease for public land through King County Regional Homelessness Authority, something they've been working on since February, which included hosting community meetings and talking with neighbors who have been overwhelmingly supported. KCRHA refused to put the vote for their lease agenda on the governing board agenda and a few days before they were due to move, which would have killed their ability to leave.

They were rightly outraged. In a system that's designed for people experiencing poverty and homelessness to fail, even a situation where they played by all the rules of the game, they're about to be denied a place to go. This included over a hundred adults and 17 children. In that situation, one could easily feel defeated. They could try to negotiate for something less than desirable. Or they could show up alone to say their piece but not have success in moving the people in power. But that's not what Tent City 4 did. Tent City 4 harvested the power of collective community where folks in the camp, supporters at their current location at Seattle Mennonite Church, people of faith and conscience across Seattle, local business owners and leaders from other shelters came together to present their values firmly and without compromise. And they won.

I was able to attend and testified the KCRHA Governing Board meeting. They started the meeting by saying, I know this is an issue of Tent City 4 that they're bringing up a lot of feelings for people today, but we're not going to talk about that today. And as the public comment came up, over 25 people in person and 11 people online ignored that directive and spoke one after the other about how important this lease is. The only reason the public attended this meeting was to insist that Tent City 4's words were heard. Following public comment, members of the governing board, which included city and county council members, as well as Mayor Harrell, pointed a lot of fingers and seemed to be confused about what their own process is and who's responsible for approving the lease.

Mayor Harrell also said to the laughter of us all, I, like, only heard about this yesterday, which was a complete lie. Many groups called and emailed his office in the weeks prior, including the church council on May 7th, and this was May 15th. Multiple people through this process also falsely claimed that Pastor Megan at Seattle Mennonite Church would extend Tent City 4's contract and Mayor Harrell said at the meeting that it was a shame that she was choosing to close down the shelter. This also was bogus. Of course, the church would be willing to keep hosting as long as needed. But the close partnership that Seattle Mennonite shares with Tent City 4, and their approach to genuine relationship and building collective power is rooted in following the leadership of the people most impacted.

In this case, that meant that they followed the leadership of Tent City 4, who as they said, were choosing to prioritize their word and to hold the city and county accountable to their words, who up until the week before, appeared to fully prepared to let them move to public land. They were clear on their worth, their values, and that they had done nothing wrong. And in the end, this worked. Joey, the other co-executive director and I, were prepared to show up with faith leaders in our network for a potential arrestable action that Saturday morning if KCRHA had not changed course. But just as we shared the call to action, Tent City 4 received the news that they would be extended a stay of one to six months. So instead, we got to celebrate with them on their moving day and help them start to unload at their new site.

There are still things that they need and ways to support, so if you're interested, I'm happy to share some of that after the service. This is also how the church council organizes, spanning the various areas of our work. And this is how the big C Church needs to operate in the midst of the ever-more frightening era our nation is in. As we look at the parable of the sower, I see a range of how people of faith have responded to systems of oppression. There are those of us who completely miss it. I would put my community growing up in that category. They can't even see that there's a problem. A truth is presented and a bird snatches it up.

I think a lot of people of faith in the PNW do hear it. They do care. We're very educated. We know what's going on in the world. But we end up being the seeds among the rocks or the seeds among the thorns. The ones among the rocks have an emotional pull when we hear about what's going on with our neighbors. We might even start a book club to learn more about the history of the problem and the stories of real people who are being harmed in our community. But that's it. It stays in the book. We might have a few enlightening conversations with our families about what we learned. But what happens when ICE begins snatching people because they are? What happens when police kill another black or brown person? What happens when students at UDub are being targeted by the President of the United States?

We don't know. We don't have roots in deep relationship with these communities and are not grounded within our own power enough to respond. Or maybe we're stuck among thorns. We're keenly aware of what's happening. We've seen this coming for decades now. We see the patterns of history repeating itself. We too may be the outspoken ones in our families who share what's really going on in the world. But we're scared. We're scared to take a risk to our safety, to our reputation, to stand in the fear and do it anyway. If that's you, that's my tip for you because I'm often this person. You're not going to get rid of the fear, you just have to act in the midst of it. So how do we get to the good soil?

I'm not telling you to leave this room and immediately go do something. That's not actually the most helpful nor is it wise. Through our work in recent years, we at the church council have learned that we need to live into a difficult paradox around urgency if we're going to organize effectively for transformational and sustainable change. There's so much urgency that comes with deep-rooted oppression. People need relief now, justice now, resources now, repair now. And we're not going to do it well if we respond with our own urgency. That tends to result in performative white savior type actions that don't benefit those most impacted.

Instead, we act with intention, investing deeply in relationships so that we are building collective power. We model this well through our Faith Land initiative. FLI gathers three to five faith communities each year for a seven-month learning cohort that roots leaders in anti-racism and organizing principles and shares tools for engaging their entire congregation as well as their communities in this process, toward community stewardship of their land and assets. We've seen faith communities who act quickly to start developing affordable housing on their property without taking that intentional time to discern together, who then have to restart when they realize they're not all on the same page, or did not spend enough time researching their project.

Even after the seven months in the cohort, many communities are not ready to jump into a building project. We continue accompanying them in the years following until they have set down roots and are ready to proceed. We did something similar internally regarding how we do our immigration organizing. We hosted an accompaniment hotline during the last Trump administration for volunteers to support migrants as they attend sensitive meetings. And over time, when we realized that we were duplicating efforts of the Washington Immigrant Solidarity Network, WISN, and many of our volunteers went on to form the Jewish Coalition of Immigrant Justice, we listened for what else the community needed. And what what emerged was our Grupo de Solidaridad, our solidarity group.

Grupo came out of folks that we were accompanying who said, actually, what we need is a place to gather in community, in convivencia, to build relationship, share culture and work together in supporting our communities. And now Grupo is the foundation to our immigration organizing work. And they along with WISN and other immigrant justice organizations inform the direction of our work. As we look to reigniting the sanctuary network as one tool to combat mass deportations facing migrant communities, we've learned from sanctuary communities from the first Trump administration, as well as from our Faith Land initiative, that intentional discernment is going to be key to doing this well and sustainably.

Being a sanctuary congregation is much more than opening the doors to house someone. This needs to be a strategy for the long run. And so we want faith communities to take the time to be clear among their membership how this fits into their mission and values, to be rooted in their rights, and to have the muscle memory for how to respond if ICE comes to their doors. And to be committed to relationship with the person or family being hosted within their doors. This includes things not prying like not prying into their lives and their stories so that the less you know, the more you can keep them safe. It means creating a wider network of volunteer support that's spread out across the community so no one is overly burdened. This creates the sustainability of human resources.

It means being committed to welcoming all migrants, not just those who meet the model minority image. So in the coming weeks, we will be offering an expedited discernment cohort for those interested in offering sanctuary. I know, you know that it is beyond time for the big C Church to step up in big ways. We cannot afford to be seeds that get eaten by birds or that fall among rocks or thorns. We have to root in deep. And we have to meet the urgencies of the moment with deeply committed, intentionally built community power.

I'm going to end on one last image of that triple braided cord. One final way that the pastor at the wedding got it wrong. A triple braided cord is not just a cord of three strands, bride, groom, Jesus. It's three braids which are then braided together. So it's really nine cords. That's really strong. What do we need to do as our small communities, our small C Church, in order to make just one of those braids? And then how do we weave our braid among the power chords held by other communities? People have been asking us where are faith communities in this moment? They want us to be there. They know that we have the power just like Tent City 4 to not compromise on our values. So we need to step up even if we're scared. We need to do it intentionally and we need to do it rooted in deep relationship. We've seen time and again that such a strongly woven cord will not be quickly broken. Amen.
