**Preacher:** Rev. Doug Cunningham
**Scripture:** Mark 1:9-15

It is a pleasure to be with you. Uh, it's a joy to be with uh relatives, friends, colleagues. Wong Jee and I have ministered together for a couple of decades back in New York. And it's so good to be with her and and uh Ben. We had a wonderful day uh hiking yesterday and uh, so I am really grateful to have this opportunity to share with you about this memoir that I've written uh over 40 years of ministry and it's really been a lot about awakening to some of the realities of our society and how they impact the church. And then joining with colleagues that have really inspired me and we've worked together to disrupt racism in the church. It's so good to hear this week from the Council on Bishops of the United Methodist Church that the four amendments that were passed at General Conference 2024 have been ratified by the annual conferences and now become part of the Constitution of the United Methodist Church. And particularly significant for this day is Amendment number three which calls the United Methodist Church to step up into its role in combating racism and white supremacy and colonialism and imperialism in the church and in the society. And if this is to be our mandate, if this is to become a priority, it's going to transform the United Methodist Church. The United Methodist Church has always been considerably whiter than the society around it. And our structures and our policies have emboldened uh white supremacy rather than, which is the exact opposite of the gospel of Jesus Christ. So I want to start with my own experience of awakening. And it really started for me in seminary at the Pacific School of Religion in Berkeley, California. Um, I grew up in Columbus, Ohio and I had an an experience meeting Jesus when I was 16 on a youth work camp. It was a profound experience where um, the we were working with uh folks in Appalachia that uh struggled with poverty that we were working in a house where there was no indoor plumbing, where there was a leak in the roof. We were there to repair the roof. But the people I connected with had such a deep sense of the home, of this being their home. And I led a Bible study midweek. My pastor invited me to do that on First Corinthians 13 where I invited my high school friends to consider this love that Paul talks about, where we are not insisting on our, on our own way. That that phrase in in uh First Corinthians 13 so struck me, a love that does not insist on its own way. And I had grown up in a suburb that uh, you know, my parents and teachers and everyone around insisted that ours was the best, that we lived in the best community. This all white community was the best community. We had the best high schools and we had the best lives. And working with these folks in Appalachia, I wondered if I was missing something. That that didn't seem to be true. And what did it mean to really enter into a love that does not insist on its own way. So when I felt a call to go to seminary, I went to the Pacific School of Religion because I wanted to get as far away from Ohio as I could. I wanted to get out of this suburb. I was looking for radical change in my life. I felt like Berkeley, California would probably be the best place to go. So I enrolled in the Pacific School of Religion. And sure enough by my second semester, I was taking a course on the Book of Revelation, the last book in the New Testament, taught by Father Daniel Berrigan. Now, Father Berrigan on the first day of class stood behind the podium with his gray hair cropped close and his sparkle in his eye. And he said to us, "Faith is not about where your head is at. Faith is not about where your heart is at. Faith is about where your ass is at." And his ass had been in jail. He was part of the Catonsville Nine. He had been arrested for protesting the Vietnam War. He got a three year sentence. He didn't even show up for jail. He went underground. He uh was at a Cornell University dramatic presentation of the Last Supper. And he hid in a, one of these giant papier-mâché uh puppets of the Apostles and slipped into the woods and on to the top 10 most wanted list of the FBI. Well, they caught up with him and put him in jail. So that's where his ass had been. And fresh from that experience, he was teaching us about the Book of Revelation where the imagery is about the beast. And the beast is the empire. Of course, in those days it was the Roman Empire oppressing the people. And the Christian faith was seen as a threat to the empire. And Christians were being persecuted because they stood up to the empire. And he said we need to stand up to our empire. It's the very essence of the Christian church to stand against the values of empire and stand instead for the ways of the kingdom or the reign or the empire of God. Well, for him that meant opposing the threat of nuclear weapons. He saw that as the great idol of the empire. Sucking up all the resources from education and from health care and pouring them into this idolatry of weaponry and controlling the world by force. And he said to be a Christian is to stand up against this idolatry of nuclear weapons. And there were activists in the classroom with us ready to invite us to evening teach-ins where we could learn more about the weaponry of the United States and stand up against it. And then he told us that on Ash Wednesday, he was inviting us to protest, to commit civil disobedience, to go down to the University of California, Berkeley campus and to sit in at the Chancellor's office to protest the university's role in the production and the research of nuclear weapons. And I was like, oh, whoa. I was, I was like I, I enjoyed hearing about all this. But this kind of call to civil disobedience did not uh sit well with my, I had a kind of a polite religion in in uh the suburbs. I wasn't ready for this kind of confrontation. But I prayed about it. And I, I really felt convicted by his teaching. And so after a lot of prayer, I decided to join this Ash Wednesday demonstration. So we started with worship. Daniel Ellsberg talked about the Pentagon Papers releasing those. And our theology professor, Robert McAfee Brown, talked about connections with oppression across Latin America, and people rising up. And, and Berrigan called us to beat our swords into plowshares. So down to the University of California, of course, we went and sat in at the Chancellor's office and we were arrested. And boy, I felt like I had arrived. I, this is what I was looking for. I mean, here I am sitting among these great activists and, you know, learning about their struggle. So we talked about our upcoming, you know, appearance in the court and all this kind of thing. And I was really quite engaged with this community of activists. But one thing I noticed was that all these activists were white. And some didn't sit well with me because part of the reason I wanted to get out of this suburb I was living in was because everyone was white. And I was I knew there was something beyond this world that I had lived in. And I got to talking about this with uh Kathleen Jimenez, who was a student and a member of the multiracial students caucus at PSR. And asked if I could meet with the multi-students, multiracial students caucus. And so she invited me to come. And I, they had a little business and then they invited me and I shared about this experience. And I said, you know, we have learned about how nuclear weapons is draining resources that need to be gone to more important causes. And, you know, it was such a meaningful experience for me to participate in this. But I was wondering why some of you weren't interested in that. Well, there was this silence in the room and tension. And finally Don Matthews spoke up. And he said, "You don't appear curious at all about what we're doing. We're engaged in a battle against racism here in our school. We have one Black professor, and he's up for tenure and he's having a hard time getting tenure. And we don't have any courses in urban ministry. And, you know, we have a lot of racism right here in our school. And you come in and you're not even curious about that. But you're telling us that we should be doing what you're doing, when you're not interested in what we're doing." And he got up and walked out of the room. And I was dumbfounded. I was confused. I was embarrassed. I was ashamed. I had come in thinking in my mind that I was building bridges. And here I was creating division. It was a Kairos moment for me. Kairos is a very important word in the scripture that we read this morning. Jesus is starting his public ministry. And he comes out of the wilderness after prayer and discernment and says, "The time is fulfilled." Kairos, the word for time. In Greek, there are two words. There's Chronos, chronological time, and then there's Kairos, the fulfillment of time. It's like Chronos, it's like the party's supposed to start at 7:30. So Chronos, that's when you're supposed to come. But at 7:30, there's nobody actually there. So it's about 8:15 when people are starting to come in and the food and the dancing and the music and talking and that's Kairos. That's the fulfillment of time. That's when things begin to happen. Jesus is announcing the Kairos moment, the fulfillment of time. And what he says about that is that it's an invitation to repent and believe. Now, we kind of have certain connotations around the word repent. For us, there's a lot of shame in repentance. There's a lot of guilt. There's a lot of, you know, kind of heaviness in it. But it's not really what metanoia is about. Meta, change, noia, your mind or your consciousness. It's about learning to see. It's about seeing a new vision. It's about, Thomas Merton used to talk about it's about seeing the way God sees. Gustavo Gutierrez, the liberation theologian, uh, used to say, but metanoia's got to be rooted in the soil of life, of history. It's a call of transformation away from the systems and the structures of oppression to the way of God. I mean, Jesus describes the kingdom of God as the opposite of the empires of the world. Jesus says those who are last in society become first in the kingdom of God. Those who are first in society become last in the kingdom of God. It's about a great reversal. It's not just a kind of a personal kind of thing, but it's a transformation of the world. And it's a change of our allegiance. That we no longer give our allegiance to the empire. We give our allegiance to the way of God. So it's a complete transformation. I write about this experience of awakening. That moment of Kairos for me. Don brought a challenge to my life, something that I had not understood before, some information that I was missing. He was inviting me to a complete transformation of consciousness. I think those particularly those of us who are white, I mean, we grow up with this white supremacy, this racism embedded in us from day one. To the point that it becomes normalized in our lives. We don't even recognize it when we see it. I was talking to a Filipino colleague the other day who's a pastor of a church in Sacramento, uh, mostly white church. And he was saying that uh, one of the things he's trying to get across to them is that Jesus is not white. You know, I grew up in a church where all the pictures of Jesus were white. I mean, this is a most fundamental misrepresentation of the gospel. From the get go, Jesus is not white. We know that. But he said that when he would preach from the pulpit that Jesus is not white, you know, it's hard to deny it. But two of the white members of the church asked to meet with him in his office and they said, "We don't want you to say that anymore." You know, how can you be a Christian when you deny something so fundamental about who Jesus is? About what the gospel was about? So this idea of metanoia, this transformation, this change, Don set me in motion. Now, I avoided the change because metanoia is not easy. And I was physically avoiding Don. Don and I ate in the same dining hall. There were only about two dozen of us that ate in the dining hall. So breakfast, lunch, and dinner, we're in the same room. I'm so embarrassed. You know, I'm just not ready to interact with him. Finally one night at the dining table, there was a seat open next to him. And I went up to him and I said, "Don, do you mind if I sit down?" He nodded. I said, "I appreciate the conversation we had the other day. And I apologize for my insensitivity." And he looked at me over his glass, and he said, "The question is, do you have eyes to see and ears to hear?" Well, that started a relationship. I mean, I had a lot of good professors at the Pacific School of Religion, but Don was probably my most profound teacher. Because once this metanoia movement drove us into relationship, I mean, first of all, I went to an anti-racism workshop. And then he and I talked about it. He was very interested in her pedagogy. And then it just, the conversation kept deepening. Then he invited myself and Rebecca, and another student to a study of the sermons of Dr. Martin Luther King. Finally, by our third year in seminary, we put a big table and blockaded the President's office because of some actions the seminary had taken that negatively impacted some Black women on staff there. But it was a transforming experience. And once your eyes are open, you know, it's hard to unsee what's there. And I saw it in my time in Global Ministries in in the Philippines. The, you know, when we would approach the presidential palace, the, the dictator that was really oppressing the people. And the people were rising up. And all the soldiers around the presidential palace have weapons from the United States. I mean, the armolites, the shields, the, the water cannons, everything that they were, all the military hardware that they were using to keep their own people down, coming from the United States. In the Philippines they called it US Imperialism. It's not much different from white supremacy in the United States. What we have done in the United States to indigenous people, stealing the land, uh, enslavement, Jim Crow, uh, is has been globalized in many ways through our our colonization. Jesus calls us to change, to transform, to transform our complete consciousness. I mean, we can see in the society right now the impact of white supremacy unleashed. We can see the destructive components. And the call of Jesus is to stand with what we know is true. Deep in our hearts, we know that human compassion's what we're called to. Deep in our hearts, we know that we're called to cross boundaries. There's an energy crossing boundaries and learning about one another's ways and cultures. And there's so much learning to be done, so much coming together and and enlivening and engaging the gifts and the talents of all people to build a world of love and abundance and and sharing and, you know, we know this in our hearts. And yet the ways of the empire become so entrenched that it's so hard for us to see. That's the constant call of Jesus, the call to repent, to metanoia. Repent. Turn away from the ways that we know are violent and wrong, and to turn toward the difficult, bold love that Jesus is calling us to. It's not easy to buck against the grain. And that's certainly one of the things that, uh, deters us from doing what we know we're called to. We get at it unfamiliar territory. And in unfamiliar territory, sometimes we make mistakes. Sometimes we say things that don't come across right. Sometimes we have to assume a humble posture and not an all-knowing posture. But this Jesus is clear is the way that leads to life. Amen.
