## Sermon: My Top 5 Favorite Things About Seattle
**Preacher:** Reagan Page
**Scripture:** Psalm 133, Ecclesiastes 3:1-8

So, first I do have to clarify with my introduction. Jackie said we were co-chairs, but it was like she did 90% of the work and I did 10% of the work. So I couldn't have done it without Jackie. Also, I have my tissues up here because every time I talk about Seattle I start to get a little weepy. So with the sermon series being a few of my favorite things, the first thing that kind of came to mind was talking about my favorite parts of Seattle since me preaching it ended up being so close to when I was leaving. One Sunday left after this, so I'm like, oh, this is perfect. I can just talk about what I like to do. But it feels kind of just like yesterday that I came here. I've only been here for a year and a half really, but it feels like I've been here for years. It was just yesterday, it was just April when I was deciding to go to school and when I was going to move and everything about that. But I really didn't want to leave, so it was a hard decision.

I just finished my job on Thursday and have a lot of packing to do still, but kind of have been looking at my long to-do list of things in Seattle and running around with the like a chicken with my head cut off. Yesterday I was listening to a song in the car called "Pictures on a Wall" by Ira Woft and I like cried when I heard it because I think it really encapsulates kind of what I feel about this sermon, because in the line she has, "Washington'll always be begging for a piece of me to stay." So trying to write this sermon has actually given me a good chance to reflect on my service in Seattle and what it's meant to me and how it's changed me. I could go on for hours about all the different things I love about this city, but I've always preferred shorter sermons, so today I'll be talking about my top five favorite things.

Number one is Pike Place Market. It's a classic. It's something that most people would name, if you ask them to name like one or two things about Seattle, you'd probably get the Space Needle and Pike Place. But to me it shows so much more than just like a tourist destination. It shows the hidden beauty in everything, finding the intricacies of a place that has so many different things going on, that so many people only see a part of it. But there's so much more and every time I'm there I discover something new that makes me love it even more. There's that juxtaposition of tourists taking pictures while you're just trying to buy some fruit, or listening to people cheer as fish are being thrown as the butcher asks, "Where do you work because you come in here a lot?" Don and Joe's Meats, they have trail mix that's just meat and cheese. It's great. So getting to know and love Pike Place makes me think of a quote that I first heard at a camp years ago that I didn't know was initially contributed to a pastor because I only heard the first part of the quote. The first part is, "To be loved but not known is comforting but superficial. To be known and not loved is our greatest fear." Now that is the quote as I initially learned it. It was used to describe developing impactful relationships and bringing your full self to the table, even if it's scary because the right people will love all of you. However, the full quote is in relation to God and the full love that we receive on that end. The full quote is, "To be loved but not known is comforting but superficial. To be known and not loved is our greatest fear. But to be fully known and truly loved, well, it's a lot like being loved by God. It is what we need more than anything."

Everyone needs love, but that means everyone needs to give love. If we want to be fully known and fully loved by others, we need to fully known and fully love others as we feel God does to us.

Number two, public transit. It's okay. You laugh, it's not the best in this. But we have it. And having it is so important to me because the connection that public transportation can bring is those like little level things that you don't think about a lot, but really enriches your experience. The small connections that you get, you have the bus driver who's always driving your route to work who once in a while when you're gone will be like, "Where were you?" The people who wait at the same bus stop as you, you start to recognize them. When they stop coming, you're like, "I wonder what happened to them. Where what do they do now?" And the people you may otherwise find yourself avoiding, even though those relationships are important. I know especially in Seattle it can be really hard as we balance like safety with compassion for others. And I think of a lot of conversations I've had with homeless people on the bus talking about addiction, talking about other things and they talk about how great it is just to have someone to talk back to them because so many times people look away. And I'm not any better than that. I don't do it a lot but public transit is something where it kind of shifts that balance because you got to sit somewhere. It's also it's accessibility to people who can't afford cars or parking cost. It connects you to a city in the place that you can't be connected while driving. I love watching the window on my daily ride and intimately knowing this route while usually seeing something new still as well. I several things on my long, long to-do list are things that I just went by on the bus every day because I'm just like, I wonder how that gelato tastes. But it was very good. I just got it last week. It also allows time to connect with yourself and the city. It gives me that time to reflect because when I drive, I get angry and think about driving and safety. But when I'm on the bus, it's just my time to take a breather for the day as I get ready to go to work or go home. And gives me that time to reflect on how work was, how I'm feeling and especially now, all the changes that are about to happen.

And don't get me wrong, as I said, there is a lot of room for improvement. As there is in all different steps that we're taking to develop a better community for all. But this is a good start. It is a reminder that we are allowed to celebrate what we have already accomplished while working for a better world.

Number three is Kerry Park and Marshall Park. That's technically two, but they're like three blocks away from each other. So these two parks are in Queen Anne, um, which is where I live up near right, I'm in the Queen Anne United Methodist Church parsonage. Um, they have an intentional living community. So every time I walk around my neighborhood, I tell myself I'm going to take a different route because I like to look at all the houses, like to see new things. But nearly every time I go, no matter where I start and if I do start differently, I end up deciding that the route will eventually need to go by one or both of these parks. I have more memories in these parks than probably any other public space in Seattle. Excuse me. I've gone there and read, prayed, spent time with friends, shown visitors, taken pictures, and spread the word about the lesser known of the two, saying, "If you like Kerry Park, you need to go down to Marshall Park. It's beautiful." I still, you should. It's it's on it's right on the edge. You see the Olympics. Um, it's great for sunrise and sunset. I think all of them are though. So. I still remember the first time I saw Kerry Park on a rainy December morning. The bus I was supposed to take to work just never came. So I angrily walked down the hill to try to find the next closest bus stop so I could get to work. As I fumed and complained in my head, I remember turning the corner and seeing Kerry Park and all my anger just melted away because I was shocked, because I had never seen this before and it was so gorgeous. Um, I took a picture. I was like, this is awesome. And that's when you couldn't hardly see anything because it was so cloudy and foggy. But I just kept coming back. And even now, a year and a half later, I take a picture most every time I go there because the awe has not yet faded.

I want to always be able to have that awe and wonder for the little things in life. We were created to have this capacity to feel awe and wonder, and I fully intend to use as much of it as I can. I look upon what humans have created, enveloped by what God has created. How can I feel anything else in this city? Kerry Park was actually donated to the city in 1927 by Albert and Catherine Carey, who were said to do it, quote unquote, "so that all who stop here may enjoy its view." And every day I appreciate their choice to make this public land that allows a community to come together to see what is being created. As I admire this beauty, however, I sometimes begin to think about all the disparities and inequalities that exist within our city, in our world. I look down and think about people in need asking for compassion, those who can't afford homes or healthcare. The mountains that have bear witness to the impacts of climate change, habitat destruction and environmental degradation. Racism, sexism, homophobia. Our faith calls us to be agents of change, advocating for justice and standing in solidarity with the marginalized. When we thank God for this beautiful place, we must also ask, "What can I do to make it better?"

Number four, Blue Highway Games. I was just there for like 12 hours on Friday because I really like board games, but it's more than that. This is one of the places that I've become a regular around Seattle. If you haven't become a regular anywhere yet, I recommend finding that because those another set of little connections. I could say, yeah, it is just a board game shop and I love board games, but that would sell it short. What makes it so special to me is that it's focused on creating intentional community space. They specifically worked to have people who are less represented in the gaming world to feel welcome, which for reference, as many things, is most people outside of straight white men. They like their board games. And they also encourage people to meet new people and constantly foster new connections over these shared interests. It results in a place that is somewhere where I feel accepted fully as I am by the people I am surrounded by. Fully known and fully loved. I think a place like this is especially important in 2024, as places of worship used to be a place where a lot of people found community outside of family and work. But in the reality of today, a lot of people need community outside of religion because religion has hurt a lot of people. The church institution can learn a lot of lessons about acceptance, connections, and accountability from places like this. It also reminds me of the joy of playfulness and togetherness, which those who did the Sunday school about the book "Between Heaven and Mirth" should know what I am talking about. Joy is an integral part of our religion that is often overlooked by a lot of people. Experiencing joy is a spiritual experience.

And lastly, what would it be without the First Church community? Like what what would I say without it? This place celebrates an accumulation of so many of the things that I love. I love being in a place where all are welcome truly means all are welcome. And it goes beyond words, it actually shows in your actions. I still struggle with what religion is to me and what my beliefs are, but I know I want to be a part of something like we have here. What I wouldn't give to have more time at Sunday school, or in the kitchen with Joanna, or chatting for way too long before and after church where April has to drag me away when we have plans. But that is the great thing about a good community. It transcends boundaries and time. I know that you will think of me and I will think of you. I know that we will reach out to one another with real care about how the other is doing. I know I will be welcomed back with open arms whenever I visit, as if no time has passed at all. I think we see firsthand every Sunday the challenges and rewards of fostering community in today's world. It can feel hard some days. A dwindling communion due to a changing culture. The actions of problematic churches that don't represent what we believe. Moving and deaths. Perhaps some days it feels like the social justice issues we have been fighting so hard to progress are going in the opposite direction. The stretches of a church budget in this changing world. And the fear that being a part of an institution like this is being part of a problem instead of being part of a solution that the world needs. But no matter what challenges we are facing, it becomes easier as we live together in unity. We have formed so many authentic relationships here and have a shared mission to spread love and justice across the world. We are ready to have these hard conversations and commit the resources we have to the causes we believe in. We have each other. We have created an environment for personal growth, healing, and belonging. This church is a role model and has set the bar for what I want to find or create in community wherever I go.

And then a little lighthearted um extra. A Foggy Tea Shop and Turkish Delight are just great restaurants you should try. Nothing deep to say about these places except I would totally recommend. I'm a regular at both and I'm going to miss them a lot. Turkish Delight, great and Pike Place. Foggy Tea, newer business, go support. I couldn't quite stay to my five, so I just had to add a few but I don't want to keep you here all day. So, overall, my list is so ingrained in community. I find it wonderful when people can come together about something that they care about and what they can do with their combined power about those things that they care. It is powerful to care about one another. Love, I still believe is more powerful than hate. The system doesn't want people to deeply care about one another. It often pits them against each other. It is an act of rebellion against the status quo to care about all around you and act in love towards one another, even if you have nothing in common to bring you together. I hope that we can continue to rebel until we succeed in changing the status quo.

And lastly, I want to speak a little bit on change. Ecclesiastes 3:1-8 has been my favorite verse in the Bible for at least 10 years. At the beginning, it probably was because it was easier to understand than a lot of other verses, but now I find it very comforting and inspiring. I struggle a lot with thinking about how I don't have time to do everything I want to do. But it reminds me that there are seasons of experience and change, and that there is a time for all of these different experiences, good and bad. Change is scary, but this helps to ground me. So much change happens that we don't even notice it until we take a minute to look back on what it was like a year ago. I remember last March, sitting in church and still feeling like I wasn't well plugged into any community in Seattle. And I was like, you know, trying to make friends. They talk about the Seattle freeze. I call it the Seattle flake because they'll say they want to hang out and then they won't. But and that was really hard coming here because I'm like, do people actually like me? Do people want to be in these relationships? But now I think about how hard it is to leave because of all these beautiful relationships I've made at all these different places. Change seems a lot less scary when I think about how little of my life I have been here, and how much I have experienced off this list from Ecclesiastes, and how much I have changed for the better during my time here. Thank you for being a part of my growth over the last two years, and for helping me fully realize my time to laugh, heal, plant, dance, speak up, and love, while helping me now to take the next steps to weep, mourn, and pluck up what is planted. One of the few things that I know now is that is it is a time to embrace instead of a time to refrain from embracing. Thank you.
